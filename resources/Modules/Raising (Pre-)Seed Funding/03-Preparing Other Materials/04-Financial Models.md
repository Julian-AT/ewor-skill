# Financial Models

Investors will likely ask you for a financial model when considering an investment, especially if you already have some revenue traction. We therefore highly recommend creating a financial model for your startup. While our [Entrepreneurial Finance Module](https://platform.ewor.com/modules/22) covers this in detail and includes a financial template, below are some key points to keep in mind for your investor pitch.

**Keep It Simple and Clear**

Avoid over-engineering your financial model, especially for pre-seed fundraising. A simple, easy-to-understand model that highlights the key drivers of your business is far more effective than a complex spreadsheet with numerous graphs and intricate formulas. For the seed stage, a bit more detail is expected.

**Show Clear Revenue Generation and Growth**

Make clear when your company will generate revenue, how that revenue is projected to grow over time, and which of your costs are directly related and therefore scale with this revenue. Investors need to see a logical and understandable path to profitability, focusing on a realistic progression that potentially outlines phases from customer validation to eventual profitability within a reasonable timeframe (typically 2–8 years). Be sure to avoid underestimating expenses and the complexities involved in scaling.

**Include All Relevant Financial Metrics**

Clearly communicate the amount of funding you are seeking and the projected runway this will provide (aim for 18–24 months to reach significant milestones). Also, include your estimated monthly burn rate and your primary cost drivers (e.g., team salaries, marketing expenses, product development costs). Highlight compelling unit economics, such as a Lifetime Value to Customer Acquisition Cost (LTV:CAC) ratio of 3:1 or better, if your model supports it. If reliable benchmarking data exists for your industry, consider incorporating it to support your projections and demonstrate a realistic understanding of market norms.

**Demonstrate Understanding of Key Financial Statements**

At this early stage, it often doesn’t make sense to create a Profit & Loss Statement and a cash flow projection separately. Do make sure to show investors that you understand the difference between the two. You might include a note explaining that these are combined for clarity at this stage.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Unfavourable LTV:CAC Without a Plan:** Be cautious if your model indicates difficulty achieving profitability due to poor unit economics. Have a clear strategy for how to improve your unit economics.
- **Presenting Unrealistic "Hockey Stick" Projections:** Investors are wary of projections that show unrealistic exponential growth without a strong foundation.
- **Underestimating Expenses and Time to Profitability:** Be realistic about the costs involved and the time it will take to achieve profitability.
- **Lack of Clarity on Key Assumptions:** Ensure the assumptions behind your financial projections are reasonable and can be briefly explained if questioned.
