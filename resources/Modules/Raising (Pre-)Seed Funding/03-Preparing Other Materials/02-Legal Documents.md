# Legal Documents

A strong legal foundation is a must-have for any startup. It signals to investors that your company is properly registered, has a clean cap table, and is backed by clear agreements among founders and shareholders. While legal structure alone won’t make you successful, a weak setup can undermine even the best-performing business. Think of it as a hygiene factor: necessary to avoid problems and to build investor confidence.

## **Incorporation & Registration**

To sign agreements and accept investments, your startup needs to be incorporated and registered as a legal entity. Investors expect proof of registration in a reputable jurisdiction, clarity on your chosen legal structure, and compliance with relevant regulations. Incorporating in less common jurisdictions without a compelling strategic reason—or presenting an incomplete or sloppy setup—can raise red flags.

## **Cap Table**

Your cap table, which outlines the current ownership of your startup and how future investments will affect it (dilution), is something investors will look at closely. They'll want to see a concise overview of everyone who holds equity – founders, early investors, and advisors – along with their ownership percentages and any outstanding options. 

Early-stage investors prefer to see that the founders still hold a significant portion of the company and that the number of early investors is manageable. Things that might deter investors include a cap table that's messy with too many small shareholders, a lot of equity held by individuals who are no longer actively involved (often called "dead equity"), or ownership structures that are unclear or overly complicated.

## **Co-Founder Agreement or Shareholder Agreement**

Investors also look for solid agreements between founders and shareholders. In the earliest stages, a co-founder agreement is common. Once investors join, this is typically replaced or supplemented by a broader shareholder agreement. Investors pay close attention to how founder equity vests, the roles and responsibilities of each founder, provisions for founder departures (good/bad leaver clauses), and mechanisms for resolving disputes. Missing agreements, unfair or unclear equity splits, and a lack of vesting schedules are all causes for concern.

**Intellectual Property (IP) Documentation:**

Protecting your core innovations is key to securing your competitive edge and boosting investor confidence. Upload all relevant IP documentation—patents, trademarks, copyrights, and ownership records—to your data room. Ensure that any IP created by founders or employees is legally assigned to the company. This requires official employment contracts that clearly state all IP developed in the course of work belongs to the startup.
