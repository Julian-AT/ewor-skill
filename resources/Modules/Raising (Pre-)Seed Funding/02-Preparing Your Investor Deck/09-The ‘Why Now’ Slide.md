# The ‘Why Now’ Slide

[YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ofkxp6BI6Fw&t=2s)

The ‘Why Now’ slide, a staple in the Sequoia Capital pitch deck template, aims to convince investors that the timing is optimal for your startup's success. Sequoia's argument is that for many solutions, timing is crucial; being too early or too late can significantly impact success. While not universally essential, this slide is particularly valuable for highlighting the urgency and relevance of your solution in the current climate.

## Key Elements of a Successful Slide:

- **Focus on Genuine Urgency:** Clearly articulate why *now* is the critical time for your solution. For many startups, timing is paramount.
- **Highlight External Enabling Factors:** Explain how recent shifts in the economic climate, advancements in technology, changes in market dynamics, or shifts in consumer behaviour are creating a unique and timely opportunity for your startup. For example, consider a company specializing in fission-fusion test reactor technology originating from MIT. Their innovation became feasible only recently thanks to technological advancements, enabling them to secure an exclusive contract with a key partner. Similarly, before the iPhone, General Magic couldn't realize their vision due to tech limitations, but Apple seized the moment when battery and touchscreen technology matured.
- **Connect to Your Unique Position:** Align these external factors with your startup's current capabilities, team expertise, and market opportunities. Demonstrate why *your* team is uniquely positioned to capitalize on this specific moment in time.
- **Be Specific and Concrete:** Avoid vague references to general trends. Instead, focus on precise events, technological milestones, or market changes that directly create urgency for your specific solution.

## Common Mistakes:

- **Piggybacking on Overhyped Trends:** Avoid simply citing popular trends like generative AI, blockchain, or the urgency of climate change as your primary "Why Now." While these trends might be relevant to your business, investors are already well aware of them. Simply stating their existence doesn't explain why *your* specific startup is uniquely positioned to capitalize on them *right now*. To be effective, you need to articulate a more nuanced and timely connection beyond the obvious. For instance, instead of just saying "generative AI is growing," explain how a *recent* breakthrough in generative AI specifically enables your unique solution in a way that wasn't possible before.
- **Relying Solely on Market Growth Statistics:** While highlighting a growing market is crucial (and belongs on your Market Size slide), it doesn't automatically create a compelling "Why Now." Simply stating that your market is projected to grow by X% doesn't explain why the present moment offers a unique window of opportunity for your specific startup. Investors want to understand what recent changes or developments make your solution particularly relevant *today*. However, if your startup is entering a small market with a *sudden* and significant projected growth spurt due to a recent catalyst (like the emergence of sustainable aviation fuels due to new regulations), this can be a more compelling reason for immediate action.
- **Including "Nice-to-Have" Factors:** The "Why Now" slide should focus on essential, time-sensitive drivers, not just supplementary benefits or features. For example, mentioning that you plan to use a popular social media platform like TikTok for marketing is generally not a strong "Why Now" argument on its own, as many companies utilize such platforms. TikTok would only be a compelling "Why Now" if your entire go-to-market strategy uniquely hinges on a very specific and potentially fleeting opportunity presented by that platform right now, without viable alternatives.
- **Lack of a Compelling Rationale:** If you struggle to identify a truly compelling and time-sensitive reason for "Why Now," it's often better to omit the slide altogether. A weak or generic "Why Now" argument can actually detract from your pitch and make it seem like you haven't deeply considered the timing of your venture. If the timing isn't exceptionally critical, focus on the strength of your team, problem, and solution instead.
