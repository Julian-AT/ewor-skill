# Introduction

Your pitch deck is arguably the most crucial tool in your fundraising arsenal, often forming investors' first impression of your startup and vision. This section will explore the core principles of what investors seek in your pitch and the decks you present. We'll focus on crafting your investor deck for the (pre-)seed round, guiding you through essential elements, highlighting common pitfalls and best practices, and sharing effective slide examples.

## Your Startup’s Sales Brochure

Think of your investor deck as your startup's sales brochure, specifically tailored to investors. When fundraising, you're essentially selling a financial product: shares in your company.

There are various types of investor decks, all serving different purposes.

- **Teaser Deck: **Serves as your initial hook. Keep it extremely concise, ideally a one-page PDF, designed to quickly pique investor interest and prompt them to want to learn more.
- **Send-Ahead Deck: **Serves as your introduction. Sent to investors before a meeting, it should provide enough compelling information to generate interest without overwhelming them. Unlike a presentation deck, it can include more text for easier reading.
- **Presentation Deck: **Used during your actual pitch. Prioritise visual appeal and keep it brief, acting as a support for your spoken narrative. Guy Kawasaki's 30-20-10 rule is a helpful guideline: aim for around 10 slides, a 20-minute presentation, and a minimum 30-point font for readability. Conciseness is key here.

![The  30-20-10 rule by Guy Kawasaki](/api/media/file/M-L2T1%20-%20GuyKwasakiRule-1.png)

- **Leave-Behind Deck: **Sent after your pitch, this deck provides comprehensive information, including appendices and detailed data to answer any lingering investor questions. It's a living document that should incorporate answers to questions you've received.

While you don't necessarily need all four, having a strong presentation deck and a comprehensive send-ahead deck is highly recommended as a minimum for initial investor engagement. The teaser and leave-behind decks can be valuable additions, depending on your specific fundraising process.

## Decision Criteria

To craft a compelling investor deck, it's essential to understand what signals investors look for. While not every investor might have a sophisticated set of decision-making criteria and while priorities might differ between investors, our insights reveal a common pattern.

Ultimately, investors perform an expected value calculation for your startup, weighing the potential return against the probability of achieving it. Your investor deck needs to address both aspects, outlining the potential return through factors like market size, business model, and scalability, while also building confidence in your ability to execute through details about your team and the milestones you have achieved so far.

Especially at the pre-seed stage — where there might not be a product or business model yet, it down to two core questions: Is the market opportunity significant enough? Can this team successfully develop and deliver the product?

![Most investors make an expected value calculation when determining to invest in your startup or not.](/api/media/file/M-L2T1%20-%20ExpectedValueCalculation.png)

Remember that early-stage startup investing is inherently risky. To compensate for potential losses, investors (especially early-stage investors) seek ventures with high potential returns, characterised by large markets, scalability, and the opportunity for rapid customer acquisition.

### Potential Return

The potential return hinges on the market size and your business model’s ability to capture a substantial portion of that market quickly and profitably.

**The Market is large and/or growing**

Investors typically seek markets with a potential exceeding one billion euros. If your initial focus is a smaller market, clearly demonstrate its rapid growth trajectory and your future plans to expand into more substantial markets.

**The startup can capture a substantial portion of this market**

Beyond a large (and/or growing) market, investors need to be convinced that your product and business model are uniquely positioned to capture a substantial portion of that market profitably. 

Your product must present a distinctive value proposition – ideally solving a problem in a way that wasn't achievable before, addressing the crucial 'Why Now?' – while your business model needs to be scalable and clearly articulate the ways you will create, deliver, and capture value to effectively compete, even in a (potentially crowded) space. A strong business model, particularly one leveraging network effects, can be a significant competitive advantage, driving revenue and enabling you to outperform competitors.

![A well-crafted business model creates, captures, and delivers value.](/api/media/file/M-L2T1%20-%20BusinessModel-1.png)

### Probability

Beyond potential return, your investor deck needs to help investors assess the probability of you capturing a significant market share. For this, highlight that your team’s ability to execute and the steps you have taken to derisk the startup.

**The Team is up for the challenge**

Investors need strong conviction that your team has the ability to develop and successfully sell your product, and the resilience to persevere. Ideas are not scarce; an exceptional team is what turns an idea into a successful business. If investors lack confidence in your team's ability to execute, the perceived value of your venture will be significantly lower.

Convince investors your team:

- Brings a unique and deep understanding of the problem.
- Has the proven (technical and product development) capabilities to develop the product. 
- Possesses the skills and connections to sell the product. 
- Demonstrates the leadership & planning skills required or, even better, the entrepreneurial experience to lead a scaling startup.
- Embodies the resilience and determination to overcome adversity.

**Steps have been taken to derisk the startup**

To build investor confidence in your startup's probability of success, your investor deck must clearly showcase evidence of how you've derisked the venture. Remember, your startup's valuation is heavily influenced by the extent to which you've mitigated risks, so ensure this comes across strongly.

Investors are looking for tangible proof that:

- (Potential) Customers are interested in or actively using your product to solve their problems: Demonstrate this through letters of intent, waitlist sign-ups, user counts, and, if possible, user growth and retention.
- You can monetise the interest in your product: Highlight your revenue potential and growth, along with your ability to upsell and cross-sell. Indicate your Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) and compare it to industry standards. If possible, showcase your unit economics.
- Others believe in your product's potential: Signal external validation through interest or investment from other investors. Feature an impressive advisory board comprised of recognised industry experts. Also, highlight any grants and non-dilutive funding secured from reputable sources like universities, governments, or non-profit organisations.
- Your product has strong barriers to entry: Show that it will be challenging for competitors to replicate your offering by highlighting patents, unique technology, strong network effects, or other defensible advantages.
