# The Problem Slide

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/8-2xZPaUOCw)

The Problem Slide is arguably the most critical in your investor deck. It must unequivocally convince investors that the problem you're addressing is significant, widespread, and worth solving. Remember, your solution and team's capabilities are only relevant if the underlying problem resonates as crucial and urgent.

### Key Elements of a Successful Slide:

- **Start with a Compelling Hook and Clearly Define the Problem: **Grab investors' attention immediately with a thought-provoking question or a striking statement that highlights the problem. Then, concisely articulate the core problem your startup is tackling, ideally in one to two sentences that are easy for anyone to understand.
- **Make it Relatable by Identifying Your Target Audience:** Clearly specify who experiences this problem and highlight their specific pain points, frustrations, and inefficiencies. Use storytelling to connect with investors on an emotional level and make the problem tangible.
- **Show the Magnitude by Quantifying the Problem and Using Visuals Effectively:** Emphasize the significance and scale of the problem by using compelling data, statistics, quotes, or a brief, impactful story. Utilize graphs, charts, and impactful images to illustrate the problem's prevalence more effectively than text alone.
- **Explain Existing Solutions (and their shortcomings): **Briefly outline how the problem is currently being addressed (if at all) and clearly articulate the limitations or inadequacies of these existing solutions. This creates the opportunity for your unique approach.
- **Highlight the Opportunity Gap and Connect to Your Solution (Tease):** Clearly articulate the gap between the current solutions and the ideal solution that your product or service offers, creating anticipation for the next slide where you'll present your answer.

### Common Mistakes:

- **Lack of Specificity:** Avoid describing a broad, overarching issue without a clear and focused angle.
- **Assuming Investor Understanding: **Don't assume investors are experts in your industry. Keep it simple and understandable for outsiders.
- **Not Highlighting Pain Points Clearly: **Ensure you vividly convey the negative consequences and frustrations experienced by your target audience due to this problem.
- **Ignoring Existing Solutions:** Failing to acknowledge how the problem is currently being addressed can make your pitch seem uninformed or naive.

### Problem Slide Examples

#### Alto

![Source: TechCrunch](/api/media/file/alto)

Given investors' brief attention span, conveying your problem swiftly and memorably is crucial. Alto, in their Series E Pitch Deck, masterfully achieved this by stating: 'Current experience is broken for patients and providers.' They effectively used visuals to illustrate these broken experiences and highlighted specific issues, such as a lack of pricing transparency. This concise, visually impactful, and specific approach ensured investors quickly understood the significant pain points they were addressing.

#### Mi Terro

![Source: TechCrunch](/api/media/file/mi%20terro)

Mi Terro, who secured a $1.5 million seed round, effectively illustrated the magnitude of the problem by vividly visualizing their transformation of agricultural waste into plastic-replacement proteins. They underscored the issue's scale by presenting staggering data: 20 billion PVA wraps polluting waterways and soil, highlighting the emotional impact on daily essentials and demonstrating a significant problem worth solving.

#### Student Finance

![Source: TechCrunch](/api/media/file/student%20finance)

Similarly, Student Finance, who secured $41 million in their Series A, effectively addressed the globally recognised issue of reskilling in disrupted industries. Rather than dwelling on the problem itself, they emphasized the inadequacy of existing solutions, reinforcing this with relatable quotes that added a personal touch. This concise yet impactful approach quickly signalled to investors their understanding of the widespread problem and their readiness to offer a viable solution.
