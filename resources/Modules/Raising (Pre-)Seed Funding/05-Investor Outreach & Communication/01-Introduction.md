# Introduction

By now you have determined your fundraising goals and strategy and created your investor deck and other relevant materials. You are ready to start fundraising! In the following topics, we will discuss:

## Setting Up Your CRM

An investor CRM is essential for managing outreach and tracking your progress. At a minimum, it should include investor contact details, priority level, expected investment amount, likelihood of investment, and outreach status. Consistently updating your CRM ensures you always know where you stand, helps you avoid errors, and allows you to leverage traction strategically. A well-maintained CRM is not just a tool for organisation — it’s the backbone of your fundraising process.

## Creating a Target List of Investors

Accuracy is everything. After every interaction, update your CRM immediately, whether you’ve sent an email, had a call, or received feedback. Leaving an investor marked “to be contacted” after a conversation or failing to log a follow-up can create costly confusion. Taking just 20–60 seconds after each task keeps your records reliable and your process efficient. Over time, this diligence compounds into smoother investor relations and stronger fundraising outcomes.

## Reaching out to and communicating with investors

Personalisation is key. Avoid mass-mailing tools and make the effort to approach each investor individually. Warm introductions are always preferable. When reaching out, research the investor’s background, LinkedIn posts, or portfolio companies so you can tailor your message. A good outreach note should explain why you’re reaching out, highlight what you value about them, and end with a clear invitation to connect.

While each message should feel personal, you can streamline the process by preparing flexible email templates. These might include bullet points about your venture, a Calendly link, or other useful details. Tools like Superhuman or even Google Docs can help you manage and reuse templates easily.

Aim to schedule investor calls quickly — ideally the same day or the next. Consider using WhatsApp for rapid communication and updates, even if you don’t typically use it. Its speed and informality can boost responsiveness during the hectic fundraising period.

## Keeping Investors Updated

Once investors have confirmed or expressed soft commitment, keep them in the loop with weekly updates. Share your progress, highlight new commitments, and use the momentum of existing interest to build urgency. Track all soft commitments in your CRM to stay organised. If you’re raising with a CLA, this is the right moment to start sending them out.

For larger rounds led by a main investor, aim to gather as many soft commitments as possible beforehand. This allows you to approach the lead with confidence: “We have soft commitments secured; all that’s left is your due diligence and check.”

## Adjusting as Needed

If VCs raise concerns about your story or market, consider changing your strategy to address those issues. Be open to feedback and willing to change your fundraising strategy based on new information gained during the fundraising process. For example, one fellow found that shifting from targeting influencers to focusing on SMBs to reach a larger market resulted in increased traction and successful fundraising.
