# Requesting Introductions and Outreach

Once you've identified potential investors and mutual connections in your CRM and labelled them according to priority, the next step is securing those warm introductions and crafting effective outreach. Begin your outreach by contacting low-priority investors (those ranked '3' in your CRM). This strategic approach offers several benefits:

- **Feedback Loop:** These early interactions provide invaluable feedback on your pitch, deck, and overall presentation. Use their responses, questions, and even rejections as learning opportunities to refine your materials.
- **Practice and Confidence:** Engaging with less critical contacts allows you to practice your pitch and communication style without the high stakes of your top-tier investors. This builds confidence and sharpens your delivery.

## Requesting an Introduction

When you've identified a mutual connection who can introduce you to an investor, request them to make the introduction. You can make use of the [EWOR Forwardable Double Opt-in Template](https://platform.ewor.com/api/media/file/EWOR%E2%80%99s%20Double%20Forwardable%20Email%20Double%20Opt-In%20Template.pdf), as it respects the introducer's time while making the process seamless.

The core idea is to provide your connection with a pre-written, concise email that they can simply forward to the investor. This email should:

- Be concise: Get straight to the point.
- Clearly state your ask: Who you are, what your company does (briefly), why you want to meet the investor, and what you're asking for (e.g., a brief introductory call).
- Include a value proposition: Why should the investor care? (e.g., alignment with their portfolio, unique solution to a market problem).
- Attach your deck (optional but recommended): Make it easy for the investor to learn more.

## Reaching out to Investors

When reaching out to an investor in your network or to an investor you were introduced to by a mutual connection, we recommend adhering to email etiquette, without being overly formal; adhere to conventions that resonate with investors.

Let’s take a look at these conventions.

**1. Salutation and Greeting**

**Start your emails with **"Dear [Recipient's Name],". The next word should begin with a capital letter. While seemingly minor, proper punctuation signals attention to detail, which can be noticed by investors, especially those from cultures where such nuances are valued.

**2. Beginning the Body: Gratitude and Context**

Always start by expressing gratitude to your introducer. "Thank you so much for the introduction to [Introducer's Name]."

- Briefly state the purpose of your email, referencing the introduction.

**3. Introducing Yourself**

- Address the investor directly: "Dear [Investor's Name],"
- Adopt a warm yet professional tone. Express pleasure in connecting and, if appropriate, mention positive feedback you've heard about their fund or recent investments.
- Subtly imply you've done your research by referencing mutual connections or their firm's focus.
- Convey enthusiasm for a potential conversation while maintaining a professional demeanor.

**4. Share Your Availability (Calendly)**

- Provide a link to your Calendly or a similar scheduling tool.
- Emphasise ample availability for the coming week. Your Calendly should truly reflect your open slots, even if it means evening times. Hyper-availability is crucial during fundraising; it demonstrates commitment and eagerness.

**5. Concise Company Bullet Points**

- Include **three to five brief bullet points** about your company. These are a teaser, not a full pitch. Cover the problem you address, your solution, market opportunity, and perhaps a key team highlight.
- **Tailor these points** if you know the investor's specific interests (e.g., emphasize traction if they're data-driven).
- **Attach your deck with a hyperlink** for easy access. Only skip these bullet points in rare cases where market competitiveness demands a more direct, immediate call.

**6. Closing Your Email**

- Always leave the door open for further questions. Conclude by inviting the recipient to reach out for more information.
- Express eagerness for further discussion. This encourages a two-way conversation and shows responsiveness.

**7. Professional Footer**

- End with a professional footer: your full name, company name, and contact details (email address, website).
- Consider adding a professional photo to enhance your email's polished appearance.

## Scheduling and Responsiveness

Throughout your fundraising efforts, maintaining a swift response time is essential. Aim to check your emails hourly and reply promptly, even if it's just a quick acknowledgment or a scheduling link. To ensure this, make fundraising a top priority by dedicating specific time slots to tasks like working on your deck and CRM, while keeping communication channels open. When scheduling calls, streamline the process by offering clear availability in your initial email, ideally with a Calendly link. To avoid unnecessary back-and-forth, proactively send calendar invites with alternative times, and if a VC suggests a specific time, confirm quickly and provide all necessary details.

## WhatsApp and LinkedIn Messaging

Investors may use platforms like WhatsApp or LinkedIn for faster communication. When you communicate through these channels, maintain the same professional principles as you would in an email, ensuring consistent efficiency and responsiveness. It's important to adapt your style to be more casual and concise, providing only essential information like brief company bullet points and a Calendly link. Regardless of the informal nature of these platforms, ensure your messages are clear and convey a positive, confident tone.

## Final Checks

Before sending any emails or WhatsApp messages to investors, make sure to do some final checks:

- **Proofread:** Double-check every email for grammar and authenticity. Tools like ChatGPT can help, but ensure the tone remains naturally confident, not arrogant.
- **Seek Feedback:** Especially in early stages, have others who've fundraised successfully review your emails. EWOR can also provide input.
- **Long-Term Investment:** Fundraising is a periodic, intense process (every 18-24 months). Investing time now in perfecting your outreach will lead to a smoother, more successful round.
