# Legal Set Up

Maintaining a sound legal framework is paramount, even though it might not be explicitly featured on your investor deck. Good angels and VCs will inquire after your legal decisions concerning the incorporation of your startup, the geography you are incorporated in and your co-founder agreement. An unprofessional legal set-up might cause them to decide against funding you, even when your business case is very promising. Two critical aspects warrant discussion in this regard.

The first one being a **clean cap table**. It should be devoid of unnecessary complexities and reserved primarily for co-founders, perhaps some advisors, and an early business angel during the pre-seed round. Employee equity and other stakeholders typically enter the picture during subsequent funding rounds or through agreements like RESA or ECMP, ensuring a streamlined structure in the initial pre-seed phase.

Post pre-seed round, a standard dilution of 10 to 30% occurs, with this equity ideally held by active investors. Subsequent rounds introduce new investors, capturing another 10 to 30%. Issues arise if non-active co-founders retain equity, deemed as "dead equity," which is perceived negatively by investors. To remedy this, resolving such situations before pursuing the next funding round is crucial.

Secondly, a well-structured **shareholder agreement** is paramount. This legal document outlines equity redistribution mechanisms, typically involving vesting periods (usually 3-4 years) with a cliff (often around 12 months). A transparent agreement signals to investors that the team has established protocols for handling equity matters, instilling confidence in the company's governance.

In cases where a co-founder leaves, the vesting schedule becomes pivotal. Vesting should occur gradually over a period of four years. This structure ensures that departing team members receive equitable compensation corresponding to their contributions over time. The vesting schedule aligns with the principle of rewarding substantial contributions and dedication to the company's growth.

Having a robust vesting agreement in place signals to investors that you comprehend the intricacies of co-founder dynamics and possess a legal understanding of building a business. Demonstrating awareness of potential challenges and the ability to explain the terms within the agreement instil confidence in investors. Mastering these aspects is crucial, as future agreements may span up to 100 pages, and lacking comprehension could leave you vulnerable to exploitation in the market.

## How Investors Gather Information

To understand what information investors seek, let's delve into how they acquire these insights. Your investor deck and your pitch are of course important sources of information for them, but there are other ways they gather information on you to substantiate their investment decision. 

The first one is your interactions with them in person or over (video) calls. Coming across as confident during these interactions is key, but it doesn't equate to being overly salesy. Studies show that excessively sales-oriented pitches don't necessarily secure more funding. True confidence stems from a self-determined approach, showcasing your knowledge and conviction in what you discuss. Striking this balance will enhance your effectiveness during investor calls.

Another way investors gather information is via email. Frequently, investors pose questions via email. A prompt response, ideally within a few hours, signals efficiency and attentiveness. This not only reflects your ability to address investor queries swiftly, but also suggests a proactive approach to handling other aspects of your business such as customer complaints. Demonstrating this responsiveness is crucial to underscore your comprehension of the situation's urgency and significance. However, if you were to need five to ten days to respond, this would be a red flag for investors.

Furthermore, investors may conduct informal due diligence by examining your online presence, such as your LinkedIn profile, Facebook page, and articles about you. This preliminary check often precedes a formal data room setup. Consider reviewing these elements from an investor's perspective and ensure they align with the narrative you want to convey in your pitch. It's common for investors to discuss their findings with other investors, and the information uncovered during this process can significantly influence their decision-making. Take the initiative to Google yourself, scrutinise all available information, and proactively address any discrepancies to maintain alignment with your desired pitch narrative.
