# Financial Models

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/I_MW09aaigA)

This topic is all about financial models, as we highly recommended that you create a financial model for any business you are starting. At the very least, you should show your costs and the runway you'll have after funding. In many cases, it is advantageous to include revenue growth, unit economics, and other relevant financial metrics in your financial model.
While we have an [Entrepreneurial Finance course](https://platform.ewor.com/modules/22) that covers this topic and includes a [financial template](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/18CjdH21KeMw1b3jGX261hh18MwxPYYd6sqKi29jcpBM/template/preview), there are a few key points we can highlight here.

### Keep It Simple

When designing your financial model, avoid over engineering it. The most effective financial models are often simple. Sophisticated models with numerous graphs and complex templates can be overwhelming. Therefore, keep your financial model simple so that you can see all aspects at a glance. For pre-seed fundraising, your initial financial model should be simple. If it becomes too complex, it may fail to serve its purpose effectively. However, this is a different story for the seed-stage.

### Clarity of Information

When creating your financial model, make sure it clearly shows how your company generates revenue, how revenue changes over time, and how costs are related to revenue. A well-structured financial model provides an intuitive understanding of your company's operations. It is preferable to have a simple but clear Excel table over a highly sophisticated and complex model. While a visually appealing financial model is desirable, simplicity, clarity, and directness should be prioritised.
Avoid presenting an unrealistic dream story in your financial model, claiming that a small investment will result in exponential growth and immediate profit. These narratives indicate a lack of understanding of business complexities, which can raise red flags for investors. Remember, your financial model should be realistic, with phases ranging from customer discovery and validation to eventual profitability. Furthermore, it's important to convey a realistic timeline for profitability, spanning from two to eight years. Providing projections that are too optimistic may suggest oversight or lack of experience. Consequently, it's crucial to steer clear of underestimating expenses and complexities in your financial model.

### Distinguish Between P&L, Cash Flow, and Balance Sheets

Third, it is critical to demonstrate to investors that you understand the differences between the P&L, cash flow, and balance sheets. If you are unfamiliar with these financial statements and their differences, educate yourself. While you can combine everything on one page and may not need to explicitly outline the balance sheet or distinguish between the P&L and cash flows, it's a good idea to include a note stating that the cash flows and P&L are combined for simplicity.

Additionally, include key financial metrics in your presentation, such as the amount of funding required, the projected runway (typically 18 months), the estimated monthly burn rate, and the primary cost drivers. Clearly communicate your key expenses and cost drivers, whether they relate to staffing, training, or other factors. This transparency will demonstrate to investors that you understand your company's finances.

These are the fundamental principles to follow when building your financial model. Ensure that you understand unit economics, market sizes, and the specifics of financial models.
