# At What Valuation?

The valuation at which you will be able to raise hinges on a combination of factors over which you have varying degrees of control.

## Derisking Your Startup

Early-stage investing is inherently risky. A significant portion of startups that investors back will not generate returns. Therefore, investors look for indicators that suggest a high probability of success. The stronger their conviction in your startup’s potential for success, the more likely they are to invest and the higher the valuation you may achieve.

In general, we advise derisking your startup by reaching as many milestones as possible before raising dilutive funding. This strategy not only enhances the likelihood of a successful raise but also tends to increase your valuation, resulting in less dilution for you as founders.

There are typically four key stages at which startups aim to raise a (pre-)seed round.

- **Team Stage:** At this very early stage, you possess a defined problem, a proposed solution, and a founding team. A Minimum Viable Product (MVP) has not yet been developed. Given the inherently low probability of success at this point, raising funding is generally recommended only for startups with substantial initial capital requirements, such as DeepTech or specialised hardware companies. If you intend to raise at this stage, your primary focus should be on articulating a compelling and visionary narrative, highlighting a significant market opportunity, and presenting a clear roadmap for product development along with the necessary investment. Furthermore, demonstrating a strong, experienced team with undeniable founder-problem fit or founder-market fit is crucial to convince investors of a low execution risk.
- **MVP Stage: **A startup is considered to be at the MVP stage once a tangible Minimum Viable Product has been developed, a proof of concept has been reached or patents have been granted. At this stage, significant user traction has not yet been achieved. Many startups seek funding at this point. When fundraising at the MVP stage, your narrative should centre on your strategy for acquiring initial users and how the raised capital will be utilised to further develop the product based on early feedback and market validation. While raising at the MVP stage will likely lead to a higher valuation compared to the Team stage, for the majority of startups, it is often more strategic to postpone fundraising until you can demonstrate some level of traction.
- **Traction Stage: **A startup is typically considered to be in the traction stage when it can demonstrate early traction in the form of active users, beta testers, letters of intent, or a waiting list, but has not yet begun to generate revenue. When raising capital at this stage, your primary focus should be on explaining how the funds will be used to achieve product-market fit and scale the initial traction you have gained. The traction stage is often an opportune time to fundraise, as valuations can be attractive and are not yet constrained by revenue-based benchmarks, offering more negotiation flexibility.
- **Revenue Stage: **Startups that have successfully begun to monetise their user base and are generating recurring revenue are considered to be revenue stage. When seeking funding at this stage, your narrative should emphasise key financial metrics such as Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) and Customer Lifetime Value (LTV). Be prepared for investors to conduct a thorough review of your financial data and projections. A key consideration when raising at this stage is that investors may rely on industry-standard revenue multiples to determine your valuation. Raising at the traction stage is therefore often advisable, as it usually provides more flexibility in valuation.

## Valuation & Dilution Benchmarks

When you're fundraising, investors will compare your target raise and proposed valuation against a benchmark of other startups at similar stages, ideally within the same industry and geographical region. This benchmarking helps them assess whether your ask is reasonable within the current market.

For pre-seed funding in Europe, expect typical dilution to range from 10% to 20%, with a median pre-money valuation hovering around €4 million to €5 million. Moving to the seed stage in Europe, valuations generally fall within the range of €5 million to €15 million, with investors typically expecting a dilution of 15% to 25%. Keep in mind that these figures can vary significantly based on both geography and industry. For example, pre-money valuations in the US can be more than twice as high, and even within Europe, you'll see considerable differences between countries and sectors.

A European startup we invested in faced a San Francisco competitor with double the valuation. Initially benchmarking against them, the founder was disappointed when investors  only wanted to invest at about half the valuation. However, their coach convinced them that their funding needs were significantly lower due to the disparity in developer salaries ($180k in San Francisco vs. €50-60k in Germany). Ultimately, this European startup successfully raised half the amount at half the valuation of their San Francisco counterpart and still achieved comparable milestones.

In another example, an EWOR Fellow aimed to raise a €500k angel round with a €10 million valuation. Investors were hesitant due to the low 5% dilution, which often doesn’t provide enough incentive for early-stage investors. Furthermore, comparable start-ups in the same sector had raised funds at a €5 million valuation cap. Consequently, the founder eventually settled on a €5 million valuation cap.

When determining your valuation, make sure to find relevant benchmarks for your geography, industry, and stage.

## FOMO Among Investors

Investors are frequently, at least partly, motivated by a Fear of Missing Out. They don't want to be left behind on emerging trends or miss the opportunity to invest in the next groundbreaking startup that everyone else is talking about. Although you are unlikely to influence which industry or technology is considered all the rage in the investor community, you can strategically cultivate FOMO by creating a sense of scarcity around your funding round.

Since investor behaviour isn't always purely rational and price-driven, social proof plays a significant role in fundraising. If investors hear that a funding round is generating significant interest and commitments, they become more inclined to participate and might start a bidding war. Conversely, a perception that other investors are passing on an opportunity can create a negative feedback loop, regardless of the perceived value or valuation.

To ensure you can raise your targeted amount of money, creating at least some FOMO is crucial. Your outreach and communication strategy are key. Indicate that others are interested in your startup by sharing the grants you have secured or interest from early angels. Mentioning you are an EWOR Fellow is also a strong signal for other investors. 

One EWOR Fellow experienced how FOMO can significantly increase the valuation and funding amount. After closing their round, a surge of interest led to multiple term sheets. Their initial target of a $3 million raise at a $10 million pre-money valuation quickly ballooned to a $13 million raise at a $40 million post-money valuation. While some initial investors withdrew, the remaining VCs, observing the traction and confidence of others, reassessed and offered higher valuations.

## Economic Climate

The broader economic climate significantly impacts startup funding. During periods of strong economic growth, investor confidence is generally higher, leading to increased availability of capital and a greater willingness to invest in early-stage ventures. Conversely, in times of economic uncertainty or recession, investors tend to become more risk-averse, making funding harder to secure for startups. Factors like interest rates also play a crucial role. Higher interest rates make borrowing more expensive, potentially reducing the funds available for investment and making safer, lower-return investments more attractive relative to the high-risk nature of startups.

Keep the current economic climate in mind when setting your fundraising goals.
