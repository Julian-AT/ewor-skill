# From Whom to Raise?

When raising funds, you have to have a clear sense of whom you want to raise funding from to ensure your reach out campaigns are successful.

There are different individuals and organisations that invest in startups, differing in their motivations for investing and the source of their capital. In this topic, we will only focus on providers of dilutive funding for early-stage startups.

- **Your Close Network**: Friends, family and other acquaintances invest their own money and are primarily driven by a personal connection and a desire to help you.
- **Incubators and Accelerators**: These programmes use their own funds or capital raised from investors to provide early-stage support. Their motivation is to acquire equity in promising startups, potentially developing new products/services within their structure (incubators) or accelerating growth for a return on investment.
- **Angel Investors:** These individuals invest their personal funds. Their motivations are varied, ranging from seeking financial returns and portfolio diversification to a passion for supporting entrepreneurship or strategic interests based on their experience.
- **Angel Syndicates**: These groups pool personal funds from multiple angel investors to make larger investments. Their motivations mirror individual angels, often with a focus on diversification and potentially a longer-term outlook.
- **Family Offices**: They deploy capital from the wealth of high-net-worth families. Their motivations can be diverse, including wealth preservation and growth, personal interests of family members, or strategic alignment with their existing businesses.
- **Corporate Venture Capitalists (CVCs)**: These are investment arms of corporations using the company's own funds. Their primary motivation is often strategic, seeking access to innovation, new technologies, or potential acquisitions that align with their core business.
- **Venture Capitalists (VCs)**: VCs invest funds raised from institutional investors and high-net-worth individuals. Their main motivation is to generate significant financial returns for their investors through successful exits of high-growth potential startups.

When determining whom to try and raise funding from, there are a few factors to consider.

1. The funding round the investor focussed on
2. The industry & geography focus of the investor
3. The check size the investor can write and their potential for follow-up investments
4. The support you want from your investor(s)
5. The alignment of your exit strategy with the goal of the investor
6. The reputation of the investor

## 1. Funding Round

Different investors invest in different funding rounds. For instance, raising from Friends, Family, and your close network is typically most appropriate for your very first capital injections, often at the pre-seed stage. When considering Angel Investors and Venture Capital Funds, remember that their investment focus can vary significantly. VC funds often have specific mandates regarding the stage of companies they invest in (e.g., early-stage seed, later seed, Series A). To determine an investor's focus, review their website or portfolio companies and don't hesitate to ask them directly about their preferred investment stage.

## 2. Industry and Geographic Focus

Many investors strategically focus on specific industries and/or geographies. For example, angel investors often back sectors they know well from personal experience, while family offices and corporate VCs tend to invest in areas aligning with their core businesses. VC funds may also have specific investment theses, such as investing only in GreenTech or the DACH region. Thoroughly research potential investors' websites, LinkedIn, and portfolios to confirm their industry and geographical preferences align with your startup.

## 3. Check Size & Potential for Follow-up Investments

Even for investors investing in the same funding round, the check size can vary significantly. For instance, individual contributions from friends and family are typically smaller, often ranging from a few thousand to tens of thousands of euros. Angel investors in Europe can invest anywhere from €25,000 to upwards of €1 million, with the average being closer to €50,000-€200,000, although larger syndicates can pool together much more. Venture Capital firms generally invest larger amounts, with typical seed rounds in Europe ranging from €1 million to €4 million, and Series A rounds often exceeding €4 million. It is generally advisable to, especially in your seed round, to accept a few large tickets and a handful of smaller ones to keep the cap table lean and reduce the time you have to spend fundraising.

Family offices, corporate VCs and VCs might be able to offer follow-up investments in later rounds. This can be highly beneficial as they can take the lead and already commit a considerable part of the targeted funding amount, saving you time in reaching out to new investors.

## 4. Expected Support

Depending on the level and type of support you expect from your investors, some are far more suitable than others. Investors who offer not only capital but also relevant expertise and guidance are often referred to as Smart Capital. This is in contrast to Dumb Capital, which primarily provides funding without significant additional support.

It is advisable to strive for a cap table with a diverse set of investors to ensure access to diverse resources. Here are different types of support you might be looking for:

- **Mentors: **If you want (some of) your investors to act as mentors, it's often beneficial to seek out angel investors who are former founders themselves. They can offer empathy and practical advice based on their own entrepreneurial journey. Similarly, VC funds with former founders or experienced operators as partners can become mentors. When assessing potential mentors, pay close attention to your personal chemistry during meetings. Don't hesitate to ask about their mentorship style and how involved they typically get with their portfolio companies. 
- **Network & Connections: **If you're looking for investors with a significant network and connections within your specific industry, for instance, to introduce you to potential customers, strategic partners, or key hires, focus on investors who specialize in your sector. Angel investors who have previously worked or founded in your industry often have extensive personal networks. Similar for VC funds specializing in your industry. Ask investors for specific examples of connections they've facilitated for their portfolio companies. A well-connected investor in your industry can significantly accelerate your business development efforts. 
- **Functional Expertise: **Do you require support in specific functional areas such as operations, finance, legal, or marketing? Seeking out angel investors who have held senior operational roles in these fields in the past can be incredibly helpful. They can offer practical advice and even roll up their sleeves to assist with specific challenges. Clearly articulate the specific functional expertise you are seeking during your initial conversations with potential investors. 
- **Industry Expertise: **If deep industry expertise is a priority for you, seek out angel investors who have a strong track record of working or founding companies within your specific sector. Similarly, VC firms that specialize in or have a significant number of investments in your industry are likely to have a strong understanding of the market dynamics, key players, and potential future trends. Review the investment portfolios of VC firms and the backgrounds of individual partners. Don't hesitate to ask potential investors about their understanding of your industry, their insights into the competitive landscape, and their vision for the future of the sector.

## 5. Exit Strategy

Depending on your long-term vision for your startup, certain investors might be more suitable than others.

Venture Capital firms, operating on roughly a 10-year cycle with a 4-6 year active investment period, inherently require an exit (typically acquisition or IPO) within 5-10 years to return capital to their investors. If you're not aiming for this kind of exit within that timeframe, traditional VC funding might not be the best fit. Always inquire about a VC's fund cycle stage to understand their investment horizon.

If a rapid exit isn't your primary goal, consider investors with potentially longer horizons, such as angel investors. Angels may be more open to long-term returns or even dividends, offering greater flexibility.

Conversely, if your ambition is to be acquired by an established industry leader, seeking investment from Corporate VCs or Family Offices can be strategic. The parent corporation behind a CVC is often a natural acquirer of its portfolio companies, and similarly, the core business of a family office can be a potential acquirer for startups in their fund's portfolio. 

## 6. Reputation

When determining which investors to target or from which to take investment, consider their reputation. Connect with founders of their portfolio companies and make sure to reach out beyond the references provided by the investor themselves. Try to get an honest opinion on how the investor truly operates: are they reliable and responsive? What tangible value, beyond the investment, do they bring to the table (mentorship, connections, strategic advice)?  Inquire specifically about the quality and relevance of introductions they can facilitate, whether to potential customers, strategic partners, or even future investors. It's also vital to understand how investors treat their portfolio companies, especially when startups face challenges. An investor who abandons or treats founders poorly during tough times can be detrimental to your venture and morale.

Be wary of 'almost-CEO' angels who may overstep and seek excessive control over your company's direction. First-time angels might be enthusiastic, but they lack the experience of seasoned investors and might end up requiring lots of your time for education.

Ideally, prioritise investors with a strong track record of investing in successful startups or even unicorns. Not only is this a sign that they know what they are doing, but having investors with a good reputation interested in your round or even on your cap table helps in convincing other investors.
