# Which Strategy to Adhere to?

There are two main strategies when it comes to timing your fundraising: the crunch raise (aiming for funding within a short timeframe) and the rolling raise (continuous engagement with investors). While both can work if executed well, avoid blending them. Let's explore each:

## The Crunch Raise

The crunch raise aims to complete fundraising within a focused two to three-month period. Closing quickly frees up time for execution and can create urgency, fostering competitive bids that may increase your valuation. This strategy often works well with angels who can make faster decisions, and is particularly effective for startups in high-demand sectors or when the round is expected to be oversubscribed. When participating in the EWOR Grand Pitch, a crunch raise is highly recommended to capitalize on the immediate investor attention and buzz. 

The Crunch Raise consists of three phases:

- Preparation phase** **(1+ month): Thoroughly prepare and refine your investor deck, develop supporting materials like a financial model, organize your data room, and identify target investors.
- Meeting Phase (2 weeks): Begin with practice meetings with less critical investors to hone your pitch. Dedicate the latter half to meetings with top-tier VCs and angels, remembering that VCs often require multiple conversations.
- Closing & Negotiation (1-2 weeks): Allocate two weeks for follow-up, pushing for investor decisions, negotiating term sheets, and finalizing the round.

Even with a crunch raise, starting investor engagement early is beneficial. This “casual dating” involves sharing your deck and seeking advice without a formal pitch, allowing you to build relationships and gather valuable insights that can accelerate your fundraising later.

## The Rolling Raise

The rolling raise involves continuous engagement with potential investors as they approach you or are introduced. When a good fit arises, you can offer them a deal. To incentivise timely decisions despite the ongoing nature of your fundraising, always include a valuation cap with a clear expiration date. This encourages investors to act rather than waiting indefinitely.

The rolling raise offers flexibility for learning and refining your pitch and investor deck over time, allowing you to balance fundraising with ongoing execution. It's common to close multiple smaller tranches in a relatively short period. For example, you might raise $150k at a $5 million post-money cap, followed by $200k at $7 million three months later, and another $200k at $9 million three months after that. By limiting each tranche's size and setting an overall target number of investors, you can create a sense of urgency within a continuous fundraising effort.
