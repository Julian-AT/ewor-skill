# Cost Assumptions

For startups, we believe it is best to think of your costs in three categories:

1. **Unit costs** (directly attributable to either the sale of a product or the acquisition of a customer or delivery of the product), which should be in your unit economics calculation. This is covered in the next lesson.
2. **General costs **(such as other product related costs, office costs, marketing costs not attributable to your product, etc.). Those costs scale, but not 1:1 with the sale of more products / acquisition of new users.
3. **Staff costs** (your employees). We recommend you treat those separately because the assumptions about your staff costs should be carefully mapped. We recommend you do so in a ‘headcount table’, while the growth of your general costs is tracked in a ‘growth table’.

Below, there is a screenshot of a headcount table, a growth table and the resulting overall costs. Good investors will have a thorough look at this table and see if your assumptions are realistic. We recommend that you answer the following questions for yourself when building your headcount and growth table:

- How strong do my staff costs scale with my total number of customers / newly acquired customers? Make sure to differentiate between new users and overall users. As described above, your onboarding staff members growth might be directly proportional to your user growth, while other staff positions, such as developers, might scale concavely or linearly compared to exponential user growth.
- How do my staff costs relate to milestones that I talk about in my pitch deck? For example, when you announce a launch of a new product in 2 years from now, you would assume that developer costs go up close to the launch. Alternatively, when you announce a specific marketing stunt at one point in time, check how many marketing team members you have planned for by then and if you believe they can handle the described marketing stunt. If your headcount table plans for three marketing members in two years from now, but you pitched your investors about a marketing stunt that would require dozens of them, you're admitting that your assumptions are not valid.
- For every single year, how many staff members do I have in each category, and how do I feel about them getting the job done for the goals I planned for? Make sure to sanity-check every single year in your forecast.
- How does my growth table link to other numbers in my cost and revenue calculation?  For example, link office costs to your total employee number, but in a degressive fashion. You may also want to link other marketing costs to the number of total users or legal costs to your continental expansion plans.
