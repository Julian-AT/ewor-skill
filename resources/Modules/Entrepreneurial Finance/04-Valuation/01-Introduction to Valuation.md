# Introduction to Valuation

Valuation plays an important role in many life situations, as knowing what an asset is worth and what determines its value is the basis of all sophisticated decision-making. Even though we can value a broad range of different things, the core principles to valuation always remain the same. 

You should, however, be careful when valuing a firm or house, as you should never forget about biases. As your subjective view on the value of an asset is often made before one even starts valuing the asset, valuations offer a lot of potential for biases. In some cases, the mere fact of an individual buying and owning a house, biases their opinion upwards. The potential for this upward bias becomes even more apparent when you value your own startup.

There are different ways how potential biases can enter a valuation:  

- In the inputs and the assumptions made
- By comparing obtained results with observed market prices 
- By adjusting differences between results and expected values with “synergies”, “control premia”, etc.

In general, there are two views on valuation: People who think **valuation is science **and** **thus use a correct model with correct input numbers will deliver a correct result. Then there are people who think** valuation is art**, as no one can predict the future. The argument from that side is that you can always manufacture a model in a way that the result shows what you want. 

In reality, however, the truth most likely lies somewhere between those views. This comes from the fact that besides the biases already discussed, there is always a certain level of imprecision and uncertainty that enters the valuation model and prevents it from being a clear science. Mostly, this uncertainty arises from the following three factors: 

1. **Estimation uncertainty: **Any valuation requires a model of the reality, which leads to uncertainty. For some projects that uncertainty is relatively low (e.g. a solar panel park where you have a lot of weather data and data about the panels available). For other projects such as startups this uncertainty might be very high 
2. **Firm-specific uncertainty: **A firm might perform differently than expected. This might affect its value
3. **Macroeconomic uncertainty: **Even if a firm performs as expected, changes in non-firm specific factors such as interest rates or global pandemics might affect its value

While **estimation uncertainty** can be reduced by using more complex models that rely on more information and input data, increasing a model's complexity is not always beneficial. There is a trade-off between precision and costs (e.g. other uncertainty).

Dealing with **firm-specific and macroeconomic uncertainty**, however, requires us to take assumptions, which are usually more influential than the technical part of valuation. Therefore, you should: 

- Build models based on the best unbiased information available
- Identify the input factors that matter most to your valuation result and provide sensitivities of valuation results to certain input factors
- Be transparent and discuss both valuation *results* and valuation *uncertainty*. In addition, you should provide valuation ranges rather than point estimates and provide probabilities for different valuation results

In this lesson, you will gain an overview over different valuation techniques and have a closer look at those that are most used in entrepreneurial finance.
