# Relative Value (RV)

While fundamental valuation methods such as the DCF method discussed in the previous lesson try to evaluate the value of a venture based on its expected future cash flow, growth and risk and therefore does not compare the venture to other ventures, the relative valuation (RV) method evaluates the value of an asset based upon how similar assets are currently priced by the market. The underlying assumption of the relative value method is therefore that ventures with similar cash flows and risks should have a similar value. 

Performing a relative valuation is much easier than going through all calculations necessary in the DCF method. To come up with a relative valuation, one only has to search for market data on comparable companies, scale this market data to a common variable, and adjust it for differences across the comparable assets. The dimensions of comparability thereby include industry, business model, stage, size, and accounting ratios. This simplicity is one of the reasons why relative valuation is so popular.

Another reason is that a relative valuation is relatively easy to sell compared to DCF valuations, as the necessary explanations easily fit into a short sales pitch. Furthermore, it is easier to defend, as the multiples used to calculate the value come from what the market is currently paying for similar firms.

However, it is also important to understand that this ease with which a relative valuation can be made can easily result in inconsistent estimates of the value of a venture. This is for example the case if key variables such as risk, growth, or cash flow potential are ignored or misjudged. Furthermore, the relative valuation method can be misused to justify almost any value, as the one performing the valuation can freely choose the multiple and the comparable firms on which the valuation is based. In the entrepreneurship world, the relative value method is widely used for exit strategy valuations for IPOs or acquisitions.
