# The First Chicago Method

Just like the VC method, the First Chicago Method (Named after the first Chicago Bank) combines elements of the DCF and relative valuation methods discussed in the previous lessons and is also based on a successful exit. In fact, it works very similar to the VC method, but takes it one step further. 

![EWOR assets_formula 17](/api/media/file/EWOR%20assets_formula%2017%404x%20(1).png)

To calculate your venture's value based on the first chicago method, you follow the same steps as in the VC method. This time, however, you create a certain number of discret scenarios (e.g. success, moderate, and failure). You then calculate the EXIT-value for each of the created scenarios and assign a certain probability to each scenario (e.g. 20% success, 60% moderate, and 20% failure). The only other difference of the First Chicago Method to the VC method is that it uses an estimate of the opportunity cost of capital as the discount rate, which is often considered more realistic. However, the method itself provides no basis for assigning probabilities to the different scenarios used in the valuation. Also, there is no general guidance on how to estimate the above-mentioned opportunity cost of capital.
