# Discounted Cash Flows (DCF)

The discounted cash flow (DCF) valuation method is a fundamental valuation model used throughout finance. It attempts to determine the value of an investment today based on assumptions of how much money this investment will generate in the future. Thereby, it uses future cash flows to project an investments' ability to generate money. It then uses discount rates to convert these future dollars into their present value equivalents, which tells us what they are worth today. As we use certain discount rates to discount those future cash flows back to the present, we call the method the discounting cash flow method. 

As you should know by now, the DCF method values a firm on the basis of future performance. This makes it a good method to value a startup as you often, especially during the (pre-)seed stage, will not have realised any historical performance yet. On the negative side, however, one can argue that the DCF method is nothing more than a mathematical operation. This operation is very sensitive to the input variables and thus very sensitive to your capability to create an accurate forecast.

A DCF model always follows the same simple steps:

## 1. Create financial projections for your venture

First of all, you need to create a financial model for your venture. Among other things, this model should project your revenue streams, costs, expenses and investments for the years ahead. Please refer to the previous sections to learn more about your profit & loss statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement, as you will need those to come up with your free cash flow. 

## 2. Forecast your venture's future free cash flows

In the DCF method, the future financial performance is measured by the future free cash flows. Usually, a DCF model uses free cash flow forecasts for the next five to ten years.  The free cash flow is a good measurement for the performance of a firm, as it represents the cash that is available after all potential short term liabilities have been fulfilled. ### 3. Determine the Discount Factor

As you might already know, the value of money deteriorates over time. This means, future money is worth less today and is the reason why we have to discount the expected future cash flows. Thereby, the discount factor we use to discount our projected free cash flow is calculated based on the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WAAC). The WAAC is a percentage and in essence defines the risk an investor is taking when investing in your venture. ### 4. Calculate the net present value of all your future cash flows

Now you have all the ingredients you need to estimate the value of your venture with the DCF method. All you need to do is to discount the future cash flows (FCF) with your discount factor. The value of your venture using the DCF method is therefore defined by the following formula:

![EWOR assets_formula 15](/api/media/file/EWOR%20assets_formula%2015)

What you see in this formula is that the value is calculated based on all future cash flows. However, in step 2 we said that you only forecast the FCF for the coming five to ten years. That is why you also need to calculate a terminal value (TV) that represents the value of the cash flows generated in the years after your FCF estimations. The only additional thing you need in order to calculate the TV is the expected growth rate (*g*) for all the FCF coming thereafter. If you want to use a conservative approach, you simply use the inflation percentage as growth rate (*g*). If you feel optimistic, however, you can also use a projected yearly growth rate (*g*) of the free cash flows of your firm. ### 5. Create Different Scenarios and Analyses

Having followed all these steps, you should now have a first estimate of your venture's value. As mentioned above, however, the mathematical operation you have performed is very sensitive to the input variables. It is therefore common practice to play around with different growth factors for your FCF estimations and to create the worst case, base case, and best case scenario. Also, as the result is heavily dependent on the adopted WACC, the risk indicator, and as the risk of not achieving the expected earnings is relatively high for a startup, it is better to set your WACC higher than lower.
