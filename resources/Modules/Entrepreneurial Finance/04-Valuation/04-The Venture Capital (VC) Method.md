# The Venture Capital (VC) Method

The venture capital (VC) method is a popular valuation method in the private equity and venture capital arena. It combines elements of the DCF and relative valuation methods discussed in the previous lessons and is based on a successful exit. Thereby, it uses a high discount rate (*IRR*) to capture time value, risk, bias of using success scenario cash flows, as well as dilution from subsequent financing rounds. 

The VC method was first described by Professor Bill Sahlman at Harvard Business School in 1987. It is a useful method for estimating a new venture value before a VC investor enters (pre-money). Unlike the DCF method that discounts future cash flows of a new venture, the VC method discounts the exit proceeds (EXIT value) at the planned exit date from the investor’s point of view. Thereby, the value of the venture is calculated by discounting the estimated EXIT-Value at the time of the sale or IPO by the expected return by the investor between the time of the entry and the sale. The expected return (Internal Rate of Return or just IRR) depends on several factors such as the risk assessment and usually lies between 25% and 100%. With the VC-method, the value of a new venture is calculated in two steps. 

1. **Calculate the expected EXIT value**

To value a venture with the VC method, you have to first estimate a key figure such as the turnover or the EBIT for the time of the expected exit (e.g. in 5 years). Like in the DCF method, this figure is estimated based upon your financial model. Second, based on this key figure, you derive the venture value for the exit date. This is where your knowledge about the RV-method comes in handy, as the venture value is derived from your key figure and a market multiple (EXIT multiple).

1. **Discount the expected exit proceeds to derive the value of the venture at the time of the investment**

Even though the VC Method is one of the dominant methods used in early stage investments, it also has some shortfalls. First, the value of the firm is based on unknown/speculative key figures such as the turnover or EBIT. Second, it relies upon valuation multiples in deriving a terminal valuation and therefore suffers from the same potential pitfalls as the RV method. Finally, the IRR required by investors is a subjective determination based upon the perceived risk of a given investor. Therefore, the venture valuation derived by the VC method can produce wildly varying results between investors.
