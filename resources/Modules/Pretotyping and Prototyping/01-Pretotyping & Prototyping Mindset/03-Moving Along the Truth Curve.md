# Moving Along the Truth Curve

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/hOZq20o85Z0)

In the previous lesson, we introduced the Truth Curve—a framework that shows how different types of research yield different levels of insight. At the lowest-effort end of the curve, we focused on conversations, which are easy to conduct but often deliver surface-level truth. Nevertheless, conversations also tend to reveal the lowest degree of 'truth'.

If you want to discover which solutions people truly want—and are willing to use or pay for—you need to invest in uncovering a higher level of 'truth'. To do that, pretotypes and prototypes are the easiest and cheapest options you can make use of.

## Pretotypes

The concept of pretotyping was introduced by former Google engineer Alberto Savoia in his book *The Right It* (2010). In his words, a pretotype is a low-fidelity version of a product designed to test whether people will actually use or buy it—*before* you invest heavily in building it. Think of it as a "pretend prototype"—a fast, cheap way to simulate the core experience of your product or feature using minimal time and resources.

Pretotypes help answer critical early questions like:

- Does this solution truly address the problem I identified?
- Is the value proposition clear and compelling?
- Would someone be willing to pay for this?

Or, at an even higher level, to quote Savoia: “Is this idea The-Right-It”? Simply put, you are asking,  “Should this iteration of my solution be built?”

## Prototypes

While pretotypes help you test *whether* a solution is worth building, prototypes help you explore *how* to build it. 

A prototype is a more tangible, often higher-fidelity version of your product or service. Prototypes are typically used after initial desirability and viability have been validated—often through pretotyping. Though there are exceptions to this order, especially in deep-tech or engineering-heavy ventures.

Where pretotyping asks, *“Should we build it?”*, prototyping asks, “*How do we build it right?”*  They are especially useful for testing technical feasibility and usability, helping you refine product mechanics, user flows, and experience before moving to full development.

## Rapid Experimentation Methods

One of the most influential approaches to rapid experimentation in the startup world is the Lean Startup method, introduced by Eric Ries in his 2011 book The Lean Startup. At its core is the concept of validated learning—gathering real insights from real users through a simple loop:

![The Lean Startup Methodology (Adapted from Eric Ries)](/api/media/file/M4-L1T3-BuildMeasureLearn.png)

Rather than relying on opinions or assumptions, Ries advocates building something quickly to test a hypothesis, measuring how people actually interact with it, and using those insights to refine your next steps. This feedback loop helps you continuously improve your solution based on market signals—not guesswork. While Ries often uses the term Minimum Viable Product (MVP), many of the examples in his book closely resemble what we call pretotypes and prototypes. What matters isn’t the label, but the logic: you test early and often to reduce risk and avoid wasting resources.

> The best way to predict the future is to create it.

In short, pretotyping and prototyping fit perfectly into this approach. They are lightweight, low-cost ways to test your riskiest assumptions—before investing heavily in development. Instead of asking people what they think, you let them interact with a tangible expression of your idea and observe what they do. By grounding your decisions in actual behaviour, you avoid the danger zone of false validation and move closer to building something people truly want.

## Diving Deeper

**The Right It by Alberto Savoia**
The 2010 book by Alberto Savoia, the father of pretotyping. In this book, Savoia shares lots of examples of companies that engaged in pretotyping and a compelling argument as to why to pretotype in the first place.

**The Lean Startup by Eric Ries**
One of the most well-known books in the startup world, in which Ries describes the Lean Startup Methodology he developed. 

**The $100 Startup by Chris Guillebeau**
Guillebeau shares a method for fast iteration and learning to ensure you don’t invest lots of time and money in an idea that won’t work. In addition, he shares lots of examples of how people started successful businesses using this method.

**Pascal Weinberger on Prototyping for Moonshot Ideas**
For some solutions, the most critical hypothesis is not whether people would be willing to pay for the solution, but whether the solution can be built in the first place. In this [Podcast Episode](https://open.spotify.com/episode/71IsKY7TPwxfS4o5yNGyVa?si=62jHH8lXSsO4BRbGfCqR9A&t=35), Pascal Weinberger, CEO of Bardeen and former head of the AI & Rapid Prototyping team of the Telefonica Moonshot Factory, talks about a different approach to prototyping.
