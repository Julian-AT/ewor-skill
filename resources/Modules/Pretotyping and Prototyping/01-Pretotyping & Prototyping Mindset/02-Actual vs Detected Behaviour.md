# Actual vs Detected Behaviour

When testing whether a solution is worth building, founders often seek early validation signals to guide their next steps. These signals usually fall into three categories:

- Stakeholder and expert opinions
- Trends and historical data
- Observable user and customer behaviour

In *A Problem Worth Solving*, we determined that asking people about hypotheticals in the future does not yield helpful information. We talked about minimising fluff by talking about specifics in the present.

![EWOR Actual versus Detected Behaviour Framework: statements about a hypothetical future fall in the danger category.](/api/media/file/M4-L1T2-ActualVsDetected%20(for%20platform).png)

Asking people about their opinion of your idea or whether they would interact with it is no more recommended now that you ideated a solution than it was during the customer discovery phase. As such, the only data we recommend basing your product decisions on is the observed and measured behaviour of users and customers.  Still, it’s important to understand why relying on the other two can lead to costly mistakes, which we’ll illustrate with cautionary tales.

## Opinions of Stakeholders and Experts

**Quibi**

Founded in 2018 by Jeffrey Katzenberg (former chairman of Walt Disney Studios) and Meg Whitman (former CEO of eBay and HP), Quibi raised an astonishing $1.75 billion before launch. Backed by Hollywood studios and telecom giants, it positioned itself as a mobile-first streaming platform offering short-form, vertical video for young audiences.

When Quibi launched in April 2020, it offered more than 50 shows and movies featuring stars like Jennifer Lopez, Chrissy Teigen, and Reese Witherspoon. The app was downloaded 1.7 million times in its first week, yet few users converted to paying subscribers. As a result, Quibi pivoted to allow TV streaming within months—a move that undermined its original value proposition. By October 2020, subscriber numbers had fallen far short of the projected 7.4 million, and the company shut down entirely.

![Jeffrey Katzenberg at the launch of Quibi.](/api/media/file/Jeffrey%20Katzenberg%20at%20the%20launch%20of%20Quibi-1.webp)

According to *The Wall Street Journal*, Quibi’s failure came down to failed instinct. The founders, experts, and investors all seemed to have equated the high mobile screen time of young audiences and the reports on decreasing attention spans with a desire to watch 10-minute episodes on their phones. However, real user behaviour told a different story. It was reported that at the closing of Quibi, the company had $200 million in the bank. It later sold its content library for a reported $100 million to Roku. So of the $1.75 billion in investments, no more than $300 million remained just two years later. An expensive mistake that might have been prevented if the product had been tested with users early on.

**Ring**

Sometimes, stakeholders and experts dismiss a solution that the market ends up embracing. In 2013, Jamie Siminoff, founder of the video doorbell company Ring, pitched on *Shark Tank* asking for $700,000 in exchange for 10% equity. All of the Sharks passed—one even claimed, *“You will never be able to sell this on QVC.”*

The market proved otherwise. Just a few years later, Ring sold 140,000 units in under 24 hours on QVC, marking one of the platform’s most successful product launches. By 2018, Amazon acquired the company for over $1 billion.

## Trends and Historical Data

When it comes to trends, many founders tend to follow them as a shortcut to success. Unfortunately, copying what worked yesterday doesn’t guarantee users want it today.

In late 2019, Disney+ launched to massive success, attracting over 28 million paying subscribers within just a few months. Analysts quickly concluded that subscription-based streaming still had plenty of room to grow and that users were willing to pay for multiple services. Riding that momentum, CNN partnered with McKinsey to design its own streaming platform, CNN+. With a $1 billion budget and a target of 2 million subscribers in the first year, they leaned heavily on industry data and expert forecasts.

What they didn’t do was test real user interest. CNN+ launched in March 2022—and shut down just four weeks later. Audiences weren’t eager for another subscription; they were already experiencing subscription fatigue. This was yet another case where trends suggested opportunity, but only user behaviour could have revealed the truth. As Sushil Prabhu, CEO of Dropp, observed:

“CNN+ could have avoided the shutdown outcome by better understanding the consumer pulse of subscription fatigue.”

## Behaviours of Users and Customers

A famous example of user behaviour outweighing expert opinion is the story of *Harry Potter*.

When J.K. Rowling first submitted *Harry Potter and the Philosopher’s Stone*, 12 publishers rejected it. The feedback was consistent: the book was too long, too niche, or simply not marketable. But one publisher, Bloomsbury, decided to take a chance—not because an expert believed in it, but because of an 8-year-old reader. Nigel Newton, Bloomsbury’s chairman, gave the first chapter to his daughter. She devoured it and immediately asked for more. That genuine enthusiasm convinced Bloomsbury to publish the book—and the rest is history.

The *Harry Potter* series went on to sell over 500 million copies, becoming the best-selling fantasy series of all time. Thirteen experts said no; one real reader’s behaviour said yes—and it became one of the greatest publishing investments ever made.

The only data we recommend basing your product decisions on is the observed and measured behaviour of users and customers.
