# Examples of Desirability Pretotypes  

Below, we will discuss some pretotypes that can serve as inspiration for when you will be building your own pretotypes.

## Fake Door

**Test: Initial Level of Interest**
**Costs: $**

By simply suggesting the existence of an undeveloped solution, a fake door pretotype can gauge the initial level of interest in this solution. This can be done, for instance, by creating a visual representation of the solution. 

To give an example, imagine a restaurant that simply places a new product on the menu, or another company that creates a website to showcase their potential offerings. In either case, it becomes possible to see how much interest actually surrounds that product or service.

### Dropbox Product Video

In 2010, the Dropbox founders and engineers were working to prove the technical feasibility of its product. This was no easy task and would likely take another few years. However, they were also still grappling with their leap-of-faith questions: “If we can provide a superior customer experience, will people give our product a try?”.

To answer this question, Drew Houston, founder of Dropbox, made a video showcasing how the product would work — this is sometimes referred to as a *Media of the Future Pretotype*. Note that this was **not **an actual product demonstration, as the product wasn’t functional yet.

This video garnered a lot of attention after being posted online. “It drove hundreds of thousands of people to the website. Our beta waiting list went from 5,000 people to 75,000 people literally overnight. It totally blew us away.” Houston is quoted as saying. You can watch the original video [here](https://youtu.be/7QmCUDHpNzE).

## Facade

**Test: Initial Level of Interest**
**Costs: $$**

A facade pretotype tests the initial level of interest in your solution by offering a non-scalable version that is also sometimes only offered for a limited amount of time or to a very specific geography. Unlike the Fake Door pretotype, a Facade pretotype actually delivers the product to the customer.

### Selling Cars Without Inventory

In the late 90s, founder and CEO, Bill Gross, had the idea to sell used cars online. To test whether people would be willing to buy cars online, he bought some advertisement space in a local newspaper advertising CarsDirect, for which he created a simple website listing available cars.

When people pressed ‘buy’, Bill would buy the car at retail value and deliver the car to the customer. On the first weekend, he sold 4 used cars and thereby validated his idea.#

### An Airbed and a Home-Made Breakfast

Legend has it that the co-founders of Airbnb wanted to test whether people would be willing to sleep in a stranger’s house for money rather than in an expensive hotel.

They created a simple website and offered an air mattress and breakfast at their San Francisco home for $80/night — way cheaper than any hotel room in the area. Soon, three people had signed up and paid $80 each, validating their assumption.

## Wizard of Oz

**Test: Initial Level of Interest**
**Costs: $-$$$**

The Wizard of Oz pretotype replaces your tech solution with human skills to simulate what the technology would do.

### A Can of Beans Turned Voice Assistant

Although he did not work on the actual product development team of the Amazon Echo, Alberto Savoia made various pretotypes of a voice assistant device to test his main assumption of whether he would actually use the device.

To do so, he wrapped a can of beans in black paper and placed it on his work surface and pretended it was real. He found himself speaking to the can of beans quite frequently, be it for weather updates or to just set timers. Later, he tested whether others would use it by concealing a phone behind the can of beans and having someone else on the other end of the line answer the questions people asked the can of beans.

### A Computer Without a Keyboard

In the 80s, IBM wanted to sell personal computers to everyone. However, very few people could type, hindering the adoption of personal computers. Internally, the IBM team started challenging the assumption that a personal computer would need a keyboard.

IBM ideated a solution of a speech-to-text machine but before actually building it, they wanted to test if people would actually buy it. People who had given positive feedback in interviews and more simple, pretotype tests were asked to test out their speech-to-text machine. 

They were placed in a room with a computer screen and a microphone but no keyboard. The person would speak in the microphone and text would appear on the computer screen. IBM did not actually have a working speech-to-text machine. Instead, they had someone in the other room transcribing what was being said in real time.

What IBM learned is that people did not want to speak into microphones all day. First, their voices get hoarse, but even more important, many office managers did not want their employees to speak confidential information out loud.

## Infiltrator

**Test: Initial level of Interest**
**Costs: $-$$$**

An infiltrator pretotype leverages the existing customer traffic in an established store (whether physical or online) to assess whether these potential customers would purchase your solution. This is achieved by placing your product on the store's shelves and observing the response.

Note that it does not have to be a functioning version of the product — it could even be an empty box with the imagined specifications on it or a relabelled product of the store you are testing in. In a digital store, you can show a mock-up of the product.

### Walhüb 

A San Francisco-based design agency had created what they called the WalHub, a switch plate that also served as a key and mail holder. They were bullish on the product but wanted to test whether people would buy it. They created about 100 WalHubs and decided to infiltrate the local IKEA.

They bought a used employee shirt on eBay and placed the WalHub, renamed to the Walhüb to fit the IKEA naming convention, at different places in the store. IKEA customers interacted with the product and tried to buy the product. Of course, the bar code didn’t work, and IKEA employees told the people they could just take the products for free. A cheap and easy test to validate levels of interest! You can watch the video the design agency made [here](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XVnqSIVG6WE).

## Impersonator

**Test: Initial Level of Interest**
**Costs: $-$$$**

An impersonator prototype uses an existing product and turns it into a representation of your solution. This is also sometimes called the relabel pretotype.

### Second-Day Sushi

An example often used by Alberto Savoia is that of second-day sushi. While having lunch with a few students at Stanford University, they debated whether or not anyone would be willing to buy sushi that wasn’t freshly made if they received a steep discount. 

Savoia recommended the students translate that idea into an XYZ hypothesis, such that, “At least 20% of packaged-sushi eaters will try Second-Day Sushi if it’s half the price of freshly packaged sushi.”

Then, he told the students to make it an XYZ hypothesis, something that can be tested here, now and for cheap, which became, “At least 20% of students buying packaged sushi at Coupa Café today at lunch will choose Second-Day Sushi if it’s half the price of regular packaged sushi.”

He recommended the student print some labels stating “Second-Day Sushi — 50% Off” and put them on half of the Sushi containers at Coupa Café. The students would stay in the café and observe how many people bought sushi and how many of them picked up the half-priced sushi. The answer was zero. Zero students bought second-day sushi.

Note that in this scenario, the students used a combination of pretotypes. The first was the impersonator pretotype when they relabelled fresh sushi to second-day sushi, and the second was the infiltrator pretotype when using traffic generated by Coupa Café to test the product desirability.

### An Electrical Lotus Elise

In the early 2000s, Elon Musk wanted to build a $120,000 all-electric two-seater car. To test whether people would be interested in this car, he decided to make an impersonator prototype. Musk and his team converted a Lotus Elise by taking out the combustion engine and replacing it with an AC propulsion drivetrain and prototype battery pack; this car was called the Mule 1.

Local sports car enthusiasts were attracted to the car as it was driven around. To test the level of interest, Tesla started asking people for $5,000 cheques to be added to the waiting list. Several hundred depositors were said to have contributed to the test.
