# Examples of Feasibility Prototypes

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/B6w-ZGtbXqE)

Below, we will discuss some prototypes that can serve as inspiration for when you will be building your own prototypes for testing feasibility.

## Testing User Flows Using Paper Prototypes

When Alex and his team were working for a bank on developing a new user experience for customers that lost their credit or debit card, they created a simple paper prototype on Post-It notes of what the screens could look like in the app. They asked people to interact with the paper prototype where each screen was one Post-It and iterated until the user flow was intuitive to people. Only after that, did they build more high-fidelity mock-ups.

### Flexible Display for Drive-Thru ATM

The question to get answered was how users of a drive-thru banking terminal would operate a display out of their cars. Given that, the size and style of the cars can vary a lot. Since the position to operate from a sports car like a Ferrari would be very low to the ground, while the next car possibly being a giant SUV, like a Hummer, where the position would be almost a meter higher. One of the ideas was to mount the display on a swivel arm, so the users could pull it to the right position for them.

To build a swivel arm would be possible, the team knew. But it would take time and resources and would cost quite a bit in material and additional services. But why make that expense when all the team wanted to test was how the experience would fair with users? So they decided to use a human as the proxy for the swivel arm instead. Very flexible and very cheap, as it could be tested on day two after the idea was born and the experience was a little different from a fully functional technically built swivel arm. This way, testing was done fast, and the learnings helped to make decisions about the feasibility as well as the desirability of the idea.

### Electric Toothbrush for Kids

On one project, Alex and his team were hired to design an electric toothbrush for infants. They learned that young children have underdeveloped fine motor skills and therefore needed to test the right volume and weight of an electric toothbrush first. The way to figure this out cheaply and effectively is by applying Play-Doh to represent different sizes of the toothbrush handle.

### The Dimensions of an Aeroplane Toilet

In 2007, a new Passenger plane was being developed. There was a design competition ongoing amongst the design agencies. Most teams were working in 3D, but Alex and his team built a 3D model of a cross-section of the plane at scale. 

The executives of the aircraft company aimed for the toilets to adhere to the legally required dimensions without exceeding them, both to conserve space and to facilitate the addition of extra seats in the plane.

Alex and his team built a prototype of a toilet in the legally required dimensions. They invited the executives to go into this toilet, put their pants down, sit down on the toilet and pull it back up and come out. Immediately, the executives saw that this toilet would be way too small for people to actually use.

This is a great example of how experiencing a prototype can change people’s opinions. They had known all along what the legally required toilet dimensions were, but only once they experienced what that would mean that they realised it wouldn’t work.

### Volume Models

A volume model is a non-functional prototype built to the dimensions you have in mind to test out whether the dimensions make sense. Jeff Hawkins, co-founder of Palm Computing, created a wooden version of the Palm Pilot to test whether he would carry it around and gave ot to other people he met for meetings or lunches to see where they would put it and carry it. This is a very simple way to test whether the dimensions are user-friendly and acceptable.

### Service Prototypes

Alex and his team worked on the McDonald’s self-service terminal. The product that was developed was the display, but since the display would be part of a larger user journey, they prototyped the entire journey. Using some IKEA furniture and print-outs of the menus and promotional material, they created a prototype of a McDonald’s store. They asked people to come in and visit their store. The people would go through the entire service journey and provide feedback to the team, In return, they received a €10 voucher for an actual McDonald’s.
