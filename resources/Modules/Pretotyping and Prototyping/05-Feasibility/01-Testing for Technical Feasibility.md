# Testing for Technical Feasibility

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/lJclxxAih1Q)

The third set of leap-of-faith assumptions revolves around the technical feasibility of your solution. Can you actually build it? As thinking up some amazing wizardry of tech is one thing, making it work, and especially making all of it work, is a whole different story. 

## Proof of Concept

Proof-of-concept hypotheses centre on the requirements of the solution. This does not mean you have to build the solution in its entirety. Questions a feasibility prototype will help to answer:

- What are the technical challenges of developing the solution?
- Will the functionality fulfil our target user requirements?
- Will the solution create the experience that we have in mind and that will influence the behaviour we aim for with the users? 
- Can I estimate how many resources I need to develop the product? (e.g. development costs, invested time of the founding team, external capital, required development partnerships, etc.)
- Is it worth investing in a certain feature? Is the cost value equation positive?
- What could a realistic development roadmap for the product look like? What to prioritise when?

**Note: **Feasibility prototypes, especially when there is proprietary technology involved, can take considerably longer to build and are more expensive than desirability or viability prototypes. That's why it's a good idea to stage them — to go from low resolution prototypes through many iterations to higher and more refined iterations.

> Start out by building prototypes to determine the specifications required. Once you have a good grasp of the specifications, build prototypes based on specifications.

For some of you, testing for feasibility might be the first time that you build a visual representation of your idea. When doing this, you will notice that you will likely uncover feasibility issues yourself without even having to show it to potential customers and users.

You might also discover that you and your teammates were not completely on the same page when it comes to what the solution should look and feel like. Therefore, make sure to start with the cheapest possible representation of your solution (often a low-resolution form like a paper prototype) before investing time and money into higher resolutions that are more polished and functional. 

## The Three R’s

The three R’s are a good principle to follow when thinking about building a prototype:

- **Right:** When deciding about the form the prototype should take, and therefore the method you chose to build it, always also think about the audience you will show it to and what you want them to experience and what you want to get answered from them. The prototype for the person that will be using it should look very different to the one that might be paying for it. Or a prototype that you want to show to possible investors should look different to the one you will show to possible development partners. 
- **Rapid:** Build one prototype for each question you like to get answered. This leaves it open to follow different directions and make very fast iterations. And even though this sounds like more work, it ultimately makes you faster. 
- **Rough:** Never spend too much time on building a prototype. Rather stop early when the desired expression for the intention of that prototype shows. Especially the quick and dirty early prototypes are all about learning rather than validation. You should always be a little bit ashamed of showing your first prototypes to the world. But never doubt, every prototype offers the opportunity to learn by testing them. In addition, it helps you to not fall in love with your own prototypes and the connected habit of defending them in front of your testers.

## Prototype Dimensions

When developing your prototypes to test feasibility, consider the following dimensions of your prototype which fall into the categories of “Looks like”, “Behaves like” and “Works like” prototypes. So the dimension should follow its intended learning:

- Form; such as the size, proportions, aesthetics, and ergonomics
- Action; functioning, mechanical interaction
- Behaviour; how would people use it, where would they keep it (when not in use), how would they discard it?
- Context; in which context should people interact with or encounter your product

Oh, and don’t forget — feasibility prototypes, especially when iterated frequently, are a fantastic way to show both customers and investors how quickly you're progressing. They’re one of the clearest ways to demonstrate milestones and momentum!
