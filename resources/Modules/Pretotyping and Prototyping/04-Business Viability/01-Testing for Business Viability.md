# Testing for Business Viability

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/of7SnwONQLA)

After having established the desirability of your product, the next biggest assumption usually centres around business viability. As mentioned earlier, you have likely already tested certain aspects of your business model when testing for desirability.

## Proof of Profitability

Testing for business viability means looking for proof of profitability. Even if you don’t yet know the exact costs of distributing your solution, testing for viability can give you insight into your customer acquisition costs and customer lifetime value. Together, these metrics help you understand the maximum amount you can afford to spend on distribution. If the market is not willing to pay enough for you to make a profit on the solution, there is no need to develop the solution.

Some questions a viability pretotype or prototype can help answer are:

- How much is the target audience willing to pay for the solution?
- How often will people come back for my solution?
- How many people would renew their subscription?
- Can I observe early signs of market acceptance and interest in the current direction of my solution?
- Does my solution have enough space in people’s lives, considering the competition from existing solutions for the same need?
- How does my solution compare to the existing products and solutions available in the market?

### The Story of NEWNOW

When Daniel was building NEWNOW, a B2B SaaS company offering AI tools to businesses, he and his team wanted to test whether businesses would be willing to pay a few thousand dollars per user.

Without building anything, they went to large corporations telling them about the (still non-existent) solution and that it would cost a lot of money to maintain and hence the licence fee per user would be quite high. The companies were happy to pay that amount of money.

Later on, they aimed to examine how typical users would interact with their product. They built rudimental interfaces that they offered as a desktop app. The app did not connect to the cloud and didn’t have any features that allowed NEWNOW to control the product. Users could do whatever they wanted with the app. Initially, the feedback they received was not positive — users had not used the interfaces at all. After talking with them, they found out that users wanted to be able to upload data into the interface. After making that adjustment, users started using the interfaces regularly.
