By now, you have probably run a few experiments and gathered data on how people interact with your pretotypes and prototypes. In this lesson, we will discuss how to interpret your data and what your next move should be.

Let’s take the previously discussed meal box subscription for people with diabetes as an example. Assume your XYZ hypothesis was that at least 10% of diabetes patients will sign up for an $80 per month, diabetes-friendly meal box subscription. You translated that hypothesis into an XYZ hypothesis that goes:

> “At least 25% of diabetes patients visiting the diabetes clinic in my local hospital sign up for the waitlist for the meal box subscription”.

The results from your first experiment are not what you hoped for. Only 10% of people enter the waitlist.

You wonder whether you caught your target audience at the right time, as most people come to the clinic to pick up their prescription medication. You decide to run the experiment again, but now at a local dietician's office. The third iteration of your experiment involves a nicer-looking folder, and the fourth a reduction in the monthly subscription fee.

Your results will likely mimic one of the following three graphs:

![Your data will show whether your hypothesis is correct or not. Sometimes, the data is inconclusive.](/api/media/file/M4-L6T1-ThreeGraphs.png)

## Outcome 1: Your Hypothesis Was Proven

Congratulations, that is good news! 

This means you can proceed to the subsequent hypotheses and formulate tests to validate them. The hypothesis you test next should build on the hypothesis you just proved. In our meal box subscription example, the** next** XYZ hypothesis could be, “25% of diabetes patients on the waitlist will pay $50 for a 4-week trial period.”

## Outcome 2: Your Hypothesis is Neither Proven Nor Disproven

It seems that certain diabetic patients who visit their local dietician's office and see your flyer show interest in your $70 meal box subscription waitlist, but note how specific that statement is.

There might be tons of reasons why you are not able to reach a 20% conversion to your waitlist. Is the dietician’s office not the right place to catch their attention? Is your flyer not compelling enough? Is the price still too high? Or, are people sceptical of the quality that can be delivered for that price point, and should it be increased?

Keep iterating! Can you ask the dietician to recommend your service? Can you hire a freelance designer to work on your flyer? Can you A/B test the price point? 

Bear in mind, you shouldn’t stay in this ambiguous place for too long. Set clear thresholds for when a hypothesis is proven, and don’t adjust those levels after gathering data. In addition, set a clear number of iterations you allow yourself to test before declaring a hypothesis unproven. Keep in mind that small incremental changes are very unlikely to achieve significantly higher levels of (sustained) interest in your solution.

> A startup should not be optimising, as that often yields small incremental changes. Not enough to go viral or achieve viral growth.

## Outcome 3: Your Hypothesis is Disproven

While unfortunate, if you have tinkered with your pretotype or prototype, and you are not able to reach the ‘proven’ threshold, it is time to pivot. 

In the event that customer discovery research uncovered a need for diabetes patients for meals that contain high fibre and minimal added sugars and refined carbohydrates, you can return to your ideated list of concepts and ideas.

What other ideas seemed promising? Maybe an on-the-go snack, an app that allows patients to scan food items and determine whether it fits their diet, or an app that would tell them how much insulin would be required to digest a certain meal.

Note that after having proven a few hypotheses, your changes will likely be less dramatic. For example, if you confirm that a meal box subscription of $100 for 4 boxes feeding 4 people is effective, then you might want to examine for how long continuous subscribers maintained their plan. 

Following through on that, let’s say you have calculated that you need at least 70% of subscribers to continue their plan for at least 2 months to make up for the customer acquisition costs. If your tests show that 60% of subscribers stay for the second month, fret not. You don’t have to abandon the idea completely and test another possible solution, instead go back and test whether a slightly higher subscription price could work, or whether you can sell a $100 box for 3 people.
