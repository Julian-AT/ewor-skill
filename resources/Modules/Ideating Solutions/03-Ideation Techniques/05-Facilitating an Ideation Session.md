# Facilitating an Ideation Session

Every ideation session needs a facilitator. Since you are the one who identified the problem and are trying to come up with a solution, this will likely be you. The facilitator prepares the ideation session and moderates it. Their main job is to stimulate the mind, body, and senses of the participants to get as many wild and crazy ideas as possible.

## Preparation

In preparing for the ideation session, there are a few things we recommend you do.

### Develop the HMW Questions

Develop at least 10 HWM questions. In addition, come up with a few provocative and crazy ideas that you can use during the ideation session to get ideas flowing from your participants. If you plan to have multiple ideation sessions with varying groups of participants, we recommend you focus on a certain aspect of your synthesis result in each ideation session. For this step, we recommend working on the HMW questions either individually or with just your core team.

### Recruit Participants

Next, you will want to recruit 4 to 6 participants from different backgrounds (professional background, culture, age, gender, and country of origin). The benefit of recruiting people from different backgrounds is that they will all have varied experiences to draw from, resulting in a wide range of diverse ideas. Having too many people with experience in the problem you are trying to solve might actually hinder innovation.

### Choose a Format

Determine whether you will hold the ideation session in person or in a digital format. If possible, we recommend you go for an in-person ideation session as moving around, interacting with other people and using one’s entire field of vision stimulates creativity to a degree online tools cannot yet fully mimic.

## Moderation

As a facilitator, you also have a role to play during the ideation session. During the session, your job is to manage the flow of energy, enforce the rules and manage the level of abstraction of the generated ideas.

### Introduction

Begin with brief introductions, explain the session's goal and timeframe (at least 20 minutes for ideation, minimum 30 minutes total), and review the ideation rules.

### Warm-up

Use a warm-up exercise to get your participant in a mode of generating lots of ideas quickly and to build trust and psychological safety among participants. One of our favourite warm-up exercises is to divide your participants into small groups and ask them to take three minutes to come up with as many ideas as they can on how to make money with a cow. The team that generates the most ideas wins.

![A fun warm-up exercise is to ask people to ideate different ways to make money with a cow.](/api/media/file/M3-L2T5-MoneyCow)

### Ideation

After the warm-up exercise, present one of your ‘How Might We’ questions. If possible, write it on the (digital) whiteboard. Stimulate your participants to share their ideas. Make sure the ideas are concrete enough but not too detailed. If you notice a drop in energy, you can bring in one of your own provocative ideas. If this doesn’t help, you can also introduce a different ideation question.

![Make sure all ideas are captured.](/api/media/file/M3-L2T5-InPersonBrainstorming)

### Review & Cluster

When the ideation is over, spend a few minutes identifying similar ideas and moving the post-its to the same location on the board and drawing lines to connect ideas.

### Vote

Next, ask each participant to vote for their top three favourite ideas or idea clusters. The purpose is to swiftly identify which ideas your participants found most exciting. Also, always ask participants which of the ideas they would personally dedicate their time on. 

### Close

When the time is over, thank your participants for their engagement and contribution to the ideation session. Briefly describe the next steps you will undertake. Make sure to capture the output from your ideation session. You can take pictures if you meet in person, or save the digital board.
