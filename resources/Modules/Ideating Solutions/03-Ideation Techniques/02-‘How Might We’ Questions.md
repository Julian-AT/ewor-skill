# ‘How Might We’ Questions

Ideas can only be as good as the questions they are addressing. Therefore, we recommend coming up with “Brainstorm Questions”, or “Brainstorm Topics” before starting your ideation sessions. These are usually articulated starting with “How might we….?”. 

To better understand this concept, let’s take a look at an ideation session Alex and his team conducted: 

## The Story

An international airport hired Alex and his team to redesign its ground experience. Alex and his team observed that the different stages of the airport journey were so disconnected that they seemed to contradict each other and create confusion for passengers. In order to give the identified opportunity some context, Alex and his team created a persona and a challenge that the persona might face. 

In the hypothetical scenario, they envision a hurried mother of three who has just rushed through the airport only to wait hours at the gate. In this case, the mother had to hurry and pressure the kids to get through the airport quickly. Then once at the gate, she would have to find ways to entertain her children, as the plane was not boarding for a while. The team envisioned how the kids might have annoyed their fellow passengers who were already frustrated.

### Overcoming a Broad Situation

It is a broad situation and there are many ways to solve it. As previously mentioned, our brains struggle with questions that are too broad and to find answers that cover all. To overcome this, Alex and his team broke the need down into multiple 'How might we?' questions, using all the factors found in the sample scenario, like:

- **How might we use kids' energy to entertain fellow passengers?**

This question has the underlying assumption that the energy of the kids is not in itself a bad thing at all but needs to be channelled in the right direction. 

- **How might we separate the kids from fellow passengers?**

This question focuses your mind on removing the problem

- **How might we make the wait the most exciting part of the trip?**

This question asks you to assume the complete opposite of what is normally true. No one likes waiting to board. But who says that can’t be changed?

- **How might we entirely remove the wait at the airport?**

This question is questioning the idea that waiting is necessary in the first place.

- **How might we make the rush refreshing instead of hurried?**

This question changes the adjectives. Instead of thinking about the rush as hurried, can we change that?

- **How might we leverage the free time of fellow passengers to share the load?**

This question focuses your mind on unexpected resources. Can fellow passengers help?

- **How might we make playful, loud kids less annoying?**

This last question asks you to challenge the status quo.

As you can see, Alex and his team created different questions related to one situation. All these questions focus on the situation, but all explore a very different side to the problem. Some are about the mom, some about the kids, and some are about the other passengers. All of these questions get your mind to form different connections, and this doesn’t even scratch the surface of every question that could be asked!

## Special Types of HMW Questions

Additionally, two types of HMW questions are especially helpful in generating novel ideas: constraints and analogies.

### Constraints

HMW questions with conditions or constraints are questions such as:

- How might we solve this when in space?
- How might we address this as a criminal?
- How might we keep the problem from getting solved?

Recent studies have shown that creativity under constraints follows an inverted U-shape. A few constraints really spark creativity, while too many constraints stifle creativity.

![Adding some constraints has been shown to spark creativity.](/api/media/file/M3-L2T2-InvertedUCurve)

Researchers suggest that constraints can actually boost creativity because our brains are wired to conserve energy. Left unrestricted, we tend to make easy, familiar connections rather than exploring less obvious ideas. Constraints disrupt this pattern by limiting straightforward associations, pushing our brains to make more creative leaps. In fact, introducing artificial or even absurd constraints can amplify this effect, encouraging more unconventional and innovative thinking.

A constraint limits the number of easy associations your brain can make and forces it to jump to less obvious associations and ideas.

### Analogies

HMW questions using analogies are questions such as:

- How might we make the airport feel like a spa?
- How might we make an elevator service feel like a freshly made bed?

As we’ve discussed, the goal of brainstorming is to push our brains beyond obvious connections and toward more unconventional ideas. Using analogies helps achieve this by forcing us to compare our problem to something entirely different, creating new links and perspectives.

The most effective analogies for this process are “far analogies”—comparisons between concepts that share little in common. Research shows that brainstorming with far analogies leads to more creative ideas than using near analogies or none at all.

![Brainstorming using questions that use far analogies yields more creative ideas (adapted from Joel Chan et al (2011)).](/api/media/file/M3-L2T2-AnalogyResearchGraph)

## How To Create Your Own HMW Question

To create ‘How Might We’ questions, go back to your synthesis result. Use the pain points you have observed and heard about to guide the questions you develop. Good ‘How might we’ questions are framed appropriately, inspiring, boosting and directing. We recommend creating at least 10 ‘How Might We’ questions related to each of your opportunities. 

![M3-L2T2-ApproriateFraming](/api/media/file/M3-L2T2-ApproriateFraming)

### Appropriate Framing

First of all, a good HMW question is formulated not too wide, like “How might we better work together?”. A valid question. But actually, one that's hard to find an innovative answer for. Our brains are not very good at handling such large questions as they want to solve the entire problem with one idea. Such large questions should be broken up into smaller questions.

The questions should also not be too narrow, like “How can we collect our ideas on Post-its?”. See how the last question even offers the solution? That leaves little room for creativity. A more appropriate frame would be “How might we make our ideas more visible”. As our research might have told us already, “visibility” is an important aspect of a good working environment. By articulating your research findings into the HMW questions, you open up unique ways for new ideas to arise. 

![M3-L2T2-Inspiring](/api/media/file/M3-L2T2-Inspiring)

### Inspiring

A good HMW question is inspiring. It makes you connect ideas you might otherwise not have connected. An example of an inspiring question would be “How might we make an elevator service feel like a freshly made bed?”. This question about two obviously unrelated topics draws on your concept of a freshly made bed and makes you connect that to the topic of elevators. Without this prompt, you would likely not have connected those two different concepts.

![M3-L2T2-Boosting](/api/media/file/M3-L2T2-Boosting)

### Boosting

Boosting questions creates many new ideas. Like the question “How might we turn an elevator into the meeting point in a building?”. Another somewhat related (as it's still in an office context) but still unexpected combination that challenges you to think in a different direction.

![M3-L2T2-Directing](/api/media/file/M3-L2T2-Directing)

### Directing

A good HMW question focuses your mind on one of the pain points related to the situation. An example is, “How might we make the energy consumption on an elevator visible?”. The pain point related to the situation should stem from your Customer Discovery research.
