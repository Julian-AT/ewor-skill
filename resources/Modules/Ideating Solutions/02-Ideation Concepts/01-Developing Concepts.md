# Developing Concepts

Ideation using the HMW questions results in lots of ideas for different aspects of the problem statement. In this topic, we will discuss creating a concept using many of the individual ideas. 

Think of concepts as combining smaller ideas into a bigger, more powerful solution. Concepts represent a larger piece of the puzzle, bridging the gap between single ideas and multi-dimensional offerings.

## An Example of a Concept

Alex and his team were tasked with developing a new elevator concept to reduce setup complexity and costs on construction sites. After researching the entire elevator journey and exploring various ideation directions (assembly, service, additional services, drive systems), they generated numerous ideas through "How Might We" questions.

The team then clustered these ideas, experimenting with different combinations to create a range of unique elevator concepts. One standout concept featured a rail-based drive system, allowing the elevator to be built level by level, growing with the building. This eliminated the need for separate construction elevators and resulted in significant cost savings.

This concept also incorporated other innovative ideas from the ideation sessions. For example, the cabin floor was fixed to the drive unit, and foldable "X" structures formed the cabin walls, reducing setup time. Additionally, relocating the cabin lights to the corners improved accessibility for maintenance, further lowering costs.

By combining these ideas, Alex and his team achieved approximately 50% cost savings for their client. This highlights the importance of clustering and combining ideas to create real-world solutions. Combining is as important as ideation itself, bringing your vision to life by forming tangible products and experiences.
