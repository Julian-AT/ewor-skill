# Creativity

Creativity is an important part of Ideation. The more creative your ideas, the more likely it is you will build an innovative solution. As we’ve seen, unicorns rarely emerge from incremental innovation—it’s disruptive and radical ideas that create breakthrough success.

![Unicorns are seldom born from incremental innovation](/api/media/file/M3-L1T2-TypesOfInnovation%20%2B%20pop-up%20B)

### Defining Creativity

Creativity is the ability to apply imagination to generate ideas that address a challenge, while innovation is the act of bringing those ideas to life. However, most ideas aren’t entirely new—they’re simply combinations of existing ones. As Mark Twain noted in his autobiography, and as author William Inge put it even more succinctly, 

> Originality is undetected plagiarism.

To generate new ideas from old ones, you need two key things: a pool of existing ideas and the mental space to reshape them into something new. You already have the first—ideas drawn from innovations, products, solutions you've encountered, and thoughts in your mind. 

### Relearning Creativity

Coming up with innovative solutions will require you to let your creative juices flow. If you tend to think analytically, this might feel unfamiliar—even leading you to believe you're not creative at all.

But it’s a myth that some people are creative and others are not. In fact, we all have creativity within us. The real issue, as Sir Ken Robinson highlights in his famous TED Talk, *“*[*Do Schools Kill Creativity?*](https://youtu.be/iG9CE55wbtY?t=26)*”*, is that traditional education prioritises single correct answers and discourages mistakes. This focus on convergent thinking stifles creativity, overlooking the valuable learning opportunities that mistakes and divergent thinking provide—opportunities that go beyond what textbooks could ever offer.

This decline in creativity isn’t just theoretical—it has been observed in real-world studies. In the early 1960s, NASA faced complex challenges that demanded innovative solutions. To identify the most creative engineers and scientists, they enlisted general systems scientist George Land to develop a predictive creativity test. The task was simple: participants had to come up with as many uses as possible for a single paperclip.

However, this experiment led Land to a deeper question: *Where does creativity come from?* To investigate, he conducted a longitudinal study, testing children at ages 5, 10, and 15. The results were striking—creativity scores dropped dramatically over time. When adults later took the test, their scores were even lower, reinforcing the idea that creativity isn’t lost but gradually suppressed as we grow.

![The percentage of people reaching genius level on the George Land Creativity Test decreases with age (adapted from George Land & Beth Jarman).](/api/media/file/M3-L1T1-CreativityByAge)

The conclusion George Land and his co-authors drew from this data is that non-creative behaviour is learned.

> Non-creative behaviour is learned.

Since then, numerous studies have shown that the right processes can help anyone generate more creative ideas. In this module, we’ll explore some of the most powerful techniques and strategies for unlocking your creative potential.

### Diving Deeper

**Do Schools Kill Creativity by Sir Ken Robinson**
In this [Ted Talk](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iG9CE55wbtY&vl=en) that has been topping the list of most-watched TED Talks for years now, Sir Ken Robinson explains how school systems across the world make us less creative and what we can do about that.

**The Failure of Success by Dr George Land**
In this [TED Talk](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZfKMq-rYtnc), Dr George Land, the inventor of the creativity test, explains how you can reverse the schooling effect on your creativity by postponing judgment.

**John Cleese on Creativity**
In this [video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Pb5oIIPO62g), Monty Python author and actor John Cleese shares his tips for being more creative. At ~19:00 minutes, he shares why he had more creative and original ideas than a colleague he thought was more talented. Hint: it is because he took more time to wrestle with the problem.

**How to Be Creative by Neil Pavitt**
A quick read that is full of useful tools and tips to help you be more creative.

**Creativity Rules by Tina Seeling**
Written by Stanford Professor Tina Seeling, this book takes you through the innovation cycles. From imagination to creativity, innovation and entrepreneurship. 

**Where good ideas come from by Steven Johnson**
Steven Johnson studied the origin of ideas and innovation and describes 7 key patterns in this book. A summary of the book is also shared in his 2010 [TedTalk](https://www.ted.com/talks/steven_johnson_where_good_ideas_come_from).

**Seeing What Others Don’t by Gary Klein**
The famous naturalistic decision-making researcher Gary Klein describes his framework of how new ideas and insights come about. The book discusses 4 pathways that can lead to novel insights and offers tips on how to generate more insights. Dr Klein also gave a [TedTalk](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=n5OO9L67jL4) about the learnings described in his book.

**A Technique for Producing Ideas by James Webb Young**
A very quick read from advertising mogul James Webb Young on how to be more creative.
