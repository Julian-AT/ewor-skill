# From a Shortlist to a Top 10

The next step in idea selection is to apply the decision-making criteria of Ikigai to your list of ideas.

Ikigai is a Japanese concept that describes finding your purpose or reason for living. It emphasizes the balance of four key elements:

- **Mission:** Do something the world needs.
- **Profession:** Do something you can be paid for.
- **Vocation:** Do something you are good at.
- **Passion:** Do something you love.

When all four elements intersect, you experience Ikigai, a fulfilling and meaningful life.

![The Ikigai Framework](/api/media/file/M3-L4T3-Ikigai)

This framework can help select the idea to focus on. When applying the innovation Venn diagram framework to your idea list, you have already eliminated ideas the world doesn’t need (people desirability) and ideas you can’t get paid for (business viability). Let’s take a closer look at the other two criteria.

### Something you are good at

Since you'll be the driving force behind your startup, especially in the early stages, choose an idea that aligns with your skills. While you can eventually hire help, possessing (or easily acquiring) the necessary skills for executing your idea is crucial. The idea is that, collectively, the founding team possesses all the required skills to develop and sell the solution.

Review your shortlist and assess each idea based on how well it matches your current skill set. For example, if an idea demands medical industry connections and coding skills, but your background is in the arts, it might not be the best fit.

### Something you love

Reid Hoffman, co-founder of LinkedIn, said in an interview with TechCrunch:

> The reason why so many startups fail is because the founders get tired. They don’t get tired of working twelve hours a day, they get tired of the pain. They get tired of being punched in the face. And so they give up.

Building a company is a rollercoaster. To navigate the ups and downs and stay motivated over the long haul, you need a genuine passion for your idea.

So, while this is the fifth criterion, it's arguably the most important. No matter how great an idea seems, if it doesn't excite you, it's not the right one for you. Review your shortlist and gauge your excitement for each idea. Don't overthink it – feel it. Does it make you smile? Do you keep coming back to it? Those are the ideas you should prioritise. By scoring each idea based on whether you and your co-founders have or can acquire the relevant skills and whether the idea excites you, you probably reduced the idea list to roughly 10 ideas.
