# Sales Conversion Framework

You’re now in a position where you deeply understand who your target prospect is and how to best communicate with them. It’s time to take what you’ve learned and use it to convert prospects. Note that the following framework is a combination of best practices from industry experts like SalesHacker, John Barrows, Jordan Belfort, and others who will be listed in the resources to follow.

Everything starts with the way you frame the sales process. The most useful frame is approaching sales in the same way a doctor approaches working with a patient – you’re simply diagnosing if a prospect has a problem you can solve without attachment to the outcome. (SalesHacker, 2018)

The diagnosis you’re responsible for running is understanding a prospect’s pain since that pain is driving them to seek a solution. There’s a clear separation in the head of your prospect; they have a pain they want to get rid of, and they want to get to a pleasurable state without that pain. (Belfort, 1986)

For example, your solution helps people lose weight without going to the gym. Prospects that are interested in your solution may have many different pains, like hating how they look in the mirror or needing to lose weight for insurance reasons. They’ll also have different future states they want, such as feeling confident about themselves, etc. Your first priority is to clearly define both what they don’t want in their current state and what they do want in their future state. (Belfort, 1986)

Once you’ve clearly defined both states, you’ll need to ask the prospect with questions designed to create separation between these two states. A prospect will only buy when they need your help. Your diagnosis will help them admit that they’re not able to get to the future state they desire without your help. (Belfort, 1986)

An example diagnosis for the weight loss solution from earlier would encompass questions like, “How many times have you tried to lose weight in the past?”, “What do you weigh now?”, “What do you want to feel when you look in the mirror?”, and more in order to show your prospect they’re not where they want to be and admit they need your help.

The best salespeople take that gap and really emphasize it – the more pain your prospect feels about their current state, the more likely they will run toward your solution. Using that gap, start eliminating other possible alternatives your prospect could consider to find a solution. (Belfort, 1986) With the weight loss example, it would look something like,  “Yeah you lose weight with liposuction, but do you really want to expose yourself to the risk of surgery?”

Your prospect is now at a point where they’re feeling discomfort about their current state and have fewer and fewer possible ways to solve their problem. Discomfort is the fuel for change. You’re at the point in the conversation where you have the best opportunity to make your offer. Ideally, you want to position yourself as the only viable way to bridge the gap from their painful current state towards a pleasurable future state. (Belfort, 1986)

Formula = {Target Persona} can get {Desirable Benefit} without {Pain to Avoid} using {Your Solution}
