# Underlying Psychological Principles of Persuasion & Influence

In his first book called Influence, The Psychology of Persuasion, Dr. Cialdini identifies 6 core pillars that are universal principles used in the persuasion process. The **first principle is reciprocity**, and what’s crucial to remember here is that people feel obliged to give back to others in the form of a behaviour, gift, or service when they’ve received it first. (Cialdini, 1984) The next time you find yourself in a persuasion situation, you can make reciprocity work for you by **giving something of value upfront** - this way you’ll trigger the subconscious obligation response and the person you’re persuading is more likely to do the action you want them to take.

Next up is the **scarcity principle**, where people are naturally drawn to wanting more of the things that they can have less of, simply because they feel prestigious/exclusive. (Cialdini, 1984) You can invoke the scarcity response by communicating and demonstrating what is unique about your value proposition, as well as **what the prospect stands to lose** if they fail to consider your offer.

Like sheep following a shepherd, people tend to follow the lead of credible, knowledgeable experts in what is known as the **authority principle**. (Cialdini, 1984) Make authority a powerful tool in your toolbelt by signalling what makes your company a credible, knowledgeable authority (experience, etc) **BEFORE attempting to influence your prospects.**

**Consistency **is the observed principle where people like to be consistent with the things they’ve previously said or done, because of their perceived public reputation. (Cialdini, 1984) You can use this phenomenon to your advantage by gaining small, micro-commitments (small action items) with your prospects - once they’ve confirmed and completed these initial tasks, then you’ve created consistency in your relationship with the prospect and they’re more likely to be open to larger subsequent commitments.    

Furthermore, people tend to associate and say yes to those that they like, and this forms the **liking principle**. (Cialdini, 1984) By demonstrating that you’re similar to your prospect, giving them genuine compliments, and showing your cooperation towards mutual goals, you can get your prospects to like you - making it more likely that you’ll be able to persuade them.

The principle of **social proof/consensus** is the final of the six core pillars. Cialdini observed that most people look to the actions and behaviours of others to form their own, and this phenomenon only heightens in uncertain situations. (Cialdini, 1984) For your benefit, prospects will be more likely to take the action you’re persuading them to do when they can clearly witness other people like them taking the same action. (Testimonials, etc)
