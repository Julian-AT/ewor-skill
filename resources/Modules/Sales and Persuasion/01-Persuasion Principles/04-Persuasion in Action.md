# Persuasion in Action

Now that you understand the underlying psychological principles of persuasion, you’re ready to learn how to apply these principles. The followup to Influence, Dr. Cialdini’s book Pre-Suasion takes an actionable approach to influence that we’ll now examine here.

Cialdini implores that the initial conditions and context for persuasion set the tone for how successful your persuasion attempt is. The desired initial condition is your prospects seeing you as a credible source - when they see you as an authority initially, they will be more likely to invest their attention in you with minimal scepticism. (Cialdini, 2016) This can be achieved by promoting educational marketing content to your prospects while positioning yourself as an industry authority and demonstrating third-party recognition of that authority. 

Once you’ve established this positioning, your next step is to capture and direct the attention of your prospects. Humans have a limited attention span (that seems to be shrinking year over year) and get distracted quite easily. Cialdini observed that successful persuasion happens when prospects are fed personalized and relevant stimuli - like a prospect-centric marketing message - that focus on a single, clear point without other distractions. (Cialdini, 2016) You can achieve this by understanding the wants, needs, fears, desires, and challenges of your prospect and shaping the way you communicate based on a single one of these parameters.

Upon capturing and guiding the attention of the prospect, your next responsibility is to communicate what you’re offering and why it matters to them. Simplicity is key here, as different prospects have different levels of comprehension ability. Cialdini notes that the more time a prospect has to spend trying to comprehend what you’re saying to them, the less likely they will take the desired action. His rule of thumb is to keep your communication near a 3rd-grade level, regardless of how complex your solution is - this is because a prospect will instantly understand what you’re trying to communicate without delay. The best example of this (regardless of your personal opinion) is how Donald Trump communicates - he purposefully speaks at a 3rd-grade level so everyone can understand what he’s saying. In short - keep it simple and easy to understand while focusing on how you help the prospect and why it matters to them. (Cialdini, 2016)

Standing in the way between the offer you communicated and the prospect following your persuaded course of action (buying your solution, etc) is risk, fear, and inertia of the status quo state. Prospects are burdened by many perceived risks to taking action, like losing money or looking like a fool - you can deal with most risks by using what’s known as a risk reversal (30-day money-back guarantee, etc). 

Fears form the basis of all purchasing objections. By far the easiest way to eliminate your prospects’ fear is by addressing their concerns/common objections BEFORE they’re even asked - further cementing your authority and credibility since you’ve beaten them to the punchline.

Overcoming the inertia of your prospect staying the same requires a different tact - you can do it by leveraging the scarcity principle to help them see the dire consequences of inaction for themselves. This concept leverages the limbic system with a basic pain/pleasure dichotomy - you’re essentially making your prospects run away from the pain of their current state towards the pleasure of your offered future state.

Once you’ve overcome a prospect’s internal barriers/resistance to persuasion, then you’re ready to make the true influence attempt. Your call to action (CTA) is most effective when it’s clear, direct, and understandable to your prospect. The best CTAs also reiterate the benefit for a prospect without the underlying risk they presumed. (Eg - Get {Benefit} without {RIsk} with {Your Offer})
