# Breaking Down the Problem

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/9nQwPggPScc)

When researching the problem you have identified, it’s crucial to think in terms of needs rather than wants. A need is an underlying desire that is solution-agnostic, while a want is a specific solution or feature that someone finds desirable based on their subjective experience. 

For example, a store clerk might say they need a ladder to reach boxes on a high shelf. In reality, the ladder is a want—a specific solution they think will work. The underlying need, however, is simply to access the boxes. By focusing on the need, alternative solutions, like reorganising shelves or installing an automatic lift, become possible.

## A Need Is Solution-agnostic

Focusing on needs rather than wants is crucial because needs endure longer than specific solutions. Take floppy disks, for example. They once met the need to store digital information, but while floppy disks have become obsolete, the need for digital storage remains. Companies that continued addressing this need, such as Dropbox, have grown into industry leaders.

### Needs and the Evolution of Technology

Technological advancements often reshape and expand needs by changing how people behave. Take the way we listen to music, for example:

- In the 1800s, people needed to attend a concert, invite musicians into their homes, or play music themselves to enjoy it.
- The gramophone, invented by Thomas Edison in 1877, revolutionised this need, and by 1948, Columbia Records’ long-playing records made it more affordable and convenient to listen to music at home.
- Over time, this sparked new needs: more control over song selection (met by CD players in 1982), portability (met by devices like the Discman and MP3 players), and eventually access to an entire library of music anytime, anywhere (met by services like Spotify and Apple Music).

![The evolution of music players.](/api/media/file/M2-L1T3-MusicPlayersHistory.png)

As technology evolves, new needs emerge, and existing ones are often extended or refined. For instance, Spotify doesn’t just meet the need for music access but also caters to deeper needs like personalisation and convenience. However, earlier needs, such as portability or control, may fade into the background once mainstream solutions exist, making them easy to overlook.

## Jobs-to-be-Done: A Framework for Identifying Needs

Understanding needs can be challenging, but the Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) theory offers a helpful way to distinguish needs from wants. Developed by Tony Ulwick and popularised by Clayton Christensen in his book *The Innovator’s Solution*, the JTBD framework shifts the focus from products to the underlying “jobs” customers are trying to accomplish.

A simple explanation of this concept comes from Harvard professor Theodore Levitt, who famously said, 

> People don’t want a quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole.

In other words, people buy products or services as a means to an end. For example:

- You buy a chair because you need somewhere to sit for dinner or to make your flat look stylish.
- You visit a hairdresser to improve your appearance.
- You pay for a gym membership to stay healthy and get in shape.

Thinking in terms of jobs to be done helps uncover the underlying needs that drive these decisions. By identifying the job a customer is hiring your product or service to do, you can better understand their motivations and design solutions that truly address their needs.
