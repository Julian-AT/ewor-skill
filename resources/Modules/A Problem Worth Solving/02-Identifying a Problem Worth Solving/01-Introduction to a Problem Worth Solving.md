## The Definition of a Problem Worth Solving

A successful company thrives by identifying a valuable market opportunity and creating a solution that customers are willing to pay for. In many cases, these opportunities arise from pain points that customers actively experience and are motivated to address—what we at EWOR call “problems worth solving.” We define problems worth solving as those that meet the following three key criteria:

- **A true problem…**

This is something that people genuinely experience—not just claim to—and find frustrating enough to seek out solutions. In fact, some may have devised clever hacks to work around the problem or might even be willing to pay you for a solution early on, making them your *extreme users*.

- **… of high magnitude…**

The problem is costing people experiencing it lots of time, money and/or emotional pain. On top of that, it affects a large group of people. 

If the problem is not deeply felt, it will be difficult to convince people to spend their hard-earned money on your solution. If too few people experience this problem, the market likely won’t be big enough to attract VC funding.

- **… that is unavoidable**

This is a problem that people can’t avoid within the budget they’re willing to spend. If the problem can be addressed with a simple hack or by hiring another company to do the job well enough, it won’t be unavoidable, and winning customers will be tough. However, existing solutions might be too costly or overly complicated for some people facing the problem. If that group is large enough and has sufficient spending power, the problem can still be worth solving.

### Problems vs. Opportunities

Not all great businesses start by solving an obvious problem. Some of the biggest products and services didn’t address a direct pain point but instead created new demand by introducing something people didn’t even realise they wanted. Companies have also succeeded by shaping behaviours or creating the perception of a problem that didn’t exist before.

Take the beauty and wellness industries as an example. Hairdressers solve an explicit problem—people need haircuts. However, high-end salons and beauty brands thrive not just because of necessity but because of desire, trends, and social pressure. In the same way, many startups succeed not by solving a clear problem but by tapping into opportunities driven by trends, technology, or new consumer behaviours.

Opportunities can emerge from more than just solving problems—they can stem from:

- A desire for something new
- Trends or peer pressure that create demand
- Changing behaviours due to new technologies
- Technological advancements that open up new possibilities
- Shifts in values that drive new consumer priorities
- Regulatory changes that require new solutions
- Efficiency and optimisation that create better ways of doing things

While many of these could be framed as implied problems, the key question remains: Is this an opportunity worth pursuing? Can it support a scalable, profitable business?

Throughout this module, we will primarily focus on problems worth solving, as they are often the most reliable foundation for building strong businesses. However, never ignore an opportunity that arises outside of an explicit problem—if it has the potential to create value and gain traction, it may still be worth pursuing.

## Founder-Problem Fit

While working through this module, we highly encourage you to reflect on a crucial question: Is this the right problem *for you* to solve? Even if the problem is worth solving, it might not be the best fit for you. This is where founder-problem fit comes in.

Founder-problem fit is the alignment between your personal experiences, passions, and skills with the problem you're tackling. Here's why it's so important:

- **Deeply Motivated:** When you're personally invested in the problem, you're more likely to push through challenges and stay committed. Startup life is tough, and this passion is essential for the long haul.
- **Unique Understanding:** Often, founders with strong problem-fit have experienced the issue firsthand. This gives you invaluable insights into the problem and your potential customers' needs.
- **Increased Credibility:** Investors and customers alike are more likely to believe in your solution when they see your genuine commitment to and understanding of their problem.

Ultimately, a strong founder-problem fit increases your chances of success. It ensures that founders are deeply motivated, possess relevant knowledge, and can effectively navigate the challenges of building a new venture.
