# Extreme Users

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/RYZYbcV4edI)

For a problem to be a true problem, there must be extreme users—people who are either incredibly enthusiastic about about a product or service and push the boundaries of its capabilities, or struggle getting value from the product or service. Extreme users (either the heavy users or the non-users, depending on what problem you are researching) not only experience the problem you’re researching but also know they have the problem and are actively seeking a new and better solutions.

![Extreme users either have a low engagement with existing solutions (left tail of curve) or a high engagement (right tail of curve).](/api/media/file/M2-L1T5-AdoptionBellCurve%20(1).png)

The existence of extreme users is crucial to validating whether the problem you’re researching is a true problem. Extreme users of existing solutions will often become early adopters of new solutions and therefore your pilot customers, champions within their organisations, and a valuable source of feedback. Without extreme users, it’s unlikely the problem is worth solving, and your chances of finding a product-market fit are slim.

Take Airbnb as an example. The founders, Brian Chesky, Nathan Blecharczyk, and Joe Gebbia, were researching the problem of finding affordable accommodation during conferences. Extreme users in this case were people posting in conference discussion groups asking to share a room or reaching out to friends in the area for a spare bedroom or couch. These individuals were not just experiencing the problem—they were proactively trying to solve it, making them prime candidates for Airbnb’s early user base.
