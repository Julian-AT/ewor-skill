# The Importance of Validating the Problem

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/1Y7HMnUCB6g)

At EWOR, we believe that for the vast majority of entrepreneurs, validating the problem they have identified is the place to start.

## Customer Needs Are Not Within Your Control

A promising startup offers a technologically feasible product, delivered through a viable business model, that is desired by customers. Within this framework, you have a degree of control over technical feasibility and the business model. However, customer desirability lies outside your direct control, making it the most significant risk to your business. Therefore, mitigating this risk should be your top priority.

![To build a promising startup, you need desirability, feasibility and viability. Desirability is not within your control.](/api/media/file/M2-L1T1-VennDiagram.png)

Nearly 90% of the pitch decks we review emphasize the solution's technical feasibility and business model. However, most fail to address the critical question: is the problem they're solving real, of high magnitude, and unavoidable? If a founding team doesn’t prioritise the problem or convince us of its magnitude, we won't invest. Without a problem worth solving at its core, a startup is unlikely to achieve product-market fit.

> Start by researching the area posing the highest risk to your business.

## Identifying a Problem First Is (Usually) Cheaper and Faster Than Starting With the Solution

Building a startup means being smart about how you use your time and money. Learning quickly and validating your hypotheses early on, and continuously, without spending too much time or money, can make a big difference in your startup's journey.

The Truth Curve by Giff Constable visualises this principle. On the left-hand side, conversations are depicted, and on the right-hand side, a live product. The believability of the information gathered increases when moving towards a live product. From anecdotal to facts. 

![Although the believability of information gathered from a conversation might be low, the effort required is also low (adapted from Giff Constable).](/api/media/file/M2-L1T2-TruthCurve_TruthAndEase.png)

We’ve added the level of effort to this curve. As you can see, having a conversation is low effort, while building a live product is far more challenging—time-consuming and often costly. Conversations are therefore a great place to start as your investment is minimal, but you will gather valuable insights. To help you determine whether the problem you’re exploring is a problem worth solving, this module discusses several research methods.

> Learn as fast as you can, so spend as little time, energy and money as possible on verifying or debunking an assumption - but still do it!
