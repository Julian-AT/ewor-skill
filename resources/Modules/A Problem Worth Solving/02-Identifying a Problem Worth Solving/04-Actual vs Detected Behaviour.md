# Actual vs Detected Behaviour

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/ur3I3QNL7pc)

For a problem to be a problem worth solving, it needs to be a true problem. A true problem is one that people genuinely experience, not just claim to. This might seem like a trivial distinction, but it is crucial. People often behave differently when observed, or provide socially acceptable answers instead of honest ones. This can mislead you into thinking they have a problem when, in reality, they don’t.

To illustrate this concept, we've created the Actual vs. Detected Behaviour Framework. This framework uses two axes:

- **Horizontal Axis (Actual Behaviour):** This represents the true behaviour of an individual, as they would act when they believe they are unobserved. Think of it as their natural, unguarded behaviour.
- **Vertical Axis (Detected Behaviour):** This represents the behaviour an individual exhibits when they are aware of being observed or when they are describing their actions to someone else. This behaviour might be influenced by their awareness of being watched or their desire to present themselves in a certain way.

The framework helps us understand the potential discrepancies between what people do naturally and what they report or display when observed

![Actual versus Detected Behaviour Framework: For a problem to be a true problem, actual behaviour and detected behaviour need to align.](/api/media/file/M2-L1T4-ActualvsDetectedBehaviorFramework.png)

The top-right and bottom-left quadrants indicate alignment between what people say they do and what they actually do. Whether they perform a behaviour or not, their reported and observed actions match. The real challenge lies in the other two quadrants, where detected and actual behaviour differ. In the bottom right, individuals do something but don't report it, potentially due to embarrassment, lack of awareness, or issues with the research method. In the top-left, they claim a behaviour they don't actually exhibit. Pinpointing whether an observation belongs in one of these mismatched quadrants is difficult. The best solution is to use multiple research methods in conjunction, a topic we'll explore further in this module.
