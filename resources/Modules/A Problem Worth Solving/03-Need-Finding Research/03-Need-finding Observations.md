# Need-finding Observations

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/N_vOuV1xNgQ)

Earlier we discussed that there are three main need-finding research methods: interviews, observations, and immersing yourself in your customer’s situation. This topic is about need-finding observations.

During observations, you observe the behaviour of people experiencing the problem you are researching in the context in which this behaviour naturally occurs. It is a great tool for identifying hacks and workarounds and helps you get a better sense of whether what you heard during interviews aligns with actual behaviour.

## Need-finding Observations Activities

Observations can help you gain lots of insights into your problem. There are several observation activities you can use to uncover these needs, the underlying motivation or the context in which the need occurs.

> Observations help uncover needs, the underlying motivations and the context in which the need occurs.

### Fly on the Wall

In this activity, you stay in one place for a longer time - we recommend anything upwards of 15 minutes. Many of us normally rush through life and never stay still. Once you take the time to slow down and observe, you will see the world differently.

Alex and his team once did a project for a network provider at a time when there was no network reception in the underground yet. As an observation activity, he and his team played fly on the wall near the exits of subway stations. They saw lots of people frantically looking at their phones when emerging from the subway station. Alex still believes this observation has contributed to the decision to ensure network connection in the subway - ask him about it. 

### Shadow Someone

Rather than staying in the same place for a while and observing lots of people passing through, in this activity, you follow one person for a longer period of time. This is especially valuable when you need to understand a journey of steps a user might take. Note that if you are caught observing someone too closely, it can become awkward very soon. 

### Guided Tour

A guided tour is a less awkward solution to shadowing someone. Ask someone, for instance, someone you are already interviewing, to show you how they normally use a certain product or deal with the problem. Look for artefacts in their location that might refer to the problem you are researching. Make sure to ask questions during the observation.

### Narration

This activity helps to uncover the mismatch between what people think they do and what they actually do. Ask your research subject to perform a task and narrate what they are doing. This means people have to articulate behaviour that has become a habit. For the first time in a long time, people have to think about how they complete the task and this often results in them wondering about their own behaviour. This observation activity frequently leads to rich innovation opportunities. 

### Providing Head Cameras (or Meta Glasses)

Another activity that can give very interesting insights is providing head cameras to your subjects and asking them to do what they normally do. This method is especially helpful when you are researching locations or processes that you, as an outsider, are not allowed to be near or part of. Or, if you are, for instance, in a different country than your subject. Also, in cases where speed is important, and a live guided tour might not be an option, asking the interviewee to videotape it for you and later watching the recording together can be a great solution.

There are many more activities you can do to learn from observations. Be creative and find ways to observe the problem and the users you are researching!

## What to Look for in Observations

Just like when interviewing, it is essential to collect as much information and as many artefacts as you can that resemble the situation as closely as possible. Don’t try to summarise the information, but collect the exact phrasing, details and emotions.

A powerful tool you can use is the AEIOU Observation Framework, which stands for Activities, Environments, Interactions, Objects & Users.

The tool provides a structured approach to observing and categorising qualitative data, facilitates analysis and identification of key insights, and helps to understand user behaviour and context.

### Hacks & Workarounds

Hacks and workarounds can be a great inspiration for solutions to the problem you are researching and can be turned into products that can be monetised. *No one owns a workaround* – they are not patented. 

![Workaround: Home-made mixing battery](/api/media/file/lipton.jpg)

Above is an example of a workaround. Someone created their own mixing battery to regulate water temperature as their sink has two separate faucets, one for hot water and one for cold water.

Here’s another example, uncovered during a guided tour as part of a project Alex worked on for a Swiss pharmaceutical company. The company had hired Alex and his team to help regain market share for a drug used to treat Rheumatoid Arthritis—an autoimmune disease that causes joint swelling and stiffness. During the tour, Alex asked a woman suffering from the disease to demonstrate how she used the product. He handed her the medication, which came in a small cardboard box containing a glass bottle with a plastic cap. She took it into the kitchen and used a bread cutter to remove the cap. The cap, similar to those on soda bottles, required significant force to open the first time, revealing a critical usability issue for patients with limited joint strength.

![One-hundred percent of customers could not use the product without inventing a workaround.](/api/media/file/breadcutter%20pill.png)

This observation led the team to ask all future interviewees how they opened the bottle. It turned out that every single user struggled with it and had to find a workaround. Some relied on family members for help, while others asked their pharmacist to open it when collecting their prescriptions. Discovering that 100% of the drug’s target audience couldn’t use it independently prompted the company to redesign the packaging, ultimately increasing its market share.

Workarounds can be a valuable source of inspiration, but they also come with a caveat. If all your subjects have a working workaround that solves the problem to their satisfaction, they are unlikely to pay for a new solution. A common example is the double-knot used to prevent shoelaces from untying. If you were to develop shoelaces that never come untied, it’s unlikely people would buy them, as the double-knot is a simple, free, and effective solution.

What you want to look for are workarounds that solve the problem but create additional pain points. These might take more time, cost extra money, are simply inconvenient, result in dangerous situations, or affect someone’s dignity. For example, in the case of Rheumatoid Arthritis medication, the woman using a bread cutter to remove the bottle cap was at risk of breaking the glass bottle, creating a dangerous situation. Meanwhile, most other users had to ask someone else for help opening the bottle, which could be humbling and frustrating over time. These added frustrations make the problem worth solving.

### Misalignment Between Stated and Observed Behavior

Actual behaviour is often more nuanced than people describe. Therefore, combining observations with interviews almost always helps you to get a richer understanding of the problem.

One example stems from a project Alex did regarding grocery shopping habits. One interviewee, living on a basic income in Germany, explained that providing food for her family was challenging with limited funds. She emphasised that ‘cheap’ was more important than ‘fresh.’

Alex went grocery shopping with her and noticed that she often crouched down and reached to the back of the shelves. When he asked why, she explained that the cheapest products were usually on the bottom shelf, while the freshest items with the longest remaining shelf life were placed at the back. Her actions revealed that both ‘cheap’ and ‘fresh’ were important to her—but only in the right balance and context.

![Needs are more nuanced than people realise; both cheap and fresh matter.](/api/media/file/cheap.png)

Here’s another example from a project Alex worked on for a financial institution in China. During an interview with a young woman in a café, she confidently stated that she was on top of her finances and always knew how much money she had. Wanting to dig deeper, Alex asked her to show her wallet.

From her wallet, 26 different payment cards emerged. While she had a story for each card, it quickly became clear that she wasn’t as organised as she thought. This realisation surprised her as much as it did Alex. She wasn’t lying about her financial awareness—she simply hadn’t recognised the chaos in her wallet until it was laid out before her.

## The Hawthorn Effect

When doing observations, be aware of the Hawthorne Effect: people altering their behavior when they know they're being observed. The term stems from a series of studies conducted at the Hawthorne Works during the 1920s and 1930s. Researchers initially aimed to study the impact of workplace conditions, like lighting, on productivity. However, they found that productivity often increased simply because the workers were being observed, regardless of the specific changes made.

Especially when you shadow someone or are a fly on the wall, make sure your subject is not aware of the fact that you are observing them. For the guided tour, the narration and the head cameras, note that your subject might have altered their behavior slightly because they know you are observing them.
