# Need-finding Immersions

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/w6djWg8PdAg)

The third research method for Need-Finding Research is self-immersion. Immersion is the ultimate way to emphasise. You experience the problem firsthand. Immersions are a great way to both physically and emotionally experience the difficulties a potential user or customer might have.

Many innovative ideas stem from inspiration entrepreneurs had due to pain points they experience themselves regularly. Immersion allows you to have experiences that you normally would not have, and thereby open up a whole new range of pain points that can inspire you.

Especially when your potential customer or user is different from you, we highly recommend finding a way to do a self-immersion. For instance, if your potential customer or user is a different age group or is differently abled than you. There are lots of attributes you can use to immerse yourself in someone else's situation. For instance, wearing an age suit, a fake pregnant belly, or glasses to obscure your vision. Spend a day in a wheelchair if you want to see how your building, public transport, or work environment is hindering people who can’t walk.

> Especially when your potential customer or user is different from you, we highly recommend finding a way to do a self-immersion.

Self-immersion does not necessarily require body-morphing attributes. It can also be about activities and experiences you don’t normally have. For instance, Alex and his team were once asked to do a workshop for a medical company that made medical devices. The goal of the workshop was to understand what happens during emergencies in a public area. The team was thinking about what to do in this workshop and decided to take the group down to the local train station, where one of them would then pretend to have a heart attack.

This experience was way more insightful than what could have been learned from need-finding interviews, as most people have never experienced an emergency in a public place. Therefore, most people cannot tell you about concrete events in the past. Add people who are really bad at predicting their own actions in an emergency to that, and you see why need-finding interviews were not going to be helpful.

Observing the reaction of the public in this staged emergency was a truly immersive learning experience that taught us so much more than just speculating about what could happen from the comfort of a workshop room.

However, this also came at an immense cost. The entire chain of emergency response was triggered, and emergency services showed up at the train station. Alex and his team had to cover the cost for that.

So, be mindful of how far you want to take your self-immersion experiments.
