# Finding Research Subjects

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/mZ1czWGT_8I)

We have seen how to conduct need-finding research and whom to talk to or observe. Let’s have a look at how to find research subjects.

- **Activate your network. **Your network is often a great source for initial interviews, especially in the early stages of your research when you are not trying to sell anything yet. Ask around if anyone has a relation to the subject you are researching, or if anyone knows anyone that does.
- **Cold outreach. **Cold outreach often has a (very) low response rate, but it can lead to some good interviews. And the good thing is, the interviewee might be able to introduce you to other people you can talk to.
- **Engage with potential customers and users where they are. **To find extreme users, try to think of behaviours an extreme user would display to try to solve the problem you are researching. In the Airbnb example shared earlier, extreme users posted on the conference forum. So, go onto the forum and look for these people and message them.
- **Let them come to you. **Post in dedicated Facebook groups or on the advertisement board in your local supermarket or coffee shop. Ask people who are facing a certain problem or who meet certain criteria to contact you. Those who reach out are often extreme users. In addition, you can organise meetups, workshops or conferences - preferably in person to encourage casual conversations. Your role as organiser positions you as an expert, resulting in interest from attendees in talking with you.
- **Warm introductions by other research subjects. **The absolute best way to get interview contacts is through warm introductions from the people you interviewed. At the end of an interview ask the interviewee “Who else should I talk to?”. They can often introduce you to other extreme users or extreme users who are experiencing the problem as well. Another important reason to ask this question is that you want to interview all stakeholders involved in a certain problem or decision. Especially when researching B2B, this is important. Your interviewees can make warm introductions to these other stakeholders.
