# Need-finding Interviews

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/mMNeXGJrqTE)

Interviews are one of the three qualitative research methods used in need-finding. Interviews can be a very powerful tool to uncover needs and test your hypotheses, but if performed incorrectly, interviews can quickly result in false positives — where actual and detected behaviour do not align. This is worse than not doing need-finding at all, as false positive feedback will give you a false sense of security.

## The Rulebook of Need-finding Interviews

To help you avoid this pitfall, we’ll outline some rules and guidelines. Many of these are inspired by Rob Fitzpatrick, a successful entrepreneur and author of *The Mom Test*. This book serves as a practical guide to the common advice, “talk to your customers,” often found in startup literature and taught at EWOR. We’ve also supplemented Fitzpatrick’s rules with best practices from our own startup experiences.

1. **Talk about their life instead of your idea. **Talking about your idea focuses the conversation on what you think the problem is or what you think would be a great solution, reducing the chance of learning something new. Second, it puts your ego on the line, resulting in interviewees providing socially acceptable answers. They might agree they have the problem you mentioned, say they like your idea, or make non-committal statements like claiming they’d become a customer if you solved the problem—all of which focus on a hypothetical future rather than concrete insights. Instead, focus on the interviewed person. Ask them about their life and especially about their relation to the problem. How they experience it. How they articulate it.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past instead of generics or opinions. **Opinions, hypotheses, and general statements made by your interviewees are worthless. Fitzpatrick calls these types of answers and statements ‘fluff’. When people talk in generics, they talk about how they wish their life was, not how it actually is. You need facts. To get to the facts, ask about specifics in the past. Avoid asking questions such as “Would you like to buy a product which did X?” or “How much would you pay for X?” as they inquire about a hypothetical future.
3. **Dig deeper. **Ask plenty of follow-up questions during your interviews. Keep digging until you truly understand what the interviewees were feeling, what actions they took, and why. You might be familiar with the *Five Whys* method, pioneered at Toyota to uncover the root cause of problems. This technique can be incredibly valuable in customer interviews as well!
4. **Meet your interviewees where they are. **Your research will be far more effective if you meet interviewees in their natural environment—ideally, where the problem you’re researching occurs. This approach not only helps the interviewee feel more comfortable, but also gives you the opportunity to witness the problem firsthand or even see an impromptu demonstration. 
5. **Keep it casual. **Formality puts people on their guard. They are more careful about what they say and will also expect that you are trying to sell them something. If possible, it is best to not even let people know that you are interviewing them, but just have a casual chat. If it is a scheduled interview, you can start by saying “I’m not here to sell you anything, I am just trying to learn more about how you do X”.
6. **Collect quotes and artefacts. **When interviewing, don’t try to summarise what your interviewee says, but collect the exact phrasing, details and emotions of their statements. The original words from your interviews are to be kept alive as long as possible, as they carry the specifics of each person. Note down your interpretation of the emotional state of your interviewee, for instance by drawing emojis next to their quotes. In addition, take photos (after getting permission of course) and take away artefacts, such as things your interviewee showed you (often times if they have an old or broken version, they will give it to you).

> Minimise the amount of fluff by asking about specifics.

## What to Ask in Need-Finding Interviews

The goal of need-finding research is to find out if the problem is a true problem, how deeply people feel the problem and why they can’t avoid it. Below are examples of questions for each of your main goals.

![Examples of questions to ask in need-finding research to determine whether the problem you are researching is a problem worth solving](/api/media/file/M2-L2T2-ATrueProblemInterviewQuestions.png)

**Q: What are the implications of that?**

The question, “What are the implications of that?” distinguishes between a true problem that people would pay money to solve and problems that are just a bit annoying. Sometimes people get all riled up talking about a problem, but when you ask about the implications, it turns out that it costs them an hour a month. This would not constitute as a problem of high magnitude. On the other hand, responses like, “We have an entire department with over ten thousand employees worldwide dedicated to handling this issue,” clearly demonstrate high magnitude.

**Q: How are you dealing with it now?**

“How are you dealing with it now?” is another example of a good question, as it asks for specifics. In addition, it can give you an idea about potential competitors and the price they are currently paying to solve the problem. If they already have a decent solution that costs very little, the problem isn’t unavoidable. However, responses like, “I have three different subscriptions to piece together the solution I need, but the synchronisation between them is horrible,” would indicate an unavoidable problem.

**Q: If you were me, researching this topic, what else would you have asked?**

A final content-related question we recommend asking is, “If you were me, researching this topic, what else would you have asked?”. This allows the interviewee to share insights you didn’t know to ask about. For example, they might share that another department is involved in decision-making or mention a Facebook community dedicated to sharing workarounds for a specific problem.

## Enhance Interview with Visual Tools

It can also be very valuable to ask your interviewees to make what they are telling you visual. There are three commonly-used visual tools we recommend:

**The Mind Map**

Ask the interviewee to visualize connections between key concepts. This will reveal their understanding of relationships between these concepts.

**User Journey Map**

Ask the interviewee to draw the process they describe step by step.

![Example of a journey map for visiting the gym. ](/api/media/file/M2-L2T7-UserJourneyMap%20(B4%20size)%20(1).png)

**Process Map**

In a B2B setting, it could be interesting to ask interviewees to draw or complete a process map. When you ask multiple stakeholders to draw the process, you could be surprised by the differences you encounter. It is also a great way to see which other stakeholders are involved in which parts of the process. 

**Emotional Journey Map**

Another powerful tool is to ask your interviewee to draw an emotional journey map, mapping emotional fluctuation over time (e.g. during a process). Time is on the horizontal axis, while the emotional state of the interviewer is on the vertical axis. This is a great tool to understand where people are confused or unhappy with a certain process and what issues your solution should solve.
