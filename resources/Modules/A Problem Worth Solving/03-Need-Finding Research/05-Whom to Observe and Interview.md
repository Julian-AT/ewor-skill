# Whom to Observe and Interview

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/mKOVM2UaLi0)

When deciding whom to interview or observe for your need-finding research, there are a few tips to keep in mind:

## Extreme Users

Earlier, we mentioned that the existence of extreme users is a prerequisite for a problem to be worth solving. Extreme users are experts on the status quo and can tell you exactly what they love or hate about current solutions. They are the non-users or heavy users of certain products. 

People who have difficulty using products as intended due to physical or other impediments are also extreme users. For example, people with mobility issues, such as the people with Rheumatoid Arthritis discussed earlier, people who are pregnant, or people who suffer from obesity. Other impediments that can make using certain products difficult are learning difficulties such as dyslexia.

![Extreme users either don’t use current solutions at all or are heavy users of current solutions ](/api/media/file/M2-L2T5-StudyTheExtremes.png)

Nintendo, for instance, learned valuable lessons when it interviewed people who hated playing video games when it was developing its Wii console. The extreme users said they found most games too complicated and the controllers too difficult to use. If you remember the Nintendo Wii, you probably know that Nintendo took this feedback to heart. The games were quite simple and the controllers mimicked real-life motions. The Wii became an instant hit, revolutionising video games and forcing competitors to play catch-up with their own systems.

> The Nintendo Wii became a hit because they took feedback from people who hated playing video games to heart.

Extreme users on both ends of the spectrum are often a source of inspiration, way more than the users in the middle. Who your extreme users are, depends on the problem you are researching. So, you will have to be a bit creative in defining what ‘extreme user’ means to you. 

Note that only a small percentage of the overall population qualifies as an extreme user for the problem you are researching. During your need-finding research, you will therefore likely encounter way more mainstream users. Mainstream users might not have the problem, might not be aware of the problem or might not be actively looking for a better or different solution. Don’t let this discourage you. If you have found people who have the problem, know they have the problem and are searching for a solution, there is a high chance that your problem is worth solving. 

## Stakeholders

Stakeholders are those who will be impacted, directly or indirectly, by the solution you develop. For instance, if you are researching online tutoring for primary school students with dyslexia, the stakeholders include the students themselves, but also their parents, their school and maybe even their existing reading coach. In a B2B setting, stakeholders include anyone who might have a say about the purchasing and implementation of a potential solution, such as the procurement department, the IT department, and the legal department.

In your need-finding research, make sure to interview people from all stakeholder groups to avoid missing obvious reasons that might hinder adoption, and to ensure you have a complete picture of the problem.

The most commonly made mistake when interviewing or observing people is to interview or observe people who are demographically very similar to each other or to you. Falling in this trap will make it difficult to translate your findings to a wider population and it might turn out that only a small group of people experiences this problem. Remember that for the problem to be worth solving, a lot of people need to experience it. Therefore, it is important to talk to everyone who might experience this problem.
