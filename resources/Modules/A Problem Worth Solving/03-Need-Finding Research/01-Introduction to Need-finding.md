# Introduction to Need-finding

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/QqSu44Ms_EE)

In earlier topics, we defined ‘a problem worth solving.’ We highlighted that problems should be framed in terms of needs rather than wants and discussed how conversations can be a valuable starting point for validating a problem. However, we also explored the risk of misalignment between actual behaviour and detected behaviour.

To reduce the likelihood of this misalignment, we recommend a research method called ‘need-finding.’ This approach is designed to uncover unmet or latent needs—problems or challenges that existing solutions, products, or processes don't adequately address.

The term need-finding was first introduced by Robert McKim in the 1970s when he was working as head of Stanford University’s product design program. He developed this qualitative research method when exploring the idea of designing to fill a missing need rather than trying to give customers what they want. 

Since then, need-finding has become a widely used method among design and product teams around the world. It shares many similarities with the first stage of the Design Thinking process—*Empathy*. For those familiar with research methods, need-finding is akin to Naturalistic Research, where you don’t start with a clear hypothesis or predefined data goals. Instead, you observe real-world behaviour without subjecting participants to controlled lab conditions, learning directly from their actions and experiences.

Need-finding is a great place to start when you want to build a new product or service. A need is an opportunity waiting to be exploited. It is not a guess about the future but something people are already experiencing as a need. A need spurs action and makes people search for solutions.

## Need-finding Methods

Need-finding uses three main types of qualitative research methods to uncover needs: interviews, observations and immersions. 

- **Interviews** are exactly what you think they are – a question-answer conversation with someone who experiences the problem you are researching. The goal is to discover the emotions that guide behaviours and understand if there is a need that is not currently being met.
- In **observations**, you observe the behaviour of someone experiencing the problem you are researching in context. Observations allow you to identify discrepancies between what people say they do and what they actually do. They provide valuable insights into how people currently handle a problem and reveal any workarounds they’ve developed. However, observations are less effective at uncovering what’s happening inside someone’s mind. For this reason, they are often paired with interviews to gain a more complete understanding.
- **Immersions** involve stepping into the shoes of the person experiencing the problem, making them the ultimate way to build empathy. By immersing yourself, you can physically and emotionally experience the challenges a potential user or customer might face in context with the problem.

At EWOR, we recommend using different methods during your need-finding research, depending on the information you are trying to gather.

**Johannis Hatt on Continuous Product Development**
In [this video](https://youtu.be/Y9cq3xA5Cws), serial entrepreneur and angel investor, Johannis Hatt talks about the importance of involving your customers in product development and best practices for discovering with them.

**Why Lean Startup Changes Everything by Steve Blank**
A [Harvard Business Review article](https://hbr.org/2013/05/why-the-lean-start-up-changes-everything) written by Steve Blank. A quick and easy read compared to his book.

**The Four Steps to the Epiphany By Steve Blank**
Steve Bank’s book started the Lean startup approach, in which startups learn as much as they can by constantly experimenting. In the book, he talks about the four-step Customer Development process, of which Customer Discovery is the first step.

**The Entrepreneur’s Guide to Customer Development by Brant Cooper and Patrick Vlaskovits**
Two students from Steve Blank translated his book ‘The Four Steps to the Epiphany’ into a more practical and easy-to-read guide. 

**The Lean Startup by Eric Ries**
The famous book by Eric Reis lays out the steps to the Lean startup method. The idea is to learn what your customers want by continuously testing, adapting and adjusting your business. This book is more focused on learning through prototyping than through conversations, but the idea of rapid learning and innovation accounting can be very helpful to familiarise yourself with early in your journey.
