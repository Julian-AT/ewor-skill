# Calculating the Total Available Market

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/02hXkcAiUzo)

TAM stands for the Total Available Market. The TAM describes the total global revenue potential if you were to solve the problem you are researching for everyone who experiences it. There are two often-used methods for estimating the Total Available Market: a bottom-up approach and a top-down approach.

## Bottom-up Approach

The bottom-up approach starts with detailed, specific data about a potential customer and their spending and scales that up to estimate the total market. To get a realistic estimate, segmenting (potential) customers might be required.

A bottom-up approach is often more accurate than a top-down approach as it is more granular than a top-down approach. The assumptions are on a per-customer basis and therefore easier to sanity check.  Follow the next steps to determine the market size or opportunity:

#### Step 1: Define your (Potential) Customer Segment(s)

Start by defining the customer segment or segments that make up your market. Your need-finding research should have helped you determine a segment(s) of the market or population experiencing the problem. Don’t overcomplicate this and only distinguish between segments if the behaviour and/or spending for each segment differs significantly. 

#### Step 2: Determine the average spend per customer in each segment

The next step is to determine the average revenue per person or company experiencing the problem during a certain period (usually a year) for each segment. 

During your need-finding research, you might have heard how much money people are currently paying to get their problem solved, either to an incumbent or for a workaround. This should give you an upper bound of what your revenue per customer would be, as you would likely have to charge less than their current solution to attract them as customers. An example is the total spend on labour costs to manually enter all data into a system. As companies would want to be better off when buying your product or service, you would probably be able to charge somewhere between 30 and 60% of their current costs for your solution, meaning your average revenue per customer would be 30 to 60% of their current spend.

In some cases, potential customers are currently not spending anything to get the problem solved. In those cases, you make an assumption regarding their potential future spending:

##### No solution is available:

- Are there similar people or companies who do have access to a solution, for instance in a different country? 

Their spend on the solution can be the starting point for your assumption. You might need to adjust the per-customer spend based on the price points in your country.

- Would your solution increase profit or reduce costs for the customer?

Make assumptions about their willingness to pay for the solution based on the expected results. Take as your average revenue per customer a portion of the additional profit or reduced costs you will help them achieve. 

For example, if you help reduce the amount of abandoned online shopping carts for online retailers, you could assume you can charge retailers a certain percentage of the extra profit they make through your product.

##### Available solutions are too expensive:

- Can you make an estimate of the price point at which these current non-consumers would purchase a solution? Calculate a market size using this assumption. Note that this assumption becomes one of the most important assumptions to validate when prototyping.

#### Step 3: Estimate the number of (potential) customers for each segment

Determining how many people or companies belong to each of your defined segments. Market reports or national statistics databases can be a good starting point for a reliable number of people or companies who fall in that segment.

#### Step 4: Sum the Segment Revenues

For each (potential) customer segment you identified, multiply the number of potential customers by the expected annual spend per customer. Sum up the total spend for all customer segments to get to the overall market size or market potential.

### Example

To illustrate the bottom-up approach, let’s calculate the market size for electric toothbrush heads in the US.

1. The target customer is people with electric toothbrushes living in the US. We don’t see a compelling reason to segment this group based on buying behaviour or spending habits.
2. Our need-finding research revealed that, on average, electric toothbrush owners replace their brush heads twice a year. Store visits show that a set of two electric toothbrush heads typically costs $20 (excluding VAT). We’re exploring the idea of introducing a subscription model for electric toothbrush heads to encourage more frequent replacements. Assuming our pricing remains at $10 per head, the goal would be to increase replacement frequency to three times per year.
3. We recognise that we didn’t speak to very young kids or those with dentures. Therefore, we assume about 70% of the 320 million people in the US fall in the range of people who could use an electric toothbrush. From our need-finding interviews, we also learned that roughly 1 in 2 people use an electric toothbrush. Therefore, we estimate there are approximately 112 million potential customers (70% × 320 million × 50%).
4. Multiplying 112 million potential customers by an annual spend of $20 per person gives a current market size of $2.2 billion. If the annual spending per customer increases to $30, the market potential would rise to $3.3 billion.

## Top-down Approach

The top-down approach uses existing market research reports from companies like Nielsen, Forrester, Statista and Gartner to determine the market size. It offers a broader, more macro-level view of a market and is especially useful in established markets. 

**Note:** These reports are often expensive and often do not contain the number you are looking for, as they use different categorisations and segmentations of the market than you would. We recommend using a top-down approach as a sanity check for your bottom-up calculations.
