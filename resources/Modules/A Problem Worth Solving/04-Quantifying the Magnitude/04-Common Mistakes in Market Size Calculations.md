# Common Mistakes in Market Size Calculations

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/7qdbkB6diTA)

Although market sizing sounds relatively simple, there are a few common mistakes we see entrepreneurs make.

### Your Customer’s Revenue Is Not Your Market Size

The Total Addressable Market should represent the amount of money your (potential) customers spend or would be willing to spend to get their problem solved. For instance, if the problem you are researching is inventory management in independently owned restaurants and stores, the market size is their expenditure on that problem - not the revenue they make.

AirBnB’s market size for instance is the total number of overnight stays multiplied by a reasonable fee AirBnB can extract from mediating between the homeowner and the visitor. The total amount spent on overnight stays would grossly overestimate the market size.

### Installed Base vs Newly Installed

Depending on the problem you are researching (and the solution you might have in mind) your market size might be mostly dependent on the installed base or the growth and replacement of the installed base. For instance, if you develop a revolutionary new smartphone, your market size is the price times the newly bought smartphones per year. That number would represent both people replacing their current phone and people buying a smartphone for the first time. Using the total amount of smartphones currently in use would grossly overestimate your market size.

However, if you were to develop a new software tool for smartphones, the number of smartphones currently in rotation is relevant for your market size.

### Lower Prices Can Shrink or Grow Your Market Size

If you are researching a problem for which solutions already exist, you will likely offer your solution at a lower price point than the incumbents. On the one hand, this might reduce your market size. Keep in mind that your TAM is dependent on the price your customers would be paying for your solution, so if you charge a tenth of the price compared to incumbents, your market will be 10 times smaller.

On the other hand, lower prices might persuade current nonconsumers to purchase your solution, thereby increasing the market size. SpaceX’s market size for instance arguably is not the current annual budget of all space agencies combined. If SpaceX succeeds in significantly reducing the costs of space exploration and satellite launches, a whole new generation of companies will make use of that. In other words, SpaceX’s lower prices could result in market expansion.
