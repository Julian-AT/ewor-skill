# Generating Opportunity Areas

Once you and your team have mapped your passions and strengths, the next step is to turn that self-knowledge into a pool of possible directions. The most powerful source of ideas is still you. Start by listing your passions and skills in concrete terms. For passions, ask: what topic areas energise you? What have you always been drawn to, whether as a lifelong hobby or a curiosity you’ve wanted to master? For skills, be thorough. Don’t just note technical abilities like coding or data analysis, but also softer skills like inspiring others, listening, or empathy.

## Shift Your Focus

With your new lists in front of you, shift your perspective from enthusiasm to problems. Reverse-engineer your passions.

- When has your excitement been driven by something that didn’t work or felt frustrating?
- Think your skills forward: in which fields could they make a meaningful change, or where are they in high demand?

This exercise helps surface opportunity areas that aren’t random—they’re rooted in what you already care about and know how to do.

Once you’ve captured your personal and team-based lists, expand outward. Explore external sources like startup idea requests and topic lists from accelerators such as EWOR, review trend reports from McKinsey, Forbes, or the World Economic Forum, and scan open calls from investors. These sources expose you to larger shifts in technology, markets, and society that might intersect with your skills and passions.

Keep in mind, though, that many teams will be looking at the same lists. The differentiator is how you interpret them through your unique strengths and values. Your goal here isn’t to choose one idea yet, but to generate a longlist of possible opportunities. This longlist will be the raw material you later filter and prioritise, setting the stage for identifying problems that are both exciting and viable.

## The Decision Matrix

Once you’ve created a longlist of potential opportunity areas, the next step is to narrow it down. The most important filter is the foundation you’ve already built: your team’s passions and skills. When an opportunity aligns with both what excites you and what you’re good at, it deserves closer attention.

Beyond that, several additional criteria can help you compare opportunities more objectively. These follow the principle of **effectuation**—focusing on the factors that are within your control. Use these criteria to score and compare your opportunities, and reduce your list to roughly four strong candidates.

**Network Access**
Start by mapping the people in your team’s network. Do you know a C-level executive at a major car manufacturer? Is someone related to a media personality? Does anyone have a close connection to a professor who’s a leading expert in AI? These kinds of relationships can open doors and accelerate progress in ways that passion and skills alone cannot.

**Access to Problem Research**
 Connections to an industry matter just as much as enthusiasm for it. For example, we once worked with a team eager to innovate in anime video production. They loved anime but didn’t know anyone in the industry. Since 95% of anime is produced in Japan and the team was based in Switzerland, they had no access to insiders, making it nearly impossible to validate their ideas. Passion alone is never enough. You need access.

**Access to Market**
Similarly, you need a realistic path to reach your market. Imagine a team based in Denmark targeting affordable healthcare in the U.S.—a massive need, but practically out of reach without strong local presence. Opportunity without access is unlikely to succeed.

**Investment Case**
Most ideas require funding, and if founders can’t finance them themselves, they turn to investors. But not every idea is investable. Early AI consultancies, for instance, often needed a team to secure projects and projects to support a team — a circular problem that investors couldn’t justify funding. Case in point, strong ideas sometimes fail simply because the economics don’t work.

**Investor Interest**
Investor appetite is shaped by trends. Hardware has long been avoided, biotech often scares off generalists, and consumer startups were unpopular for years until their recent revival. SaaS dominated, and today, AI is the must-have buzzword. If your opportunity falls into an “in” category, fundraising will be easier. If not, you’ll need to spend much more time finding the rare investors who are open to your space.

**Problem Size**
Qualitative stories and anecdotes can spark interest, but decisions require numbers. Ask: how much are people currently paying to address this problem? How much would they save if it disappeared? What would they be willing to pay for a better solution? This isn’t the same as market size, but it’s a crucial measure of whether the problem is substantial enough to pursue.

**Market Size**
 Large markets are attractive because they offer room to grow—and because investors want the potential for billion-dollar companies. If your idea is to supply tapioca pearls for bubble tea, you may find the global market capped at $2 billion, with dozens of entrenched players. The chance of winning half the market is slim, and without scale, many investors won’t even take a meeting.

**Competition**
Competitors aren’t just threats—they’re signals. Study how users are currently served. Where are they underserved or mis-served? Where are solutions too expensive or too complex? Competitors reveal opportunities for differentiation, whether by simplifying, improving, or delivering in a new way.

**Business Dependency**
Consider how much control you’ll have over your product’s destiny. If you’re developing a plugin for a major ERP system, you may depend entirely on their APIs and a handful of big corporate customers. By contrast, a complementary tool that can stand alone gives you more independence and direct access to a larger base of potential users.

**Impact as a Startup**
Finally, think about how much leverage a startup realistically has in your chosen field. Some areas, like defence or government tech, have historically been nearly impossible for startups to break into due to regulations and legacy systems—though this is changing slowly. Still, certain sectors remain tough environments for new entrants.

### Build Your Matrix

Not every criterion will matter equally to every team. Revisit your Team Canvas and decide which factors are most relevant to your strengths, interests, and constraints.

Use these to build a simple decision matrix: list your chosen criteria in the rows, your opportunity areas in the columns, and score each cell based on how well the opportunity matches the criterion. The matrix won’t give you a single “right” answer, but it will help you compare options more objectively and make clearer, better-informed decisions.

Once the matrix is complete, narrow your longlist to a shortlist of no more than four ideas. Step back as a team and ask: *Do these still feel right for us?* If not, revisit the process. If yes, you now have a focused set of opportunities to explore in more depth.
