# Testing With Sprints

Once you’ve narrowed your longlist into a focused shortlist, it’s time to put those ideas under pressure. The best way to do that is through short, intensive research sprints. Think of a sprint as giving each idea a few days—at most a week—of your full attention. The goal isn’t to “prove” an idea right or wrong, but to collect enough insights to make an informed decision about whether it’s worth pursuing further.

## Get Ready to Sprint

A sprint works because it blends speed with variety. Don’t rely only on statistics or market reports—that will give you a partial view. Instead, combine different approaches: read everything you can, ask questions to large language models, scan forums and trend reports, talk to people in your network, and whenever possible, immerse yourself in the real context. The mix of qualitative, quantitative, and experiential data is what sharpens your judgment.

To structure your sprint, start with the idea that secretly excites you most and list questions:

- What do you already know about the space?
- What don’t you know, and what would change your decision if you had the answer?

Prioritise these questions and divide them across your team. Meet frequently to share findings and adjust as you go. If the idea still stands strong after a sprint, you might already have your answer. If not, move on to the next one. Often, just going through the process reveals which direction you and your team are genuinely drawn to.

### Test for Potential

Alongside your research, add two creative elements to test potential:

- **Brainstorm solutions** at the start and end of the sprint. Compare the two rounds. Has your thinking deepened? Does the team’s excitement grow with new insights?
- **Build mini pretotype landing pages** to simulate interest. A simple page describing the problem (or one potential solution) can be shared with a small audience, giving you real-world signals about resonance.

### Use the Past-Present-Potential Framework

A useful formula to remember is: **Past – Present – Potential.** Collect the past (experiences and evidence), reflect on the present (current gaps and behaviours), and then sense the potential (where change and opportunity lie). If you trust this process, the right idea will emerge.

### Compare and Decide

At the end of the sprints, create a simple comparison matrix summarising what you learned. Clear frontrunners often emerge quickly—supported by both logic and genuine team motivation. That mix of evidence and energy is what you’re aiming for.

## From Exploring Opportunities to Identifying Problems Worth Solving

Your sprints will leave you with 1–2 opportunity areas that feel promising, energising, and grounded in real signals from the world. But even the strongest opportunity space is not yet a startup. The next step—the one where most founders stumble—is determining whether it contains a problem truly worth solving. 

In the next lesson, you will shift from exploring opportunity spaces to validating the underlying problem itself. This is where direction becomes focus, and an opportunity becomes the foundation for a venture.
