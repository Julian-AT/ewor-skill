# Problem Statements: Point of View

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/b0FQ6oNVFls)

After clustering your data, you've gained valuable insights into your potential customers. Now it's time to synthesise these insights into a concise and actionable problem statement. A problem statement is a concise description of an issue to be addressed or a condition to be improved upon.

## Point of View

In human-centred design, a problem statement is often articulated as a point of view. A point of view frames the problem in a way that guides your ideation process. It focuses on the user, their needs, and the underlying motivations driving those needs.

A strong POV consists of three core elements, expressed in a single sentence:

[**User description**] needs [**need**] because [**unique insights that explain why their need is compelling**].

Notice how these statements use adjectives to describe where the current solutions failed. They are “complex” and make users feel “inadequate.” This Point of View is actionable because it clearly defines what your solution needs to accomplish. It should be simple and instil a sense of confidence in its users.

### Guidelines for a Strong Point of View

A strong POV consists of the following:

- **Reflects your data:** Accurately represents your research findings.
- **Focuses on user value:** Articulates what matters most to the user.
- **Remains solution-agnostic:** Avoids mentioning specific technologies or features.
- **Guides innovation:** Provides a clear direction for ideation.
- **Informs evaluation:** Sets criteria for assessing potential solutions.
- **Inspires action:** Motivates your team to explore solutions.

A truly effective POV goes beyond surface-level problems and delves into the user's implicit needs. It sparks a sense of possibility and fuels your team's eagerness to ideate solutions. When you reach that “aha!” moment and can't wait to start brainstorming, you know you've crafted a great POV.

### Notes on a Concrete Point of View

A strong point of view (POV) focuses on a problem that's specific enough to guide innovation, but broad enough to allow for creative solutions, not just minor tweaks. Dev Patnaik's hierarchy of needs can help you find that sweet spot:

- **Common Needs:** These are basic human needs, like the need for food or shelter. They are universal and not tied to any specific context. However, they are often difficult for a single company to address.
- **Context Needs:** These needs arise from a person's specific circumstances, such as their age, profession, or location. For example, a student might have a context need for affordable textbooks, while a doctor might have a context need for reliable medical information.
- **Activity Needs:** These needs are related to specific activities that people engage in. For example, a runner might have an activity need for comfortable running shoes, while a musician might have an activity need for a high-quality instrument. 1  
- **Qualifier Needs:** These are the most specific and immediate needs that people experience. They arise from the particular situation that someone is in at a given moment. For example, a person who is caught in the rain might have a qualifier need for an umbrella.

![Dev Patnaik’s hierarchy of needs differentiates between common needs, context needs, activity needs and qualifier needs .](/api/media/file/M2-L5T4-HierarchyOfNeedsDevPatnaik.png)

A strong problem statement addresses context or activity needs because they are specific enough to be addressed by a single company, but not so niche that they only affect a small group of people.

Focusing on qualifier needs often leads to incremental improvements rather than groundbreaking innovations. By understanding the different levels of needs, businesses can develop products and services that truly meet the needs of their customers.
