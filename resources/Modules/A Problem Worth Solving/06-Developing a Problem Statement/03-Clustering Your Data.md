# Clustering Your Data

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/-IzCR4aivg8)

With your data visualised, move on to step two: clustering. This process assists you in identifying patterns and themes by linking the dots, thereby revealing deeper insights.

Experiment with different frameworks or diagrams to explore relationships within your data. Take a photo of your initial clustering to capture it, then rearrange the data and try again. Each iteration offers a fresh perspective, potentially uncovering surprising connections.

At this stage, resist the temptation to filter out information. Keep all your data points visible, even if some don’t fit neatly into a specific cluster.

To help you out, we have gathered some of our favourite methods for clustering your data below. Although these are just starting points, feel free to experiment and develop your own approaches as you work.

### Empathy Map

An empathy map helps you identify powerful emotions. If your solution can tap these emotions, you are more likely to develop a successful product or service.

![An empathy map clusters data over 6 buckets.](/api/media/file/M2-EmpathyMap.png)

### Process Map

To identify the most impactful areas for improvement, visualise the customer journey as a process map. Start by writing each step of the journey across the top of your whiteboard, then add corresponding data points beneath each step. This approach helps you pinpoint where customers experience the strongest emotions, both positive and negative.

For example, while working with a train service provider facing increased competition, Alex and his team initially focused on improving the restaurant carriage, as the client believed this was key to retaining market share. However, Alex recommended mapping the entire customer journey to uncover unmet needs. This broader approach revealed issues beyond the onboard experience, leading to more effective improvements.

![The process map regarding the customer journey for train trips Alex and his team drew;.](/api/media/file/M2-L5T3-Process%20Map.png)

By interviewing passengers, the team gathered feedback on each stage of the journey and plotted pain points on the process map. They then quantified the frequency of negative feedback in a bar chart, revealing that passengers were most frustrated with the *waiting* and *continuing their journey* stages. Problems such as confusing signage, unclear directions, and long wait times were far more significant than the onboard experience, highlighting key areas for improvement.

![When travelling by train, people really dislike the waiting part (positive feedback is displayed in orange, negative feedback in grey)](/api/media/file/M2-L5T3-Dislike%2BLike.png)

In short, presenting this process map and accompanying bar chart to the client reframed the challenge. It clearly demonstrated that a new restaurant carriage would likely have minimal impact on overall customer satisfaction. Instead, it shifted the focus to improving the station experience, wayfinding, and communication, ultimately leading to more impactful solutions.

### Stakeholder Map

When tackling problems where the decision-maker and the user differ (a common scenario in design research), create a stakeholder map. Simply list each stakeholder role on your whiteboard and add their corresponding data points below. This visual approach helps identify and differentiate the needs of each stakeholder, revealing potential conflicts and opportunities.

![A stakeholder map differentiates between the different stakeholders](/api/media/file/M2-L5T3-StakeholderMap.png)

### Two-by-Two

To further analyse differing statements from interviewees, consider using a two-by-two matrix. Start by grouping similar statements, then identify two axes that distinguish these clusters.

For example, while researching car buyer needs for a manufacturer, Alex, and his team uncovered distinct perspectives. One group saw cars as purely functional tools, prioritising comfort for long solo drives. Another prioritised safety and practicality for transporting family and belongings. A third group viewed cars as "adult toys," valuing performance and status.

Plotting these perspectives on a two-by-two matrix—for instance, using axes like *functional vs. emotional* and *individual vs. social*—helped map the manufacturer’s existing offerings and revealed gaps in their product line. This approach can be a powerful tool for organising insights and identifying opportunities.

![Creating your own two-by-two can be an insightful way to cluster your data](/api/media/file/M2-L5T3-Create2x2.png)

### The Onion

When researching consumer products, consider using the “onion framework” to cluster your data. This method goes beyond surface-level attributes like taste, exploring the rituals, routines, and values tied to product use.

For example, Oreo’s iconic “twist, lick, dunk” ritual demonstrates how consumer behaviour can add a deeper dimension to the product experience. By uncovering these layers, the onion framework offers valuable insights into the cultural and emotional connections people form with products.

![Going beyond the obvious aspect of a product can teach you a lot](/api/media/file/M2-L5T3-Onion.png)

### Composite Character

A composite character is a semi-fictional representation of your user group, embodying key observations and insights. To create one, organise your data into three categories:

1. **Demographics:** Age, education, occupation, and other descriptive information.
2. **Needs:** Clearly defined needs and pain points.
3. **Motivations:** Underlying values and desires that explain *why* these needs are important.

When working with diverse stakeholders, you'll create multiple composite characters. However, even seemingly homogenous user groups can reveal distinct subgroups, each requiring its own composite character.

## A Final Note on Clustering

These are just a few examples of ways you can cluster your data. Use them as a starting point, but don’t hesitate to experiment and develop your own methods.

Clustering is a highly creative and iterative step in the process. It’s not something you can schedule for an hour and expect to finish. Give yourself time to reflect and revisit your data. Walk away from your whiteboard, and let your mind process the information. You might come back the next day with a completely new idea for clustering that leads to fresh insights.

Remember to photograph each clustering arrangement before moving on. This ensures you can revisit and explore different perspectives without having to recreate the clusters from scratch.
