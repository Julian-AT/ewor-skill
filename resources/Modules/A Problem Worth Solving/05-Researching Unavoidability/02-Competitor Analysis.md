# Competitor Analysis

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/zp977FqiJBw)

It is highly unlikely you are the first person to identify the problem you are researching. Other companies probably offer products or services to solve (part of) the problem you identified for a certain subset of people experiencing the problem. To get a clear picture of whether the problem is unavoidable and for whom, we recommend complementing your need-finding research with competitor analysis.

Competitor Analysis has several goals:

- **Understanding the strengths and weaknesses of current solutions**
Understanding the strengths and weaknesses of other solutions helps to determine where the opportunity lies. What parts of the need are current solutions not adequately addressing? What features did your interviewees say they loved? Which features did they not like? Who has an amazing offering but is not known by anyone - in other words, who is doing a terrible job marketing their solution? How can your product or service (or business model!) solve this for potential customers or users?
- **Understand who has access to current solutions and who doesn’t **
Clayton M. Christensen’s Theory of Disruptive Innovation says that incumbents' continuous sustaining innovations eventually result in them overshooting the performance needs of people experiencing the problem. Many customers are therefore overserviced and consider the solution expensive. In addition, there might be a large group of people experiencing the problem who are not paying incumbents for the solutions (non-consumers) as the offered solution is too expensive for them. In these markets, creating a solution with fewer bells and whistles at a lower price point can result in quickly winning over many non-consumers and winning customers from incumbents.
