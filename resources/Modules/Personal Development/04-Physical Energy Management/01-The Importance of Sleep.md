# The Importance of Sleep

Sleep is your body’s primary source of energy. It fuels both mental clarity and physical recovery, yet it’s one of the most neglected aspects of a founder’s life. To optimise it, it’s vital to understand how your circadian rhythm, hormones, and sleep cycles work—rather than simply memorising rules.

## The Circadian Rhythm

Your circadian rhythm is your body’s internal 24-hour clock that regulates when you feel alert, focused, or tired. It coordinates hormone production, body temperature, and metabolism, helping you stay aligned with natural day–night cycles. When this rhythm runs smoothly, your energy peaks in the morning, your focus stabilises throughout the day, and your body winds down naturally at night.

Your circadian rhythm follows a daily rise and fall that shapes your alertness, focus, and physical performance. Most people reach peak mental clarity about three to four hours after waking and peak physical strength between 3 and 5 p.m., though individual patterns vary. Aligning work, exercise, and rest with your personal rhythm enhances productivity, sleep quality, and overall energy balance.

## How We Sleep

Governed by your circadian rhythm, sleep begins when sleep-promoting hormones are released. During this time, your brain transitions through distinct stages marked by changing wave frequencies:

- **Delta waves** (slow waves): deep, restorative sleep, and
- **Beta waves** (fast waves): alert wakefulness, what we emit when we are actively thinking.

These shifts occur in roughly 90-minute cycles—early ones dominated by deep sleep and later ones by Rapid Eye Movement (REM) sleep, when the brain consolidates memories and restores mental energy.

Since REM sleep mainly occurs in the latter part of the night, sleeping only 4–5 hours covers physical recovery but cuts off most REM phases. This lack of mental restoration over time leads to fatigue, burnout, and reduced emotional stability.

### Sleep Cycles and Timing

Each person’s sleep cycle length varies slightly, usually around 90 minutes. Waking up during light sleep or REM makes you feel refreshed, while waking in deep sleep leaves you groggy. Experiment with your sleep duration in 90-minute increments (e.g., 6h, 7.5h, 9h) and use trackers like an [Oura Ring](https://ouraring.com/?srsltid=AfmBOoreQRaNsYoTrTs_KfyXwk2WTCettGicHiG4itj8vOx0PXWdHMUf) or [Whoop](https://www.whoop.com/us/en/?srsltid=AfmBOophoBK_RGlLIIGFcWCwWmZITVkgWFym6TfzyLTyEM7gK6H_aRW3) to identify your natural rhythm.

### Sleep Duration and Health

Sleep needs are individual, but chronic deprivation leads to serious health consequences—higher risks of depression, weakened immunity, and cognitive decline. Too much sleep, on the other hand, is also linked with poorer health outcomes. The key is balance: wake naturally, feel alert, and sustain stable energy throughout the day.

## Is Your Energy Cycle Balanced?

Your energy cycle is governed by two major hormones: **melatonin**, which helps you fall and stay asleep, and **cortisol**, which wakes you up and keeps you alert.

These hormones operate in opposite rhythms—melatonin peaks at night, cortisol peaks in the morning. Disruptions (like late-night screen use or irregular sleep times) throw off this balance and cause fatigue, poor focus, and inconsistent hunger or mood. If you’re unsure if your cycle is balanced or not, ask yourself:

- Do you struggle to fall asleep or wake up in the morning?
- Do you feel an energy crash in the afternoon?
- Do you notice bursts of productivity late at night?
- Is your sleep often restless or irregular?
- Are your digestion and hunger patterns inconsistent?

Answering yes to any of the above may be an indicator that your energy cycle is out of sync. 

### Resetting Your Rhythm

If you find yourself needing to reset your circadian rhythm, there are a few things habits you can adopt to accomplish this:

#### 1. Build Consistency

Rebuilding your natural energy rhythm begins with consistency. Wake up, go to bed, eat, and exercise at the same times every day — with wake time being the most crucial. A predictable schedule strengthens your circadian rhythm, improving both energy and sleep quality.

#### 2. Start the Day Right

Begin your morning with sunlight exposure and a glass of water. Light suppresses melatonin and triggers cortisol release, signalling your body to wake up. Hydration enhances alertness and cognitive performance.

#### 3. Wind Down Intentionally

In the evenings, focus on relaxation. Your final hour before bed should be free of screens, stress, and bright lights. A warm shower, dim lighting, and calm activities lower cortisol and help melatonin rise naturally. Avoid caffeine within ten hours of bedtime, skip carbs or large meals three hours before sleep, and schedule workouts between 3–5 p.m., when your physical performance peaks.

#### 4. Use Light Strategically

Light is your body’s primary timekeeper. Bright morning light boosts cortisol and alertness, while evening darkness triggers melatonin for rest. Limit blue light at night by dimming screens and using warm lighting or tools like  [f.lux](https://justgetflux.com/) or night shift mode to maintain a healthy sleep rhythm.

#### 5. Support Your System

Boost your energy and recovery with Vitamin D3 — ideally through daily sunlight or a morning supplement. It enhances mood, alertness, and immune function. Pair this with temperature control: warm up before bed with tea or a shower, and keep your room cool at night for deeper, more restorative sleep.

#### 6. Track Your Sleep Cycle

Find your natural rhythm by logging your bedtime, wake time, and energy levels. Most people operate in 90-minute cycles. Aim for 6–8 hours of total sleep — enough to complete multiple cycles for both mental and physical recovery. Prioritise full nights for cognitive performance and earlier bedtimes for physical restoration.
