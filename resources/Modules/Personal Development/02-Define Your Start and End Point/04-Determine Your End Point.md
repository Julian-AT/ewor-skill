# Determine Your End Point

As an entrepreneur, your time is your most valuable asset. Allocating it without a clear destination is like the conversation between Alice and the Cheshire Cat in “Alice's Adventures in Wonderland”:

**Alice:** “Would you tell me, please, which way I ought to go from here?”

**Cat:** “That depends a good deal on where you want to get to.”

**Alice:** “I don't much care where.”

**Cat: **“Then it doesn't much matter which way you go.”

To avoid wandering aimlessly, you must define your endpoint. However, the way you set goals changes dramatically depending on one key milestone: Product-Market Fit (PMF). Goal setting can be divided into two distinct phases: before and after achieving PMF.

## Phase 1: Explorative Goals (Before PMF)

Before reaching product–market fit, your goal isn’t to scale—it’s to learn. This stage is about exploration, not optimisation, so strict sales targets can backfire. Your focus should be on managing the exploration–exploitation tradeoff:

Imagine you’re in a casino with multiple slot machines—each representing a customer segment, feature, or business model—and only a handful of coins (your resources). Instead of betting everything on one machine, test several just enough to discover which yields the best return. Here’s how:

**1. Define Your Long-Term Vision:** Set one, massive stretch goal that feels almost impossibly ambitious. This is your North Star. Don't create detailed multi-year plans, as your perspective—your “horizon”—will constantly shift as you learn.

**2. Set Short-Term Learning Goals:** Your short-term goals should be framed as hypotheses you need to test to find PMF. Focus on answering the core questions:

- **Desirability:** Will customers pay for this? Do they retain?
- **Viability:** Are the unit economics promising? Is the market big enough?
- **Feasibility:** Will my technology work and scale?

**3. Maintain Flexibility:** Do not over plan. The biggest opportunities often present themselves spontaneously, and you must be agile enough to recognise and act on them.

## Phase 2: Exploitative Goals (After PMF)

Once you have validated your core hypotheses and achieved PMF, your focus shifts to exploitation: scaling your success efficiently while still exploring new opportunities. The best framework for this phase is [Objectives and Key Results](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1RBWo_Iv4Keo7LBdsxEuSakiaY_HOpTyCg8VoNXaqBgo/template/preview) (OKRs), which brilliantly combines long-term ambition with short-term, measurable execution. For this phase:

**1. Set Your Stretch Goal (1-10 Years):** This is your audacious, visionary goal—a “dream with a deadline”. It should be inspiring and slightly uncomfortable. This goal will serve as the foundation from which your quarterly objectives are derived.

**2. Break It Down into Quarterly OKRs:** For each quarter, set 3-5 ambitious Objectives supported by 3-5 measurable Key Results.

- **Objectives (The “What”):** These are qualitative, inspirational, and audacious goals for the quarter. They should be ambitious enough that they feel challenging to achieve.

*Example:* “Enhance our market position to become the preferred choice for our target customer.”
- **Key Results (The “How”):** These are the **SMART** (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) metrics that prove you have met your objective. They are quantifiable and leave no room for ambiguity.

*Example KR 1:* Achieve 25% growth in market share by the end of Q4.
*Example KR 2:* Launch two new product features based on key customer feedback.
*Example KR 3:* Increase brand website traffic by 40% through a targeted digital marketing campaign.

By setting goals this way, you create a clear path from your daily actions to your grandest ambitions, ensuring every effort is moving you in the right direction.

## Aligning Your Goals

Even strong goals can fall apart if they compete with one another. Alignment is what transforms multiple priorities into a unified drive forward.

Research consistently shows that clarity and coherence in goal-setting lead to higher achievement. Ambiguous or conflicting goals, by contrast, divide your focus and reduce motivation. Studies by Pfeifer and Locke et al. found that when people pursue opposing objectives, performance declines due to fatigue, confusion, and strained communication.

To prevent that:

- Build a hierarchy of goals—a pyramid where daily actions feed into weekly, quarterly, and long-term aims.
- Revisit your goals regularly to ensure they still reinforce one another rather than compete.
- Ask yourself whether your goals are truly yours. Externally imposed goals—by a boss, parent, or social expectation—tend to erode motivation over time.

When in doubt, keep asking *why*. The deeper you go, the more you’ll uncover whether your goals stem from genuine purpose or borrowed beliefs. Goals that align with your values naturally generate the energy to sustain them.

## Actions and Habits: Turn Vision into Action

Clarity without action is just theory. Once your long-term endpoint is clear, translate it into behaviour through consistent review and habit formation.

Start with your stretch goal—the long-term vision that feels slightly uncomfortable but deeply motivating. Then break it into quarterly OKRs that are both ambitious and measurable.

From there, cultivate habits that sustain progress:

1. **Set stretch goals.** Create stretch goals if you have a stable environment that support experimentation and tolerates risk. Otherwise, aim for small wins.
2. **Use SMART goals** for short-term execution—Specific, Measurable, Assignable, Realistic, and Time-bound.
3. **Encourage autonomy.** Set the goals yourself and let your employees set their own goals.
4. **Ensure harmony.** Check regularly that all your goals complement rather than contradict each other.

When your OKRs align, your actions sync, and your habits reinforce your vision, progress feels less like a grind and more like momentum. Each quarter becomes another confident step toward your ultimate endpoint.
