# Adjust and Pursue Your Goals

## The Ikigai Approach

Setting goals is easy—sustaining them takes alignment. The Japanese concept of **Ikigai**, meaning “reason for being,” helps you refine and adjust your goals as you grow.

![M3-L4T3-Ikigai](/api/media/file/M3-L4T3-Ikigai)

Your Ikigai sits at the intersection of:

- What you love
- What you’re good at
- What the world needs
- What you can be paid for

When these four overlap, you find purpose that lasts. If one is missing, performance usually slips—passion fades without skill, impact stalls without profit, and execution fails without meaning. Successful entrepreneurs tend to operate near their Ikigai: they enjoy what they do, excel at it, meet real needs, and build viable models around it. Those who don’t often burn out, give up early, or struggle to sustain momentum.

Finding your Ikigai isn’t a one-time event—it’s ongoing recalibration. As your interests, abilities, and environment evolve, your goals should evolve too.

To find your Ikigai, ask yourself regularly:

- Do I love what I’m pursuing?
- Am I improving at it?
- Does it create real value?
- Can it sustain me financially?

Look for what overlaps across these four categories for yourself. Then reflect on your current startup journey—how could it align more closely with your Ikigai? Small, continuous adjustments to your products, mission, or role can bring your daily work closer to that sweet spot.

## Assess and Reflect

Regular reflection and balanced feedback help you stay aligned, grow faster, and adjust your goals with clarity. Start by taking stock of where you are—your strengths, weaknesses, skills, and resources. If possible, ask a professional to conduct a 360° review with input from colleagues, mentors, and family. If not, seek honest feedback from a trusted friend and use those insights to refine your goals.

Make reflection a consistent practice. Each week, take time to review your progress, identify what’s working, and adjust what isn’t. Invite feedback openly but selectively, paying attention to recurring themes rather than one-off opinions. Use others’ perspectives to reveal blind spots, but stay grounded in your own judgment. The goal is not to please everyone—it’s to grow with awareness and direction.
