# Compensation

Compensating your team effectively is key to attracting and retaining top talent. There are three main components to consider: base pay (a stable salary), incentive pay (bonuses tied to performance), and equity-based compensation (a stake in the company). Striking the right balance between these elements is crucial to align employee motivation with your company’s goals. Let’s discuss.

## Base Pay

When determining base pay, decide whether to offer salaries above or below the market average based on your industry and business model. For high-stakes industries, such as cutting-edge technology where each project carries significant costs, paying above-market rates for top talent can be justified, as the impact of skilled individuals outweighs the expense.

Conversely, at startups like EWOR, where performance is measured by the ratio of expenses to investments, every bit counts. We need to manage salaries carefully to ensure costs align with the capital available for investment. 

Paying above market would mean hiring fewer people, which could reduce overall productivity and not serve the company’s best interests. As a result, we pay slightly below market rate but make up for it with a strong vision, attractive opportunities, and a highly engaging work environment.

In short, assess how much of your costs come from staff salaries. If they’re a major expense, consider paying below market rate; if not, you can afford to pay above. Next, evaluate how appealing your company is. Less exciting businesses may need to offer higher pay unless strong leadership or culture compensates. Groundbreaking ventures, on the other hand, can attract talent even at lower pay.

## Incentive Pay

Incentive pay works well in sales roles, where bonuses can be tied directly to measurable outcomes like revenue. However, for non-sales roles, it has been shown that incentive-based compensation often fails to deliver results and can even reduce productivity by undermining intrinsic motivation. Instead, focus on fostering a culture where employees are driven by purpose, leadership, and a shared vision. A fair wage combined with intrinsic motivation is often more effective than traditional “carrot and stick” incentives.

## Equity

Equity compensation is a powerful tool for aligning employee interests with the company’s long-term success. Structuring it effectively ensures fairness while motivating your team to contribute to the business’s growth.

### Setting Up the Stock Option Pool

Establish a stock option pool of 5%–20% of your company’s equity, depending on your growth stage and hiring plans. At least 5% should be reserved as a minimum, but note that many companies set aside 15% to 20%, as this range provides flexibility for both current and future hires. When determining equity allocations, Mark Jemazian, founder of Brace, suggests:

- 0.1% equity for standard team members, particularly when offering above-market salaries.
- 0.5% equity for senior or C-level roles.
- 1%–2% equity for high performers or early employees taking on significant responsibility.

These percentages may seem small early on, but as your company grows, the value of these shares can increase dramatically, making them a meaningful incentive.

### Vesting Schedules

Vesting ensures that equity is earned over time, promoting long-term commitment. The most common vesting schedule is 4 years with a 1-year cliff, meaning employees earn no equity during the first year but gain incremental ownership after that. For innovation-focused companies, a 7-year vesting period can be used to tie compensation to extended goals.

For high performers or early employees who take on significant responsibility, offering between 1% to 2% equity on a longer vesting period—typically 7 years—is recommended. 

### Future-Proofing Equity Allocations

When deciding how much equity to offer, consider reserving enough for future roles. Scaling from 0 to 100 employees requires a different skill set than growing to 1,000. Reserve enough equity for future hires to attract top talent as your company evolves. For example, a small initial allocation (e.g., 0.1% equity) may be valued at €3,000–€5,000 but could grow to over €100,000 as the business scales.

### VSOPs vs. Actual Shares

When it comes to equity, you’ll need to decide whether to use Virtual Stock Options (VSOPs) or actual shares, and this choice often depends on where your company is based. In some countries, like Germany, VSOPs are a more practical option because they defer taxes until the stock is sold. This means employees won’t owe taxes on paper gains before they actually have the money in hand, which can be a big relief for them.

On the other hand, granting actual shares is ideal in regions with more favourable tax laws, as it gives employees direct ownership right away. Whichever option you choose, it’s important to consult a legal expert to make sure your approach works well for both your company and your team while staying compliant with local regulations.
