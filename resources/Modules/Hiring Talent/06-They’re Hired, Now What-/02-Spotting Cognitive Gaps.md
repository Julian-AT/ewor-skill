# Spotting Cognitive Gaps

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/u6eWPUjIFPQ)

Once you compose your team, it may feel like everything is finally coming together. But, it is also important that once you have your team that you check for cognitive gaps. In this topic, we cover why doing so is necessary and how you can check for gaps within your team.

## Paper Tests 

To evaluate team dynamics effectively, two popular tools are the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) and Gallup StrengthsFinder. The MBTI, often available for free (e.g., 16Personalities), categorises individuals based on four key dimensions: introversion vs. extroversion, sensing vs. intuition, thinking vs. feeling, and judging vs. perceiving. These dimensions provide insights into your team’s personalities, helping you balance strengths across various roles.

![The four key dimensions defined by the Myers-Briggs Test](/api/media/file/bht-mbti-spotting-cognitive-gaps)

For example, extroverts excel in outward-facing roles like sales, while introverts thrive in analytical or detail-oriented tasks. Intuitive team members focus on the big picture, whereas sensing types prefer to start with detailed, step-by-step processes. Both approaches are critical, depending on the task. The thinking vs. feeling dimension highlights those who make logical, data-driven decisions versus those who rely on gut instincts. Interestingly, research shows gut feelings, particularly in experienced professionals, often lead to faster and more accurate decisions. Lastly, judging types favour decisive action—ideal in fast-paced environments—while perceiving types excel in exploring options and conducting research. A mix of these traits promotes balanced decision-making, combining thoughtful analysis with timely execution.

However, these personality dimensions are not fixed. As team members grow into their roles, their preferences may shift. For instance, someone initially inclined toward perceiving may develop into a judger as they take on leadership responsibilities. Therefore, these tools should be seen as a snapshot of current team dynamics rather than permanent labels. By using them this way, you can identify potential imbalances—such as a team dominated by judgers who might rush decisions or perceivers who might struggle to finalise them. The ultimate goal is to create a balance that fosters both efficient action and thoughtful consideration.

## Coaching 

Another valuable step for organisations is to bring in a coach. For any team larger than 10 people, it is highly recommended to have a coach who regularly (weekly or even monthly) works with the leadership team. This coach can observe team dynamics, facilitate group discussions, and identify issues that may be preventing individuals from reaching their full potential, such as unvoiced concerns or team members struggling to contribute effectively. 

Many founders may believe they do not need this, but nearly everyone can benefit from it. In many cases, successful leaders work with multiple coaches, with at least one focused specifically on organisational dynamics and leadership.

If you have the chance to work with a coach, it’s best to start as early as possible. Even if you feel like you don’t need one right now, once your team grows beyond 10 people, having a coach becomes really valuable. Research backs this up, and most seasoned founders will tell you the same. 

So, once you've built your team, take two important steps: use assessments like the MBTI and Gallup StrengthsFinder to identify any imbalances, and bring in a coach to help guide your leadership and team dynamics.
