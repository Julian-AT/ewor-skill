# Offboarding Employees

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/Qtab3wGOVlk)

As a founder, one of the most challenging conversations you'll face is letting people go. While it’s never an easy task, it's a crucial part of running a business. Unfortunately, two common mistakes are often made during this process. First, failing to create a clear framework around firing, which can result in a culture of fear. Second, handling the firing conversation itself poorly. In this topic, we'll explore these mistakes and how to avoid them.

## Avoiding a Fear-based Company Culture

One common mistake is failing to establish a clear culture and process around firing. Without defined rules or communication, firing someone suddenly can create fear across the organisation. That’s because when founders make quick decisions to fire an employee without prior notice or explanation, it leaves the rest of the team worried that it could happen to them at any moment. There are a few ways to over this, as detailed below:

### Communicate Openly 

It’s essential to communicate with your team about why someone is being let go and whether they were given opportunities to improve before this final decision. By clearly explaining the reasons behind the decision and the steps that led up to it, you help avoid creating a culture of fear and ensure everyone feels secure as long as they're meeting expectations. Moreover, when the team sees that feedback and second chances are part of the process, they won't constantly worry about suddenly losing their jobs and will feel more at ease.

### Follow a Three Step Process 

Should you question whether or not someone is truly a good fit for the team, there is a three-step process that can help create clarity and fairness in your offboarding decision. 

This process typically spans three months, ensuring everyone understands they’ve been given a fair chance. First, there’s an initial coaching conversation to address the issue, followed by setting clear goals for the next month. If no improvement is made, a second conversation is held to reset goals for another month, with a clear warning that failure to meet these goals could result in termination. Finally, if there's still no progress, a final deadline of two to three weeks is given before letting the employee go.

This approach also brings added transparency to the organisation, helping others understand that if they’re not part of this process, their job is not at immediate risk, which reduces overall job security concerns.

### Cultural Fit Considerations

When someone you’ve onboarded isn’t a good fit for the company culture, it may be more efficient to skip the three-step process and let them go immediately, saving time and minimizing disruption for both the employee and the team.

However, firing someone quickly for cultural reasons can leave others feeling uncertain about the decision. To avoid this, transparency is essential. Again, you should clearly communicate the reasons behind the decision, and if your culture is strong, most employees will already sense when someone isn’t a good fit. This explanation will reassure them, preventing a culture of fear where people worry about losing their jobs unexpectedly.

### Probationary Periods

Another instance of when should probably skip the three-step process is when it comes to the probation period, which is inherently a trial phase. If it becomes clear during this period that someone isn’t a good fit, letting them go is simply part of the process.

This is typically well understood, and since other employees aren’t in probation, they won’t feel at risk. The key is clear communication throughout the process, ensuring that no one feels their job is in jeopardy without reason. When people feel secure, they perform better and stay motivated, free from the fear of sudden termination.

## The Termination Rulebook

There are four key rules to follow when having a conversation about termination. 

1. **Be Firm: **First, be firm and never leave room for negotiation. Once you’ve made the decision to let someone go, ensure it’s a solid decision and communicate it clearly. We’ve seen first-time founders begin these conversations in a hesitant or unclear manner, which leaves the other person thinking there’s still room to change the outcome. This only leads to arguments and confusion, which isn't helpful and can result in ending things on bad terms.
2. **Don’t Make It Personal: **Never fire someone for personal reasons—it’s not a sound business decision. Even if there are personal feelings involved, avoid communicating this to others, as it can damage relationships and lead to bad terms, which can result in negative word-of-mouth. It's crucial to maintain emotional discipline during these conversations. If the employee becomes upset or even attacks you, resist the urge to react. Instead, acknowledge their feelings, stay calm, and emphasise that the decision is not personal but what’s best for the company.
3. **Handle These Conversations Yourself: **Always handle these conversations yourself, at least for the first 50 employees you need to let go. Delegating this responsibility early on can lead to misunderstandings or mishandling. As the founder, you are best positioned to manage these critical conversations and ensure they remain professional. It's also good practice for your own leadership development. Once you’ve gained experience, you can move toward an organisational structure where others handle terminations.
4. **Cover Your Legal Bases: **On the legal side, ensure your contracts include clauses regarding no duplication of company materials and the return of all company property upon termination. Make it clear during off-boarding that these conditions must be met, as it offers legal protection for you in case someone attempts to misuse company information later. Having these clauses in place and clearly communicated helps prevent legal grey areas and ensures a smoother exit process.
