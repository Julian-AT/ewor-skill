# Organisational Knowledge

As your start-up grows, managing the collective knowledge within your organisation becomes critical to maintaining alignment and driving innovation. Traditionally, organisations were viewed as systems with a simple input-process-output model. However, modern organisations must dynamically create and convert knowledge to solve problems and innovate effectively. For example, innovation is not just about solving predefined problems—it often involves redefining the problems themselves.

In this topic, we explore Ikujiro Nonaka’s “[Spiral of Knowledge](https://josephmahoney.web.illinois.edu/BA504_Fall%202008/Uploaded%20in%20Nov%202007/Nonaka%20(1994).pdf)” model, which highlights how organisations generate, share, and apply knowledge. Nonaka identifies key processes for converting knowledge and provides valuable insights that can help you structure your team and systems effectively. Below, we outline practical strategies inspired by Nonaka’s work to help you build a knowledge-driven organisation.

## On-the-job-training (OJT) Matters

Nonaka distinguishes between tacit and explicit knowledge:

- Explicit knowledge is easily expressed and documented.
- Tacit knowledge, however, is inexpressible and frequently drives the real value within an organisation.

To transfer tacit knowledge, new hires must spend time observing how things are done—learning through immersion. Encourage team members to work closely together, shadow one another, and pick up on subtle organisational practices. While virtual meetings and calls have their place, nothing replaces time spent learning by watching.

## Frequency Is Essential

Frequent communication and knowledge sharing are vital for improving operational efficiency. In dynamic environments, regular interactions become even more critical. Nonaka highlights the risks of operating in silos, emphasising the importance of consistent knowledge exchanges. To foster collaboration, every team member should participate in both formal and informal weekly discussions. For remote teams, it’s essential to proactively design and facilitate these interactions to ensure they happen effectively and meaningfully.

## Enforce Entrepreneurial Thinking

According to Nonaka, trial and error is essential for transforming explicit knowledge into tacit understanding. Organisations thrive by challenging and testing their theoretical knowledge to validate its practical application. As an entrepreneur, you should encourage your team to experiment with new projects and approaches—whether it’s trying out innovative sales techniques, exploring new business models, or adopting fresh meeting formats. These trials not only enhance learning but also foster a culture of adaptability and growth.

## Make Your Organisation Customer-Centric

Experience is a key driver of knowledge creation. Without frequent touchpoints with customers, employees miss out on valuable insights needed to transform explicit and implicit customer knowledge into effective products and services. Nonaka refers to this process as “Embodiment,” where mind and body converge to deepen understanding. This practice is essential for organisations seeking to align their offerings with real customer needs.

## Redundancy Matters In Practice

While repetition may seem inefficient in theory, in practice, the inherent chaos of organisational life makes redundancy essential. Nonaka highlights that redundancy helps reduce misunderstandings and builds mutual trust within teams. It reinforces what matters most and ensures that those who may have fallen behind can catch up and stay aligned with organisational priorities.

## Communication Channels Define The Knowledge Creation Processes

Every interaction within your organisation, whether formal or informal, contributes to the creation of knowledge and information. To ensure your organisation operates effectively, it’s crucial to establish systems and practices that foster knowledge creation. This includes designing clear lines of communication, ensuring accessibility to key discussions, and defining power structures and hierarchy to facilitate collaboration and decision-making.

Sandelands and Stablein (1987) liken organisational dynamics to the workings of the brain. Just as neurons influence each other based on proximity, pathways, and signal intensity, behaviours and communication in organisations are shaped by access, communication channels, power dynamics, and competing priorities. At its core, the politics of an organisation mirrors the intricate network of the human brain, highlighting the importance of a well-structured and intentional communication framework.

## A Final Note

If there’s one key takeaway from this section, it’s that organisations are a delicate balance of order and chaos. As an entrepreneur, your journey begins as an individual, where your personal output—your ideas, efforts, and decisions—drives everything. At this stage, the knowledge you possess resides primarily in your mind and in the tools, code, or documents you’ve created. However, everything changes when you transition to leading a team.

Once you’re no longer working alone, the effectiveness of your organisation is no longer solely dependent on your individual performance or the intelligence of individual team members. Factors like psychological safety, company culture, and internal structures become critical to success. This shift can be challenging for many entrepreneurs, as they must transition from being high-performing individuals to effective organisational leaders.

As your responsibilities evolve, your focus must expand to managing the emotions, behaviours, and systems of your team. The skills that once propelled you forward—such as individual productivity or independent problem-solving—will no longer suffice. Instead, your role will centre on fostering collaboration, maintaining alignment, and continuously refining your organisational structure. Embrace this transition, and regularly assess and adapt your processes to keep your team functioning at its best.
