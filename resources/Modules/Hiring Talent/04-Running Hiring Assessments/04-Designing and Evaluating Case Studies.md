# Designing and Evaluating Case Studies

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/n4_-HWYBlkU)

Once you’ve aligned on a candidate you’re excited about, a case study is one of the most effective tools to understand how they actually think and operate. But a case is only useful if it reflects real work, not a polished theoretical exercise.

## Step 1: Design a Case Around Reality, Not Theory

A good case should:

- Be tied to a real objective for the role (not hypothetical busywork).
- Reflect the actual conditions of your company – including messy data, unclear information, and incomplete context, because that’s how real work looks.
- Include constraints, especially on time — around 3–4 hours is enough. More than that becomes unfair and inefficient for both sides.

Think: *If this person were in the role from Monday to Friday, what would they actually do? Who would they interact with? What would great output look like?*
 Design the case to mirror that.

## Step 2: Let Them Use AI — But Ask for Their Thinking

Assume candidates will use AI. Instead of trying to prevent it, encourage it, but require transparency:

- Ask them to share assumptions, prompts, and points where they got stuck.
- Have them explain why they made certain decisions, not just *what* they delivered.

This lets you evaluate their judgement, not just AI output.

## Step 3: Define the Evaluation Dimensions *Before* You See the Work

Before assigning the case, decide what you actually want to assess. Dimensions might include:

- Strategic clarity
- Structure of thinking
- Creativity
- Operational detail
- Communication quality
- Cultural alignment or working style

Create a simple scoring matrix or weighting system. This reduces bias and helps you compare finalists against each other fairly.

*Only after doing this should you look at their submission.*

## Step 4: Use Dual Evaluation 

Evaluate manually first — note strengths, weaknesses, and surprises. Then feed:

- Your role definition
- Your evaluation dimensions
- The candidate’s submission

…into an LLM and ask for a structured assessment. This provides an additional, less biased perspective and can surface insights you missed.

Compare your intuition with the model’s analysis — alignment or divergence both give you useful signals.

## Step 5: Always Include a Live Working Session

A submitted case shows how someone prepares. A live case session shows how someone thinks on their feet.

Therefore, run a 60–90-minute collaborative session based on a fresh problem (ideally related to the case they already worked on, so they’re not starting cold). During this session:

- Let *them* lead — you observe how they structure, communicate, and adapt.
- Ask questions to see how they react under light pressure.
- Pay attention to your own energy: *Does working with them feel frictionless or draining?* This is a valid data point when imagining long-term collaboration.

If possible, have two people from your team present — one leads the interaction, and the other observes and takes notes.

## Step 6: Debrief Immediately

Right after the live session, quickly document impressions before your memory gets filtered:

- What worked well?
- What felt off?
- Did they surprise you?
- Could you see yourself learning from them?
Would you want to be in a room with this person every day?

Only after the brain-dump should you move into final evaluation.
