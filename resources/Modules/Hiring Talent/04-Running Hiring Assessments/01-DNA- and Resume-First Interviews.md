# DNA- and Resume-First Interviews

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/Arnusu7syto)

Running a great interview is about confidence, structure, and depth. You’re not just having a casual chat over coffee — you’re showing the candidate that this decision matters deeply. In this topic, we will cover all the essentials of running interviews for DNA- and resume-first interviews. 

### Setting the Stage

Before diving into questions, ask for permission: *“I’d like to ask a lot of direct questions today — is it okay if I keep this fast-paced and focused?”* This small step sets expectations and makes it easier to run the conversation with intensity.

## DNA-First Interviews

DNA-first interviews are about who the person is, not what they’ve done. They go deep into character, values, and motivations. A well-run DNA-first interview often lasts between one and three hours, and it follows three broad stages:

### Digging for Essence

You start by uncovering the candidate’s true character.

- *The Mom and Dad Question*: Which core attribute do you carry from each of your parents?
- *Edge Trait*: Which part of your personality, if exaggerated by 50%, would become problematic?
- *XYZ Question*: If you could work on any problem at any company, what would it be and why?
- *Pure Essence*: What’s the most important thing I should know about you that you’d never want me to find out?
- *Pride and Mistakes*: What accomplishment are you most proud of? What do you see as your biggest mistake, and what does that reveal about you?
- *Definition of Success*: How do you personally define success — happiness, impact, wealth, change?
- Career priorities: If you could ask your future self one question about your career, what would it be?
- Rebellion: What’s one thing you fundamentally disagreed with your parents about growing up, and how do you see it now?

These questions reveal values, blind spots, and self-awareness. You’re not just listening for the surface-level answer — you’re watching what they emphasise and how reflective they are. For this reason, push candidates with repeated “Why?” until you reach their real motivations.

Another part of uncovering a person’s essence or characteristics is asking about their strengths and weaknesses. Instead of tired questions like “What are your weaknesses?”, you reframe:

- *100-Person Room*: What can you do better than anyone else in this room of 100?
- *Magic Wand*: If you could instantly gain an attribute you lack, what would it be?
- *Energy vs. Drain*: What kind of work gives you energy, and what drains you even when you succeed?
- *Teaching Moment*: If you had to teach a masterclass tomorrow, what would it be on?
- *Invisible Work*: What’s something you worked incredibly hard on that almost no one knows about?

Drill down on specifics, and look for honesty. Weaknesses paired with a clear growth mindset (systems, habits, learning efforts) are a strong signal.

## Learn Their Life Story

After the essence questions, shift to a chronological exploration of their life. This creates context and empirical evidence for the traits you just uncovered.

- **Childhood and adolescence**: Ask about parents, siblings, and formative years (ages 6–12, 12–18, and 18+). What peers did they have? Are they still in touch? Did they “upgrade” their circle over time?
- **Education**: What choices did they make, and why? How do they reflect on those decisions now?
- **Career chapters**: For each job, ask:
- - *Why did you take this role? Why did you leave?*
- *What was most important to you at the time?*
- *What impact did you have? If you hadn’t been there, what would have been different?*
- *Stack rank yourself against your peers — top 10%, 1%, or 0.1%? In what context?*
- *What would your boss and colleagues say about you?*
- *What was your typical day? When did you start, when did you finish?*

Patterns matter: do they consistently show initiative, work ethic, and resilience? Did they upgrade their peer group or plateau? Did they leave a clear mark wherever they went?

## Resume-First Interviews

Where DNA-first interviews are about *who someone is*, resume-first interviews are about *what someone has done*. They help you validate whether a candidate has already solved the kinds of problems you’re hiring them for — and whether they can do it again in your context.

Resume-first is especially critical for later hires (after the first ~10 employees) and for highly specialized or senior roles where prior success is the best predictor of future success.

### Walking Through Their Life Chronologically

A resume-first interview is essentially a guided tour of the candidate’s career. Instead of skimming their CV, you dig into each major chapter in detail:

- **Parents and Early Background**: Start briefly with family context and early influences. It gives colour to how they were shaped, their work ethic, and motivations.
- **Education**: What did they study, why did they choose it, and what did they get out of it? Were they top of their class, or did they focus elsewhere (projects, leadership, networking)?
- **Early Career Chapters**: Walk through their jobs chronologically, role by role. For each chapter, ask:
- - *Why did you make this move?* Push for real reasons (not “a good opportunity came up”).
- *What was most important to you at the time?* Was it money, learning, impact, freedom?
- *What was your biggest contribution?* What difference did you make that others couldn’t?
- *How did you compare to your peers?* Were you top of the class, average, struggling?
- *Who did you work with?* Note the names of managers, peers, and subordinates to turn into references later.

This chronological method prevents “storytelling bias,” where candidates polish their narrative in hindsight. Instead, you reconstruct their career as it unfolded, complete with trade-offs, wins, and setbacks.

### Drilling Into Outcomes and Impact

Resume-first hires are made for their ability to *repeat success*. That means you need to validate outcomes carefully:

- Ask about **specific results**: numbers hit, projects launched, growth achieved.
- Cross-check how much *their* contribution was versus the team or external factors. (“If you hadn’t been there, what would have been different?”)
- Look for **patterns of impact**: Were they consistently a top performer, or do they shine only under certain conditions?
- Probe into **failures and recoveries**: Resume-first hires should be able to talk clearly about where they fell short and what they learned.

For example, if you’re hiring a VP of Sales to grow revenue from $1M to $20M, you want to see evidence they’ve *done exactly that* before. Not adjacent success, not “part of a team that did it,” but a clear track record of driving the result themselves.

## Building Reference Leads Naturally

Every career chapter yields a reference. When a candidate mentions peers, bosses, or reports, write those names down. Later, those people become your best validation sources. A good resume-first interview might surface 15–30 reference names without ever asking, “Can you give me three references?”

## Distinguishing Between Surface and Depth

Not all resumes tell the truth. Candidates may embellish achievements or lean on brand names (e.g., ex-Google, ex-McKinsey) without having been outliers. Your job is to separate prestige from proof. That means:

- Pushing beyond titles into what they actually *did*.
- Asking how they ranked among their peers.
- Testing whether their skills translate to your context.

For example: being the #1 rep in a Fortune 500 company is different from being the first sales hire in a startup. Both are impressive — but they imply very different capabilities.
