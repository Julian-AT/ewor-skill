# Reference Calls

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/NHU5Ii3fKhw)

Interviews can tell you a lot, but reference calls often tell you more. They provide outside validation of what you’ve learned about a candidate, offering real-world examples of strengths, weaknesses, and impact. Done well, reference calls give you empirical evidence that interviews alone can’t provide.

## Finding the Right References

Start with the references candidates provide, but don’t stop there. Look at LinkedIn to identify former managers, peers, or founders they reported to. Reach out directly and ask for a short, five- to ten-minute call. Often, those initial conversations will point you to the best person to speak with. 

> **Note: **Aim for at least three reference calls. 

If the feedback is consistent, you’ll have a clear picture; if it differs widely, keep going until the pattern emerges.

## The Core Questions

Keep reference calls simple but sharp. These six questions uncover both context and depth:

1. **Context** – How did you meet and work together?
You want to know whether the reference worked with the candidate for years or only briefly. The depth of their relationship matters for how you interpret the rest of the feedback.
2. **One Word** – If you had to describe this person in a single word, what would it be?
Patterns here are powerful: when multiple references independently say “fast” or “reliable,” you can trust that trait is central to the candidate’s character.
3. **Impact** – What were their biggest accomplishments? What was their best moment? Their worst?
This validates whether the candidate truly drove outcomes, not just participated in them.
4. **Stack Ranking** – Compared to others in the same role, where do they stand?
Are they in the top 5%? The middle of the pack? Stack ranking cuts through vague praise and tells you how they compare against peers.
5. **Percentile Ranking** – How would you rate them on the dimensions that matter most: IQ, EQ, work ethic, integrity, grit, and motivation?
These ratings reveal patterns across references. Even if the numbers vary, consistent highs or lows will emerge.
6. **Worst Possible Statement** – Who could give me the harshest, most unfiltered perspective on this person? Can you connect me?
This ensures you hear from someone who might offer criticism — not just the references most likely to praise.

### Turning Answers Into Insight

The real value of reference calls lies in the patterns that emerge. If every reference independently ranks a candidate’s emotional intelligence in the top 1%, you can be confident it’s a defining strength. If someone claims to have been the top salesperson, but references place them consistently at #3 or lower, that’s a red flag.
