# A Note on Diversity

In the scramble of the early days, speed and alignment often come before variety. Homogeneous teams can move faster because they think alike — which helps when you’re still proving your model.

However, it's worth noting that as your company grows beyond ~25 employees, the priority shifts from speed to decision quality. That’s when diversity of mindset, background, gender, race, and culture becomes critical.

Why? Because when everyone thinks the same way, you risk blind spots. Imagine a team full of analytical minds — engineers, operators, even admins — who all love dissecting data. The company becomes great at analysing problems, but no one pushes bold ideas forward. By mixing analytical minds with creative risk-takers, you create tension that leads to better, more balanced decisions.

### What the Research Says

Research by Bruccoleri et al. (2018) illustrates the need to balance diversity within your company: teams that are too similar fall into groupthink,  while overly fragmented teams risk the “Abilene Paradox” — everyone thinking they’re aligned when they’re not. The sweet spot is a team diverse enough to bring multiple perspectives, but aligned enough to debate constructively.

### A Case Study from Gucci

![Source: CBS Sacramento](/api/media/file/190207091636-03-gucci-blackface-sweater-02072019-exlarge-169.jpg)

A lack of diversity doesn’t just slow innovation; it can also lead to costly mistakes. In early 2019, Gucci pulled a sweater from its collection after facing backlash from the Black community. The design, which evoked blackface imagery, had passed through multiple layers of approval without anyone flagging the issue.

Executives were left asking, “How did no one see this coming?”

The answer lay in team composition. Without diverse perspectives in the room, critical cultural blind spots went unnoticed. This incident illustrates that diversity isn’t only about fairness or representation — it’s about better decision-making. When teams reflect a variety of backgrounds and lived experiences, they catch issues others might miss and make stronger, more informed choices.
