# Job Specs

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/xBXIM-DSQhc)

When hiring, start by asking if you actually need a new person. Not every gap, whether it’s a task you can’t handle, work that slows the team down, or a missing capability, requires a formal job specification (job spec) to help you fill in what’s missing. 

Sometimes you already know someone in your network who can fill the need, or you meet a DNA hire — someone so aligned with your culture that a spec would only slow you down. However, if you can’t solve the bottleneck with a known person or a DNA hire, and you need to evaluate multiple candidates for a clearly defined role, then it’s time to create a job specification (job spec). 

## Building a Job Spec

**Must-haves**

A strong job spec should highlight only two or three non-negotiable qualities. Trying to capture a dozen skills makes the search scattered and leads to average hires. Instead, identify the two things that matter most to the success of the role and look for someone who is world-class in those areas.

*Example:* If you’re hiring a founding engineer, you might decide the two essentials are deep expertise in C and exceptional problem-solving ability. Rather than hiring a “well-rounded” developer, you want the very best person you can find in those dimensions.

**Culture**

Once you’ve identified the must-haves, you need to consider cultural fit. Culture is not what you aspire to — it’s a reflection of who you and your co-founder already are. If you value extreme work ethic, exceptional standards, and speed, then hiring someone who isn’t aligned with those traits will cause constant friction.

## A Closer Look

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/Lv11vZ26Jdw)

Once you’ve decided a hire is necessary and a job spec makes sense, the next step is to translate your needs into clear documents. At EWOR, we distinguish between two versions: the internal role brief and the external job posting. They serve different purposes but build on the same foundation.

### The Role Brief

The role brief is written for you and your team. It clarifies exactly why this role exists, what success looks like, and how the person will work with others.

A good role brief should cover, in addition to must-haves and cultural fit:

- **Contribution**: How this role directly advances company goals. For instance: *“This person is the operational engine of our content strategy, turning founder stories into consistent, high-quality assets that attract new applicants.”*
- **Key KPIs**: What metrics will this hire directly influence? For example, a Content and Comms Producer might be measured on volume of high-quality content, audience engagement, and growth in applications.
- **Collaboration**: Who does the hire interact with most often, both for input and output. This helps you involve the right people in the interview process.
- **Domains**: The core responsibilities and decision rights of your hire — e.g., story sourcing, narrative crafting, quality control, and pipeline management.
- **Accountability**: How do you keep this hire accountable? This can be ratings (e.g., 4.5/5 quality on output), delivery speed, or reliability of the pipeline.

Think of the role brief as your internal compass. It helps you design interview questions, build a case study, and hold the person accountable once they join.

### The External Job Posting

The job posting takes the essence of the role brief and adapts it for candidates. It must be concise, engaging, and written to sell your company as well as the role.

A strong posting typically includes:

1. **About Us**: A few exciting points that explain who you are and why someone should join. Remember: not everyone has heard of your startup.
2. **Role Description**: A short, engaging summary of what the person will own. For example: *“You will be the operational engine of our founder-story flywheel, shaping narratives and managing the production pipeline from idea to final publication...”*
3. **Responsibilities**: A clear, chronological list of what the person does day-to-day.
4. **Must-Haves**: The two or three non-negotiable qualities you’re screening for. In our example: exceptional writing and natural storytelling.
5. **Why Apply**: A short sales pitch about the opportunity.
6. **Call to Action**: How to apply, ideally with a concise closing line.

Early on, your postings should fit on a single page: three bullets on why join, one or two paragraphs on the role, the must-haves, and a call to action. As your brand grows, you can afford to expand, but concise and clear usually works best.
