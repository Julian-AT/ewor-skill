# Who to Hire

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/feOijRjo6mU)

When you start hiring, your biggest challenge isn’t just finding talent — it’s knowing which kind of talent your company truly needs at its current stage. The right hire at the wrong time can slow you down, while the right person at the right moment can multiply your progress.

This topic helps you understand two key dimensions of hiring: (1) the type of person you bring on, and (2) when to bring them on, depending on whether you’re before or after product–market fit (PMF).

## DNA-First vs Resume-First Hires

When building your team, it’s important to distinguish between two broad categories of hires: DNA-first and Resume-first. This is not just about how you evaluate candidates — it’s about aligning the type of person with the stage of your company.

### DNA-First

A DNA-first hire is someone you bring on for who they are, not for a narrow skill set. These people are generalists — adaptable, resourceful, and willing to take on whatever the company needs most at a given moment.

They might start by helping with admin, shift into product testing, later support sales, or even lead a new market. What matters most is their character, drive, and learning ability.

> **Note: **For your first 10 hires, DNA-first is usually the right approach. Early-stage startups need people who think like founders — individuals who can pivot with the company, thrive in ambiguity, and take ownership wherever they’re needed.

### Resume-First 

A Resume-first hire is someone you bring in for a specific expertise, proven by track record. For example, once you’ve reached €1M in sales and need to scale to €20M, you might hire someone who has already built and led sales teams at other startups.

Here, evaluation depends on evidence of past performance. You’re betting on repetition — that if they’ve done it before, they can do it again in your context.

> **Note: **Resume-first hires typically become important after** **product-market fit, when the company is scaling and specialised expertise makes the difference between incremental and exponential growth.

In the pre-PMF stage, your team should remain very lean. Sales must be founder-led until you’ve reached at least €300K — ideally €1M — in revenue. This keeps you close to customers and ensures you deeply understand the market.

At this stage, only two types of hires make sense:

- **Engineers**, who help you ship faster and improve the product.
- **Admins**, who free up your time so you can focus on sales, customers, and product.

Once you hit the post-PMF stage, everything shifts. During this time, you understand your model, your customer, and how to scale sales, and therefore, you can make targeted hires. Specialists who can build, sell, or market better than you become valuable — and with clear direction, these experts accelerate growth instead of slowing it down.

### The Key Archetypes

Beyond engineers and admins, some roles can become relevant depending on your progress and business model. Think of these as a toolbox of archetypes, not a checklist:

- **Founding engineer** — accelerates development and helps you ship faster.
- **Structured operator** — a versatile “doer” who brings order and discipline, taking on whatever is most urgent.
- **Silverback sales pro** — an experienced salesperson with a strong network; only useful after founders have proven sales themselves.
- **Hungry sales duo** — two competitive juniors who push each other to close deals; also, only after founder-led sales.
- **Marketing wizard** — valuable in B2C startups where virality and creative campaigns drive growth.
- **DeepTech engineer** — for highly technical startups where the founders lack a specific area of expertise.

### Who to Avoid

Not every role makes sense at the start. Some hires create cost and complexity without helping you move toward product-market fit.

- **Sales (Pre-PMF)**: Sales must be founder-led until product-market fit is achieved. Hiring salespeople too soon distances you from customers and slows down the critical learning process.
- **HR (Too Early in General)**: Whether pre- or just-post PMF, HR is premature. In the beginning, culture and people management should come directly from the founders.
- **CFOs and finance hires (Too Early in General)**: A CFO in a 3–10 person startup is a major red flag. Unless finance expertise is central to your product, founders must handle cash flow themselves in the early years.
