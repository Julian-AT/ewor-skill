# Interviews and Assessments

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/C5W4NSmO5Jg)

As your startup grows, a single interview isn’t enough to make reliable hiring decisions. You need multiple data points to evaluate candidates from different angles. At EWOR, we use a variety of interview and assessment types. You don’t need all of them for every hire, but drawing from this toolkit lets you move quickly while still making high-quality decisions.

## 12 Insightful Interview and Assessment Types

**1. Screening Call**
A short, 30-minute conversation led by the hiring manager or direct team lead. Its purpose is to confirm motivation, culture alignment, and spot obvious red flags early, so you don’t waste time on the wrong candidates.

**2. DNA Deep Dive**
Designed for DNA-first hires, this interview explores character and values rather than technical skills. It tests whether someone is adaptable, entrepreneurial, and aligned with the company’s way of working.

**3. Resume Deep Dive**
Best for resume-first hires, this interview goes line by line through a candidate’s past roles. The goal is to confirm whether they actually drove results — not just participated in success — and to judge if they can repeat it at your startup.

**4. Panel Interview**
A more intense setting where three to five interviewers meet the candidate together. By challenging answers and testing reasoning from multiple angles, panels are useful for highly complex roles like engineering or leadership.

**5. Case Study**
A structured exercise simulating the role. It might involve designing a sales proposal, planning a content funnel, or analysing competitors. Case studies reveal how a candidate approaches problems and structures their thinking.

**6. Work Sample Test**
Instead of a simulation, the candidate completes an actual task they would likely do in week one. This gives a direct sense of their quality, speed, and problem-solving ability in real-world conditions.

**7. Reference Calls**
Speaking with former colleagues or managers is one of the most reliable ways to validate interview impressions. References confirm strengths, weaknesses, and whether the candidate truly contributed as claimed.

**8. Technical Interview**
A focused deep dive into domain expertise — whether coding, biotech, marketing, or another specialty. These interviews test both technical knowledge and applied problem-solving ability.

**9. Pair Programming**
For engineering hires, this assessment involves coding together on a live problem. It reveals not only technical ability but also collaboration style, communication, and how they handle feedback in real time.

**10. Presentation Interview**
Used especially for leadership roles, this interview asks the candidate to present a solution, strategy, or case study. It shows their ability to structure ideas, communicate clearly, and persuade others.

**11. Culture Fit Interview**
A deeper dive into values and behaviours. Rather than generic “fit” questions, it tests how candidates make decisions, handle challenges, and align with the founder’s and team’s real operating style.

**12. Bar Raiser Interview**
Borrowed from Amazon, this interview is run by someone uninvolved in the role — often an advisor, investor, or senior team member. Their sole job is to decide whether the candidate raises the bar for the entire company.

## Structuring the Process

You don’t need all twelve methods for every hire. Instead, think of them as pieces you adapt to the role. The right structure depends on the type of role you are trying to fill. For example:

**DNA-first Hires**

DNA-first hires benefit from broad and character-focused rounds. Start with a screening call, then use a DNA deep dive to test adaptability, motivation, and values. A case study or work sample can reveal how they handle ambiguity. Finish with a culture fit interview to confirm alignment with the founders.

**Resume-first Hires**

Resume-first hires require a more skills-oriented process. Begin with a resume deep dive into past achievements, followed by a technical interview, work sample, or case study tied directly to the role. A panel interview can help stress-test their expertise. Wrap with a bar raiser to ensure they raise overall standards.

**Leadership Hires**

Leadership hires need the most multi-layered structure. After a screening call, include both a case study and a presentation interview, since leaders must communicate strategy as well as execute it. Pair this with panel discussions to test their ability to handle scrutiny and a culture fit interview to ensure they model company values.

**Technical Specialists**

Special technical hires call for deep technical evaluation. Start with a technical interview or knowledge-focused deep dive, then test practical skills with work samples, coding challenges, or pair programming. Supplement with a case study to check how they solve open-ended problems. Finally, include a culture fit to ensure strong collaboration with the existing team.
