# Filling and Managing the Funnel

[YouTube](https://youtu.be/EnJ7xx-OAY4)

You’re ready to hire—now you need people flowing into (and cleanly through) your process. This topic gives you practical ways to fill the funnel and a simple system to manage it end-to-end. Use the channels that fit your company and stage; you don’t need all of them to work.

## Sourcing Your Candidates

Filling your hiring funnel starts with sourcing — the deliberate effort to bring enough strong candidates into the process so you can be selective rather than settling. In practice, this means combining the reach of your own network with systems that keep fresh talent flowing in overtime.

**Tracking Your Network**

The first and most reliable place to look is always your own circle. Founders, co-founders, early employees, advisors, and investors already know exceptional people. The fastest way to start is to sit down with each of them and map out who they’d be excited to work with or who matches your must-haves. These connections are usually the foundation of your earliest hires because they move quickly, come with trust, and often already align with your values.

**Extend Through Amplifiers**

Once your direct network is tapped, the next step is to reach amplifiers: people who may not join themselves but have unusually wide reach. Community leaders, connectors, and other founders often love making introductions. If you can excite them about your company, they’ll share the opportunity in their WhatsApp groups, Slack channels, newsletters, or private communities — multiplying your reach far beyond what you could manage alone.

**Use Communities and Groups**

In parallel, you can distribute roles directly into communities you or your team belong to. This might be Slack groups, university alumni networks, or niche newsletters. Prioritise the groups you have direct access to, then extend into second-degree spaces through your employees and advisors. The key is to adapt the message for each group so it feels personal rather than spammy.

**Recruiting Partners**

For certain roles, especially senior or specialised ones, you may need outside help. Recruiting partners come in several forms: agencies that run full processes, one-person headhunters with strong networks, or startup communities that share opportunities for a small commission. They’re costly and best reserved for high-stakes hires, but when you need to reach people far outside your own network, they can be effective.

**Social and Direct Outreach**

Sometimes the best candidates come from putting yourself out there. A well-timed LinkedIn post about your mission or your team can go viral and bring in dozens of applications. For targeted needs, direct outreach also works: identify strong performers at adjacent companies — not direct competitors — and reach out personally. A thoughtful message paired with a clear pitch about your startup can be surprisingly persuasive.

**Scaling Channels**

As your company matures, broader channels like job platforms, structured referral programmes, or even university hiring start to play a bigger role. Platforms like Join can create sheer volume, though they usually produce lower quality. Referral schemes are more consistent — if you reward employees for bringing in stellar hires, you create a steady pipeline of talent. Internships, meanwhile, are a longer play: they may not add much short-term productivity, but they allow you to test people over months before making a full-time offer.

**Special Events**

Finally, certain events can be goldmines for technical talent. Hackathons in particular showcase how engineers think and collaborate under pressure. Rather than competing yourself, it’s often more effective to build relationships with organisers, sponsor challenges, or simply meet the top participants and bring them into your funnel.

**The Balance**

There isn’t a single template for sourcing — it depends on your stage, the role you’re hiring for, and the kind of company you’re building. In the earliest days, your network and amplifiers will do most of the heavy lifting. Later on, referrals, direct outreach, and targeted programmes add depth. For highly technical or senior roles, external specialists and niche events can be the deciding factor. The key is to choose a few channels that suit your needs and manage them systematically so that your funnel never runs dry.

**Direct Sourcing**

Sometimes the best way to find candidates is to go straight to the source. Look at adjacent companies selling into similar markets or solving related problems. Identify their top performers on LinkedIn, reach out personally, and make a clear pitch about your startup. This works especially well for commercial roles like sales, where past success is easier to spot and reference.

**Viral and Social Outreach**

Founders themselves can also generate inbound momentum. A well-crafted LinkedIn post or tweet about your mission, culture, or recent milestone can draw attention and spark applications. The key is to share something that feels authentic and differentiates your startup — people are drawn to missions that matter and teams that feel exceptional.

**Referral Systems**

Employee referrals are one of the most powerful levers you can set up early. High performers tend to know other high performers. By creating incentives — whether cash bonuses, salary increases, or even additional equity — you encourage your team to bring in people they truly vouch for. Over time, this creates a virtuous cycle where strong hires beget more strong hires.

**Internships as Long-Term Funnel**

Internships can be an underrated sourcing tool. Even if they aren’t immediately net-positive for output, they give you months to evaluate whether someone has the mindset, skill, and cultural alignment to succeed full-time. Treat internships less as a way to save costs and more as a way to “audition” future teammates.

**Events and Hackathons**

For technical roles, hackathons can be goldmines. They showcase how engineers think, solve problems, and collaborate under pressure. Instead of competing yourself, it’s often smarter to build relationships with organisers, sponsor a challenge, or simply meet the standout participants. A few hours spent here can surface candidates you’d struggle to find elsewhere.
