Many studies point out that each makes hundreds, if not thousands, of decisions a day. However, research by Sahakian & Labuzetta (2013), indicates that we make **35,000 decisions a day**! 
As an entrepreneur, it’s up to you to make frequent and quick, yet also high-quality decisions. Though, we see as much variety in decision-making types as we do in personality types. This means that there is ‘no one size fits all’ approach to making better decisions.

That’s why we developed the EWOR Decision Type Assessment tool. This tool aims to help you understand your unique decision-making style, and as a result, gain valuable self-awareness about your natural tendencies, preferences, and biases. By understanding your decision-making style, you will learn to recognise your strengths and how to leverage them, as well as identify areas where you may need to exercise caution or seek additional input.

As every decision comes with risks and potential biases, understanding how you naturally approach decisions allows you to recognise any inherent biases or blind spots you may have. Armed with this knowledge, you can consciously compensate for these tendencies, seek additional input, or apply decision-making frameworks to mitigate potential errors.



### How Does this Assessment Work?

With the questions of this assessment, three core categories related to decision-making are assessed. The ratio of which, as determined by your answers, will lead to your Decision-Making Type. The three core categories are characterised below:

**Category A **represents a tendency to take action, make quick decisions, and be decisive. Such tendencies are often associated with the Autocratic and Risk Taker decision-making types.

**Category B** represents a tendency to analyse data and information, weigh the pros and cons, and minimize risks. This behaviour tends to dominate decision-making types such as Analytical and Risk Taker.

**Category C **represents a tendency to involve others in the decision-making process, consider the impact on relationships, and seek consensus. This characterization is most often viewed with the Collaborative and Intuitive decision-making types.

While these variables may not encompass all aspects of decision-making, they provide a way to measure and categorise decision-making styles based on these core tendencies.

### Visualising Your Score

If you have your ratio, you can simply click next on the assessment to get your result. If you are curious to see where exactly in the dimensions of decision-types yours exists, you can use the Decision-Making Type Diagram to visualise this through the following:

1. Look for the three axes: There will be three lines or axes labelled with Category A, B, and C, respectively.
2. Find the corners: Locate the lower left corner for Category A, the point of the triangle for Category B, and the right point for Category C.
3. Understand the lines: The lines connecting the corners show different proportions of the core categories.
4. Locate your point: Find the point within the triangle that represents the composition you want to analyse. For example, if you are interested in the proportion of Category B, locate the point on the line that connects Category A and B. For instance, if the point is 20% Category A and 50% Category B, the distance from the line connecting Category A and B will be 50% of the way from Category A to Category B on the left axis.

### Why am I Scoring a Combination of Decision Types?

Within the decision-making type chart, you may find that some areas overlap, as with the red score below, or seemingly blend with one another, as with the yellow score below:



![Some decision-making types are a combination of multiple types.]()

If your score falls within such an area, you might fall into a mixture of decision types. This means you have the potential to exhibit characteristics of both styles, depending on the situation. For example, someone who is an autocratic-collaborative decision-maker may be confident in their abilities and able to make quick decisions on their own (autocratic), but also value input and feedback from others and consider the impact of their decisions on relationships (collaborative).

Such a mixture of types can happen if someone has a personality or experiences that make them naturally inclined towards multiple decision styles, or if they have learned to adapt their decision-making approach based on the situation they are facing. It’s also possible that you are still figuring out your preferred decision-making style and experimenting with different approaches.

It’s important to recognise that there are strengths and weaknesses associated with every decision-making style. By understanding the traits and recommendations associated with your type, you can work towards optimising your decision-making process and, thus, make more effective decisions.
